# On the Quality of Distillate and Distillation Practice

The quality of distillate depends, within certain limits, on the type of still employed and even more on how the distillation is conducted. If the wine being distilled is defective, it is necessary to eliminate the first products of distillation—that is, the most volatile, rich in aldehydes and ethyl acetate, which alter the fineness of the spirit of wine when in excess. It is also necessary to eliminate the final products, as they are rich in amyl alcohol, butyl alcohol, etc., and acetic acid—in short, the "heads" and "tails" as they are commonly called—collecting only the middle or "heart" product. In such cases, intermittent-operation apparatus is employed, whether equipped with dephlegmators that allow a distillate of 55° to 60° to be obtained in a single pass, or without concentration apparatus and with imperfect dephlegmators, in which case two operations must be performed:

1. Distillation of the wine, collecting the low-strength heads from 20° to 25°;
2. Rectification of the heads, from which the foreshots and tails are then separated, to collect only the heart distillate at a strength of 60, 65, or 70°.

When dealing with sound wines, it is possible to collect the first products or heart products in a single batch and separate only the tails. This separation is accomplished automatically with continuous-action apparatus. The manner of regulating the heat also affects the quantity of product; it must be maintained constant and fed with dry wood.

The spirit of wines that have not fermented on the pomace are the finest and most pleasant: to this practice must be attributed the superiority of true cognac, which is produced from a white wine not fermented on the pomace.

By operating with care, one obtains a good distillate from wine that, besides alcohol, contains the aromatic substances of the raw material and with the almost complete exclusion of off-flavors.

Overall, we have initially a product colorless as water, with the hot and burning taste of alcohol, lacking smoothness and the particular character of spirit of wine. To transform itself into cognac, the distillate must be aged.
