# Liqueurs Made by Mixing Essential Oils or Essences

**Liqueurs made by mixing essential oils or essences (1).** — The preparation of these is nothing more than a simplification of the distillation method. However, while this procedure allows liqueurs to be made simply and quickly, the products obtained are of mediocre quality, since the mixing of essential oils or the dilution of essences never achieves the intimacy and completeness that results from distillation. Nevertheless, this approach saves time, materials, and fuel; indeed, the so-called cold preparation of liqueurs consists precisely of this procedure, which involves dissolving carefully weighed or measured essences—or tinctures and extracts previously obtained by maceration—directly in strong alcohol, and then mixing the solution with syrup, water, coloring substances, etc.

In certain cases it may be advisable, in order to avoid the drawback mentioned above, to distill or cohobate the mixture of essences in the alcohol, so as to obtain a homogeneous and intimate aroma. Clearly, however, this requires the use of a still, and so one returns to the distillation method.

## Aromatized Wines

This class includes all those beverages that result from the addition of aromatics to fermented liquids, and most commonly to wine: examples include vermouth and various cordials, some of which—such as mead (Idromele) and Hippocras (Ippocrasso)—were highly prized among the ancients.

Indeed, it is worth remembering that until the Middle Ages, liqueurs as such were unknown; their production began only after the discovery—or rather, the popularization—of the processes for producing alcohol, which occurred toward the end of the 13th century through the work of Arnaldo da Villanova.

---

*(1) We think it necessary to point out from the outset the distinction that differentiates these substances, whose terminology is generally used in a way that causes confusion. Essential or ethereal oils are the products extracted directly from plants, in which they naturally pre-exist; essences or extracts, on the other hand, are the dissolutions of simple essential oils, or of mixtures thereof, in concentrated alcohol.*
