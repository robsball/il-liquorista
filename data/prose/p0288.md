At this point, if the matter is consistent, it is divided and placed to drain on cloth in a cool location to prevent fermentation. The following are subjected to maceration: currants, raspberries, blackberries, cherries, quince pulp, apple pulp, pear pulp, etc.; placing the latter out of contact with air. Gelatinous and mucilaginous juices lose their density through maceration, such as those from strawberries, raspberries, currants, blackberries, etc. This operation requires the addition of a small quantity of water whenever the plants are not sufficiently succulent by themselves, naturally or incidentally. This addition of water must be carried out at the beginning of the crushing of the plants, so that it mixes intimately with the juice; without this precaution the product keeps for a shorter time.

**Cooking.** — Cooking should only be used for odorless fruits and those unalterable by heat, when one wishes to convert their juice into extract or syrup; but it is detrimental if employed to extract juice from those fruits that possess a delicate color and fleeting aroma.

**Fermentation.** — We mention this operation only because it was regarded as most convenient for obtaining fruit juice; but we repeat that it produces the decomposition of sugar, mucilage, and gelatin; and instead of obtaining true juices, one obtains only wine, cider, poor vinegar, etc.; because the sugar, mucilage, and gelatin are transformed into alcohol and acetic acid: for this reason fermentation is avoided in juice extraction, as it alters their chemical properties.

**Pressing.** — Substances treated each according to their own nature and the various uses for which they are intended, with the different preparations indicated, need to be pressed. However, this is not necessary for those that were subjected to the operations described above. Certain fruits, such as lemons, citrons, oranges, etc., need only be pressed, without other operations.
