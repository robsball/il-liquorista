# Aromatic Alcoholized Spirits

## Distinguishing Distilled from Artificial Aromatic Waters

One can distinguish distilled aromatic waters from those obtained by essential oils as follows: shake 10 cc of the water under examination in a test tube with 3 cc of almond oil; allow the mixture to settle, and thus the two layers will separate. The aqueous layer will retain the characteristic aroma if the water had been prepared by distillation; if instead it was artificial, made by means of ethereal oils, then the aqueous layer will have completely lost all aroma.

**Note** — The liquorist, should he not find it convenient or lack sufficiently large apparatus for the preparation of aromatic waters, would do well to obtain these preparations from specialist producers of first-rate quality.

## Aromatic Alcoholized Spirits

Aromatic spirits, which correspond to the alcoholates used in pharmacy, consist of alcohol which has acquired, by means of distillation, the essential or aromatic principles of one or several plant substances.

Alcoholates are obtained by distilling alcohol with aromatic substances, and are simple or compound according to whether they were prepared with a single substance or with several substances combined.

Distillation can be carried out in alembics heated by bain-marie or in vapor alembics. Figure 8 represents a bain-marie alembic; naturally, the vapor alembic is used only in large factories where a steam generator is available (see fig. 9).

One may also use a direct-fire alembic (see figures 2, 3), but this requires considerable skill in conducting the operation, continuous supervision, and even so generally does not yield the good results that, with less effort, can be achieved with the bain-marie-regulated alembic.

The aromatic substances are introduced into the cucurbit of the alembic after being minced, pounded, chopped, or powdered as appropriate. They are left to macerate in the alcohol for 24 hours or for several days according to their nature; then one dilutes with a quantity of water that is approximately half that of the alcohol employed, and proceeds to [distillation].
