# Ingredients (continued): Wormwood, Balsam of Peru, Balsam of Tolu, Bananas

**Wormwood.** — Two varieties of wormwood are used in the preparation of liqueurs. One is Great Wormwood (*Artemisia absinthium*), an herbaceous plant with small, yellowish flowers arranged in clusters. The whole plant gives off a strong, pleasant odor. It contains a bitter principle, absinthin, and a volatile oil.

The whole plant is used, but especially the flowering tops. The other variety is Pontic or Roman wormwood (*Artemisia pontica*), which is smaller than the previous one. It grows especially in Italy and Greece. It has a less pronounced flavor than Great Wormwood.

This plant is harvested twice a year; the first harvest is to be preferred.

**Balsam of Peru.** — This balsam is an exudate of *Myroxylon peruiferum*. Several types are found on the market: the white type, of pleasant odor with a mild flavor, has the consistency of turpentine; the red or dry type is almost solid. These qualities are rare. The most common is the so-called black type — brown, reddish, syrupy, with a strong pleasant odor and a sharp taste. It contains cinnamic acid. It is partly soluble in diluted alcohol. It is often adulterated with colophony (rosin), benzoin, storax, copaiba, turpentine, and alcohol.

**Balsam of Tolu.** — This balsam is obtained from *Myroxylon toluiferum*, a tree native to Colombia or Venezuela.

Two types are found on the market: one is soft, white, with the consistency of turpentine, more or less transparent, of pleasant odor; the other, called black, is brownish-red, brittle, less fragrant than the first, and over time develops a crystalline structure.

Fresh Balsam of Tolu is liquid and viscous, but on exposure to air it solidifies completely, taking on a more or less intense brown color. It contains cinnamic acid, benzoic acid, toluene, etc.

**Bananas.** — The fruit of *Musa paradisiaca* (native to southern Asia, Africa, and Brazil). This plant is now cultivated in all tropical regions. Bananas are an excellent food: they are dried and made into a kind of flour, or else sold commercially like dried figs. They have a most pleasant aroma, which makes them very suitable for making sweets and liqueurs.
