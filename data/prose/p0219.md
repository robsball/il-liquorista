# Aromatic Waters via Essential Oils

## Clarity of Aromatic Waters

Excessive cold causes cloudiness that persists for several days; waters thus affected eventually clear, but form a deposit containing a substantial portion of the aroma.

Cloudy aromatic waters can be made limpid by adding 2 grams of borax and the same amount of rock alum per liter of spoiled water. A flocculent precipitate forms. This procedure, however, sometimes leaves an unpleasant taste in the water, making repeated careful filtrations preferable.

## By Essential Oils

Just as certain liqueurs are prepared by dissolving the essential oils contained in aromatic plants rather than by direct distillation of these, so too the preparation of some aromatic waters by addition and agitation of a few drops of essential oil in water—rather than by distillation of this in contact with the plant—is practiced and accepted. We may add that some of the most recent foreign pharmacopeias have sanctioned this practice by registering the relative procedures as official: we shall mention among aromatic waters obtained through dilution of their respective essential oils those of rose, melissa, fennel, mint, etc. We observe, however, that generally, since an inert substance is required to diffuse the essence better in water—such as magnesia, calcium phosphate, kaolin, etc.—filtration of the product obtained becomes unavoidable; which, though it does not present that delicacy and refinement of aroma characteristic of waters prepared by distillation, offers the advantage of greater durability of preservation.

A very simple method for preparing it using essential oils consists in impregnating strips of soft white filter paper, or a wisp of hydrophilic cotton, with the prescribed quantity of essential oil and shaking these in the volume of water to be aromatized, after which one filters. For anise and cinnamon waters, 12 drops are employed per liter of water; for those of fennel, melissa, mint, 20 drops; for those of neroli and roses, 5 drops.
