In the everyday practice of the liquor maker, the other tables given on pages 51 and 52 may also prove very useful; in these, the dilution of alcohol is considered within the range of strengths generally adopted for preparing liqueurs.

A very simple method, though of only relative accuracy, for calculating the quantity of water that must be added to an alcohol of known concentration in order to dilute it to a desired strength, consists in applying the formula:

**x = (A · G) / g**

where *x* expresses the quantity of water to be used, *A* the quantity of alcohol to be diluted, *G* its strength (degree), and *g* the strength to be obtained. In other words, one multiplies the number of liters (or other unit of volume) of the alcohol to be diluted by its alcoholic strength, and divides the product by the figure representing the desired dilution strength. The quotient will indicate the number of liters (or other unit of volume) that must be added.

Example: to dilute 15 liters of 95° alcohol down to 80° alcohol, one must add:

15 × 95 / 80 = 17.81 liters of water.
