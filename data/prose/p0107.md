*Medicinal properties*: Tonic and antiscorbutic.

**Sandalwood (Wood of).** — Two main kinds of sandalwood are found in commerce, well distinguished from each other: the yellowish-white, citrine kind, and the red kind. The latter is used mainly as a dye-wood, while from the former a prized essential oil is obtained.

White sandalwood comes from *Santalum album* (Santalaceae), native to the East Indies. Its color ranges from light yellow to tawny yellow; it is heavy, hard, with a compact grain, and difficult to split.

Its odor is very pleasant, similar to that of rose and musk; its taste is bitter and aromatic. In commerce it is mostly found in small pieces about 10 cm long and of similar thickness.

**Santolina (Lavender Cotton).** — This is a perennial small plant, *Santolina chamaecyparissus*, belonging to the Compositae; it is common in the mountains of the southern regions of Europe. The whole plant gives off a distinctive, penetrating aromatic odor. It is stimulant and anthelmintic.

**Sassafras (Wood of).** — From *Laurus sassafras* (Laurineae), a tree native to North America, the wood of the roots and of the lower part of the trunk is used, as well as the bark. It is found in commerce in various sizes; the bark is also found separately, and it is richer in essential oil than the wood. The wood is light, porous, and reddish; the bark is thick, corky, grayish-white in color; it gives off a strong, pleasant odor, similar to that of anise. The taste is at first sweetish and aromatic, later becoming astringent; the bark is richer in essential oil than the wood. It is found in commerce in irregular pieces, gray on the outside and rust-colored within.

**Serpentaria.** — This is the root of *Aristolochia serpentaria* (Aristolochiaceae), a plant native to North America, which comes to commerce in thin, interwoven pieces adhering to fragments of the rhizome, knotty, and yellow internally. It gives off an aromatic odor reminiscent of Valerian but less disagreeable; its taste is aromatic and camphor-like.
