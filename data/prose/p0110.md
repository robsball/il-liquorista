**Saffron.** — The stigmas of *Crocus sativus* (Iridaceae), an herbaceous plant native to the Orient but cultivated in Europe, are sold commercially together with the upper end of the style, as a mass of flexible, elastic filaments of an orange-red color. Saffron gives off a sharp, distinctive odor and has a bitter, aromatic taste; it also contains an intensely yellow coloring substance, crocin (or polychroite), soluble in alcohol, in oils, and in water. Commercial varieties of saffron are distinguished according to their origin. However, because of its high price, saffron is frequently adulterated or replaced with substitutes on the market, especially the powdered form. It is used as a coloring substance, as a condiment, and also as a medicine.

**Zedoary.** — This is a drug made from the rhizomes of *Curcuma aromatica* and *Curcuma zedoaria*. Two commercial varieties exist, the round and the long. Both have a camphor-like aromatic odor and a somewhat bitter, acrid taste. They contain an essential oil.

**Ginger.** — This drug consists of the rhizomes of *Zingiber officinale*, an herbaceous plant native to Asia, cultivated in China, the Antilles, and Brazil. It is found on the market in two forms:

Gray or black ginger, formed from black, peeled rhizomes with a pale brown, grayish bark, yellowish on the inside. It has a strong aromatic odor and a burning taste.

White (bleached) ginger, formed from peeled rhizomes having the same characteristics as the previous type, but of a lighter color. This quality is often whitened by chemical means (chlorine or sulfurous acid); it is difficult to substitute with other rhizomes.
