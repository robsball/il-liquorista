# Materials (continued): Lovage, Lemons, Licorice, Hops, Mace

**Lovage.** — *Ligusticum levisticum*, also known as mountain celery, is a hardy herbaceous perennial (umbellifer) that grows throughout central Europe and can reach up to 2 m in height. Its leaves have a strong aromatic odor and a pungent taste. The fruits (commonly called seeds) have a turpentine-like odor and a bitter taste, and are about 5 mm long by 3 mm wide. The root, 2 to 3 cm thick, is black on the outside and whitish inside. It has an aromatic odor and an acrid taste, and contains, like the stem, a large amount of yellow, gummy sap. The roots and fruits are often sold in place of those of Angelica, and are used for the same purposes.

**Lemons.** — There is no need to describe the very common fruits of *Citrus limonum*. We will only note that the lemons richest in juice are not always the most suitable for distillation: those most convenient for the liquor-maker are the ones with a thick, tender peel, since they contain a greater quantity of essential oil than those with smooth skin.

**Licorice.** — From *Glycyrrhiza glabra* (a legume), a plant that grows wild but is not very common in southern Europe and Asia Minor, a juice is extracted which is condensed and shaped into black sticks resembling pitch, with a conchoidal fracture and a characteristic sweet taste. It is extracted especially from the roots and stolons (creeping runners). The best comes from Calabria.

**Hops.** — *Humulus lupulus* (Urticaceae) is a climbing, twining plant cultivated in northern Europe. During ripening the fruits form a cone-shaped cluster (hop cones), yellowish in color, pleasant in odor, and bitter in taste; they are dried and preserved, and are found in commerce compressed in zinc-lined containers. They are used especially in beer-making. Their active principle is lupulin. Lupulin flour, or hop powder, is the yellowish-red, bitter powder obtained by rubbing the inflorescences of the recently dried female hop plant; it consists of the separated glands.
