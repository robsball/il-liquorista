# Improvement and Aging of Spirits; Cognac Production

In general, spirits are improved and made sweeter and more pleasant by adding 15 grams of candied sugar per liter, or 3 centiliters of grape syrup, which also serve to color the liquor.

As for aroma and flavor, these can be improved by the addition of 2 liters of good aged rum for every hectoliter of spirit with added sugar.

Prof. Martinotti of the Royal Enological Station of Asti recommends this practical method:

When wishing to age wine spirit without resorting to the long aging that occurs in oak barrels, one places in maceration in the spirit 1–1.5% of fine white oak wood shavings, leaving them for one month and agitating frequently to change the liquid layer and thus aid the dissolution of the ethereal principles contained in the oak wood. Having done this, filter the liquid by passing it into white glass bottles filled to ⅔ capacity, which are then lightly stoppered and arranged in a space exposed to all variations of light and temperature, tilting them so that the liquid does not touch the stopper. In this way one has the maximum surface of liquid exposed to air, which, as is known, is one of the coefficients of aging, this depending in large part on special oxidations and combinations favored by oxygen from the air.

One need only take care to avoid direct sunlight striking the bottles, because then, since the stopper must be kept very loose to allow a certain freedom of air entry, there would be excessive evaporation of alcohol and the benefit would not compensate for the loss of alcohol.

With well-aged spirits, cognac is prepared. Generally, products of various ages and fineness are mixed together in different proportions to obtain cognac of a given type. Often the mixture is diluted to lower its alcohol content to 45° and 50°, if intended for cold regions, and it is clarified with gelatin; if it is somewhat rough, a little syrup and fine sugar, well aged from 1 to 1.5%, is added to give the liquid a softer flavor: it is filtered and the filtrate is stored again in barrels, which are left to rest for several more months, to allow time for the components to blend and become homogeneous.

One thus obtains that liquid of amber yellow color, with a sweet odor
