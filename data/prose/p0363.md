# Introduction to Liqueur Recipes and Classification

We have already discussed repeatedly in Part I the criteria and standards to follow for proper judgment on this matter.

We shall now present a varied collection of recipes for the preparation of artificial liqueurs, grouped into three distinct general categories in relation to the classification we have adopted, namely:

a) by distillation;
b) by maceration and digestion;
c) by mixture of essential oils or essences.

Following these, we shall provide, as a corollary, the preparation of extracts for liqueurs or the instructions for their use.

Within these general categories, we have not deemed it necessary to further subdivide the various liqueurs according to the types to which they might be classified based on their composition, or more precisely, the percentage of their respective components (Rosolios, Creams, Elixirs, etc.), in order to avoid an overly complicated arrangement to the detriment of the manual's practicality. Moreover, the reader will certainly have learned from what we have so far expounded the necessary standards that will guide him to judge straightaway from the proportions of the components the type of liqueur described; and moreover—we repeat—the recipes indicated have no other purpose than to indicate the characteristic composition: it falls to the manufacturer to modify them according to tastes, requirements, and the means at his disposal. Furthermore, from the indication of the percentage limits for each type of liqueur, it will not be difficult for the manufacturer himself to vary the fundamental ingredients appropriately (alcohol, water, and sugar) to reduce, for example, an ordinary liqueur to a fine one, or vice versa; or an elixir to a rosoli, etc.
