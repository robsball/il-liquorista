The yeast taken from the grain mash is not exhausted after a single fermentation; like that from the potato mash (due to a lack of proteinaceous substances), it remains still capable of fermenting.

Grain-alcohol factories are therefore generally also yeast factories, producing it in quantities greater than their own needs.

The fusel oil from grain is not entirely separated from the spirit by rectification.

Its odor and flavor account for part of the special properties of grain brandy, for which reason it must not be missing.

As the spirit ages, it loses its penetrating odor, and it then becomes a true aroma capable of satisfying a refined palate. Aging therefore determines the value of this beverage. By adding various aromatic substances, several varieties of brandy are obtained, which we shall describe further on.

## Characteristics and Properties of Alcohol

Some remarks are now indispensable on the characteristics, properties, and commercial value of ethyl alcohol, considered as the raw material for the manufacture of liqueurs; on the criteria and practical tests for recognizing and assessing the quality, value, and strength of the commercial product; and on the rules for reducing the strength of concentrated alcohol by dilution with water.

Ethyl alcohol is composed of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, and, chemically speaking, is an ethyl hydrate. It is a clear, mobile liquid, strongly refractive, with a pungent taste and a not very pronounced, aromatic odor. It has a specific gravity of 0.794 when perfectly anhydrous; however, its density increases according to the proportion of water contained in the liquid, and it is on this basis that the criterion for the commercial value of the product is founded, as we shall see.
