# Vegetable Juices

## Vegetable Juices

Although fruit juices find limited application in liqueur manufacture—their use being restricted to certain types of liqueurs, particularly Ratafias and Syrups—we do not think it appropriate to omit some information concerning these preparations, especially since it is generally quite difficult to procure them from industry, whereas the liqueur producer will always find it advantageous to prepare them himself. This is all the more so because these are fairly simple and practical procedures that require only particular care and attention in the purification process, which especially ensures their preservation.

The term "vegetable juices" designates the liquid portion or proper juice of plants or other parts of vegetables, obtained by pressing (squeezing), followed by other operations, with the aim of rendering this liquid suitable for the uses to which it is destined.

Vegetable juices are distinguished into 4 types: aqueous juices, oily juices or sweet oils, essential or volatile oils, and resinous juices. We shall concern ourselves only with the first type.

Aqueous juices, as the name indicates, have water as their vehicle; their composition is highly varied: they can contain various acids, sugar, gums, mucilages, coloring matter, salts, and sometimes a kind of suspension of gum-resinous substances that give them a milky appearance. These juices are designated as milky, acidic, mucilaginous, etc. They are derived from roots, leaves, berries, fruits, barks, herbs, and flowers, and so forth.

We shall limit ourselves to describing some of those from fruits alone.
