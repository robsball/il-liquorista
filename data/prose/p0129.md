**Marjoram.** — When obtained from the fresh plant it is greenish-yellow; from the dried plant it is pale yellow. With time it becomes dense. It has the characteristic penetrating odor of marjoram, a pungent, slightly bitter taste. It is often adulterated, but in turn it is used to adulterate other oils, such as that of mint, etc.

**Bitter Almonds.** — The essential oil is obtained from bitter almonds and from peach kernels. The essence is not preformed in the almonds, but rather forms in them after the fixed oil has been extracted by pressing and they are placed in contact with water.

By distilling the residues from the manufacture of this oil (press cakes) about 0.85% of essential oil of bitter almonds is obtained. The oil thus obtained still contains large amounts of prussic acid (hydrocyanic acid), which is removed by treating the oil with ferrous chloride and lime, and then redistilling. The rectified essential oil is colorless, dense, highly refractive, and turns red in light. It has a well-known sharp, characteristic odor, an aromatic, burning taste; it is poisonous and must therefore be used in small doses and with great caution.

The essence of bitter almonds contains prussic acid, which however can easily be separated. Only essence free of prussic acid should be used.

It must be kept in well-sealed bottles and away from light.

The tests for identifying this essential oil are as follows:

1) To detect the presence of prussic acid, 10 drops of the essential oil under examination are treated with 2 c.c. of a 1% solution of caustic soda, with the addition of a small crystal of ferrous sulfate and 2 drops of a solution of ferric perchloride: the mixture is shaken vigorously and then acidified with a few drops of dilute hydrochloric acid. If the essential oil is fresh, an intense blue coloration will appear if it contains prussic acid.
