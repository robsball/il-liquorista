**Arnica.** — The essential oil extracted from the flowers is blue-green or yellowish; with age it turns brown, and it is poorly soluble in alcohol. The oil from the roots is the more common one; it is yellow and darkens with time.

It has a strongly aromatic and characteristic odor and taste.

**Wormwood (Assenzio).** — The essential oil of wormwood is obtained by distillation; that extracted from the fresh herb is dark green, while that extracted from the dried herb is yellow-green or reddish. These oils deteriorate over time, becoming dark brown and somewhat viscous. They have a strong, somewhat unpleasant odor, and a bitter, harsh, burning taste; they are composed of absinthol and a blue oil.

Wormwood oil is often adulterated with alcohol, oil of turpentine, or copaiba. It is poisonous, though in small doses it is a powerful tonic. Its use in the preparation of liqueurs is forbidden in France.

**Bergamot.** — The essential oil is extracted by pressing or squeezing the peel of the Citrus Bergamia.

In commerce, a distinction is made between the oil obtained by pressing the peel over a sponge (the Sicilian method), called Bergamotte à la main, which is the best; the so-called Bergamotte dure or matura (of second quality); and Essence of Bergamot, obtained by pressing the peel or the whole fruit. Distillation yields an inferior essence.

Freshly prepared bergamot oil is yellowish; with time it becomes more deeply colored, and in copper vessels it turns green. Its odor recalls that of orange and lemon. The taste is aromatic and somewhat bitter. On contact with air it becomes cloudy, turns brown, and takes on a turpentine-like odor.

It is adulterated with alcohol, with almond oil, or with orange-peel oil, and especially with lemon essence.
