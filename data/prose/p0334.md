# Rum: Genuine Rum (Rum, Tafia)

## Rum: Genuine Rum (Rum, Tafia)

Rum belongs to the category of spirits obtained not from wine, but directly from sugar. The fermentable liquids are supplied by the waste from sugar cane processing, as well as by the byproducts of sugar cane cooking (syrups and molasses).

True rum is obtained by distilling the residues from sugar cane manufacturing—or molasses—after fermentation. Often, aromatic substances are added to these residues during distillation to enhance or vary the aroma of the product.

Rum freshly distilled is colorless, but over time it takes on color from the wood of the casks in which it is stored, as happens with cognac. Often, however, it is artificially colored.

As for alcohol content, this varies considerably depending on origin. Rum from the French colonies has, on average, only 54% alcohol, while that of Demerara contains up to 81%. For American rum on average one may estimate 74%.

Its elevated alcohol percentage is in no way a criterion of quality; rather, it may be assumed that rums with very high alcohol content have received an addition of alcohol before or after distillation.
