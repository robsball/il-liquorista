# Extraction of Vegetable Substances

Extraction is the separation of the soluble principles of plants by means of a liquid solvent that dissolves these principles.

For the manufacture of liqueurs, the solvent can only be alcoholic. The extraction is called maceration if it takes place at ordinary temperature, or digestion if heat is used with the aim of rapidly exhausting the drug (plant material).

In discussing essential oils we became acquainted with those plant constituents which, being volatile, can be separated and obtained in pure form from the drug.

But plants also possess other soluble principles that are not volatile, and which can therefore only be extracted by maceration or digestion in a liquid solvent. These non-volatile soluble substances are called extractive substances or principles. If the solvent liquid is alcoholic, and is therefore also capable of dissolving essential oils, these naturally pass into solution as well. The solution separated from the exhausted drug (the residue) is called a tincture, or an extract if concentrated.

The extractive substances found in plants are of many different kinds. In some plants they form in large amounts, in others in very small quantities. Each plant possesses particular extractive principles that are rarely found in other plants, even those belonging to the same species.

Among these extractive substances, the most interesting for liqueur manufacture is the bitter substance (vegetable bitter). The manufacturer must know these with certainty as regards their particular flavor, their properties, and their dietetic action, if he wishes to obtain marketable products from them. The best way to acquire this knowledge is to prepare a tincture from each drug. These tinctures must be obtained in such a way as to contain equal proportional quantities, by weight of drug to volume of alcohol at 45–60%. A practical proportion is 100 grams of drug to 1 liter of alcohol, that is, 10%.
