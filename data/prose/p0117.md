Some oils, over time and with cold, deposit a solid, resinous portion at the bottom of the container, as is observed with bergamot, citron, etc., oils, especially if the container is opened frequently. Others modify or lose their aroma and become, over time, dense and thus unusable; still others, without undergoing alteration, gradually take on a darker color, as observed with clove, calamus, and cinnamon oils, which become red and brown over time.

### Rules for Preservation

Essential oils are easily altered by light; colorless ones turn yellow and then brown: chamomile oil, which is blue, turns yellow. On contact with air they undergo resinification, that is, they oxidize and eventually solidify, losing their characteristic odor.

The tendency to resinify and alter is especially manifest in those essential oils rich in terpenes. Resinification is a product of oxidation and is caused by contact with air; light further accelerates it. Essential oils must therefore be kept in well-sealed bottles and in darkness. It is necessary to store them in colored glass or tin containers, away from light and in a cool environment: the containers must be well stoppered, or the corks covered with parchment. The most suitable glass is amber-yellow in color.

As a preservative against resinification, the addition of 5 g of sodium bisulfite per kg of essential oil is recommended.

Small quantities of resinified essential oils can be restored by shaking them for 5-10 minutes with a paste made of equal parts animal charcoal and an aqueous borax solution: the resin thus combines with the borax, leaving the essence with its original odor; the mixture is then filtered.

Larger quantities can be restored by rectification, adding half their weight of fatty oil and a 3% solution of common salt.
