# Distillation and Aromatic Spirits (Alcoolati)

## Distillation Process

The distillation operation is complete when all the alcohol employed has been distilled off.

In some cases, and for certain substances, distillation is conducted so that the alcohol vapors come into contact with the botanical material without the material touching the liquid in the still. For this purpose, the material is suspended in a cloth bag within the still's head, and distillation then proceeds.

To the product of this first distillation, water is added in the same quantity as was used previously, and then rectification is performed.

The liquid residues (*phlegm*) from both operations are kept separate, and for some substances can later be reused in successive distillations in place of plain water, with fresh material in further operations.

### General Example

As a general rule: To 100 liters of alcohol at 85° in which aromatic substances have been macerated, add 50 liters of water and collect by distillation 100 liters. Subsequently, mix this product with 50 liters of distilled water and distill again (rectification) to obtain 96 or 97 liters of rectified aromatic spirit.

For aromatic spirits, unlike the alcoholates of pharmacies and perfumers, a uniform strength has not been adopted; therefore, it would be advisable to designate each product with an indication of its respective strength.

## Standard Proportions for Aromatic Alcoholates

The proportions generally used for the preparation of alcoholates are as follows:

| Ingredient | Quantity |
|---|---|
| Aromatic substances | 1 kg |
| Alcohol at 86° | 5 liters |

Add 2.5 liters of water to the maceration. The product of distillation (5 liters, as stated above) is mixed with 2.5 liters of water and rectified to obtain 5 liters of final product.

Aromaticates are all colorless and aromatic; their aroma increases with time and upon cooling.
