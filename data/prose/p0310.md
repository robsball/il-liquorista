# Liquor Manufacturing — Recipe Manual

We consider it essential to offer a few remarks on the general principles of liquor manufacturing and the procedure for mixing ingredients, as a supplement to the guidelines we have already briefly set out on pages 8–10.

In a large kettle, or basin, or alternatively in a well-cleaned barrel or demijohn, pour first the quantity of alcohol indicated, then the flavored alcohol (tincture, extract, alcoholate, etc.), then the essential oils or essences previously dissolved in alcohol, and in general all other substances that must enter into the composition of the liqueur. Next, mix the liquid thoroughly and add sugar in the quantity of water that is indicated, or alternatively the syrup, and mix again: finally pour in the remaining portion of water.

It is well to recall that the use of sugar dissolved hot—or better put, more or less concentrated syrup—is to be preferred to that of solution prepared cold (page 10).

If, instead of employing sugar and proceeding to the dissolution and eventual clarification of it, one must mix the syrup already prepared directly, it is advisable to pour this in thin streams and very hot into the alcoholic mixture: this favors the amalgamation of the various substances and the homogeneity of the final aroma.
