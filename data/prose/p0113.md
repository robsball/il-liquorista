The chemistry of perfumes has made great progress, and even without flowers or fragrant leaves, perfumes are indeed prepared that rival those of nature, but which still differ greatly from them in delicacy. The fact is that a natural scent is never simple: alongside a fundamental note, there are other barely perceptible notes, and all these olfactory notes together produce the perfume. Man-made scents, on the other hand, represent only the fundamental note, and for this reason they always turn out harsher and less pleasant than those provided by nature.

Moreover, the simplicity of our chemical laboratories cannot compete with the complexity of natural phenomena. Sometimes the perfume develops fleetingly within the flower: it is enough for the perfume to be produced, and then its development immediately stops, because it could otherwise become a poison for the plant.

In other cases the perfume is formed after death: this is the case with patchouli. Without a post-mortem fermentation of the patchouli leaves, this perfume would not exist, and its sharp scent is the last farewell of the cut leaves.

Besides this, synthetic artificial products are highly prone to degradation and thus lose the intensity of their aroma.
