It is not advisable to use the bottle cork as a dropper; proceeding in this manner one is never certain of the result and may even risk losing the entire quantity of liqueur in preparation. Indeed, beyond the different drop sizes caused by the position of the cork in the bottle neck, one must account for the moment when a sudden influx of air accelerates the flow. One must absolutely avoid using droppers with tubes or rubber stoppers, on which essential oils exert a dissolving action, thereby communicating an unpleasant taste to the product being prepared.

It is advisable instead to use common bottles with dropper caps, in which essential oils can readily be stored for limited consumption.

Naturally, a good supply and assortment of essential oils is necessary for the preparation of liqueurs by means of these or essences.

In discussing individual aromatic essences, we have noted the characteristics of such essential oils when freshly obtained, and the ease with which they deteriorate, both through the action of air and of light. To better protect against such deterioration, one must always use colored glass bottles and not leave much empty space in them, transferring the essence to another, smaller bottle when, due to consumption, such air-filled space has become excessive.

Before allowing a given essential oil to fall into alcohol or into the mixture of other essences, one must always ascertain its quality and good preservation; otherwise one risks spoiling a good product.

To some, these meticulous observations may seem pedantry, but it is worth bearing in mind that the success of the preparation depends in large part on the overall collection of such precautions and their scrupulous observance.

Thus, for example, when wishing to dilute a given liqueur—that is, to make it less strong—one must not add only water, but a mixture of water and alcohol previously prepared.
