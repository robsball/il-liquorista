In the cases just indicated, where very cloudy liquids are involved, one naturally proceeds to filter only the liquids obtained by decanting, after a resting period long enough not to harm the product. In various cases the liquid obtained by pressing is filtered as-is through a linen cloth, which constitutes a kind of coarse first filtration; all this in order to avoid clogging the filter's pores too quickly, which would delay filtration too much.

Filtration methods vary according to the nature of the liquids to be filtered and the degree of clarity required — ranging from the simplest, most primitive methods to those complicated and perfected by large-scale industry.

When only a coarse filtration is needed, without any pretension of obtaining a perfectly clear product, one uses fabrics of greater or lesser density — cotton, linen, or wool — or even felts arranged in various ways, as shown in our figures (figs. 13, 14, 15, 16).

Chamois leather is very useful for filtering syrups and maceration products. It must be degreased before use by means of a diluted alkaline solution (sodium carbonate) and then rinsed thoroughly. Filters made from this material, chosen of uniform thickness, allow rapid operation and, when carefully washed after each use, remain unaltered for a long time.
