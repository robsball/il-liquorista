# Artificial Liqueurs

## Artificial Liqueurs

These are beverages based on alcohol or spirits, made pleasant and sweetened through the addition of aromatic substances and sugar during production.

This group is divided into:

1) Spirits;
2) Liqueurs proper, or sweetened liqueurs, which differ from the spirit-type only in having a lower alcoholic strength and in the presence of sugar.

According to their method of preparation, artificial liqueurs are further divided into:

a) Liqueurs made by distillation;
b) Liqueurs made by maceration or digestion (commonly, though improperly, called 'infusion');
c) Liqueurs made by mixing essential oils (essences, extracts).

**Liqueurs made by distillation.** — Liqueurs obtained by this process have the advantage of containing the full aromatic character of the raw materials used, while being free of the volatile free oils, which generally impart a harsh taste to liqueurs and cause cloudiness.

The refinement of these liqueurs cannot be achieved through the maceration process, nor through the mixing of essences or extracts, which never attain so high a degree of delicacy or fragrance.

Distillation in fact makes it possible to obtain purer products — that is, free from those constituents that are sometimes useless or sometimes harmful or dangerous (such as alkaloid substances), which instead pass into tinctures prepared by maceration.

Moreover, distillation produces an intimate blending of the essential principles and aromas with one another and with the alcohol: the fragrances merge together and yield that delicate, refined bouquet that could not otherwise be achieved.
