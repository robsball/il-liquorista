**Laurel (Bay).** — From the fresh berries an essential oil is obtained (about 30%). It is prepared especially in Spain, Greece, Crete, and along the shores of Lake Garda. At ordinary temperature it has a buttery consistency and a somewhat bitter taste. It is often adulterated with tallow or pork fat and artificially colored with indigo, turmeric, and copper sulfate.

**Lavender.** — From the flowers the well-known and widely used essential oil is obtained. Its quality depends greatly on the method of preparation. The lowest grade is obtained from the stems, a medium grade from the flowering tops, and the finest from flowers stripped of their bracts and stalks. The best is the English oil (Mitcham and Hitchin varieties), while the French oil is of much lower value. Freshly prepared, lavender oil is yellowish and fluid, but with time it becomes brown and dense; it has a penetrating aromatic odor which becomes ever more delicate with age, and a somewhat bitter, warm, pungent taste.

**Lemon.** — From the peel of common lemons, the fruit of *Citrus limonum*, the essence or essential oil of lemon is obtained by simple hand-pressing over a sponge; distillation yields an inferior product. On average, lemon peels yield 1.0% oil. This oil is prepared mainly in Sicily, and Messina is its main export center. The oil obtained by pressing, before being rectified, is colored yellow and has the odor and taste of lemon. The refined oil is obtained by washing the crude oil and rectifying it; it is lighter in color and has a more delicate fragrance. Lemon oil is soluble in absolute alcohol and, in equal weight, in 95% alcohol. Adulterated versions are found on the market, mixed with oil of turpentine or diluted with alcohol; it is also replaced by an artificial product obtained by the action of hydrochloric acid on French oil of turpentine. The oil from lemon flowers has an odor very similar to that of the oil obtained from orange blossoms.

To verify its identity, triturate 1 gram of the essential oil under examination with sugar, then shake with 1/2 liter of distilled water; the liquid should exhibit a distinct lemon odor.
