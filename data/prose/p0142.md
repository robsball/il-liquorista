In practice, an excess of permanganate and a little alum are added to the water to be purified. When, after stirring, the pink coloration persists in the liquid, sodium hyposulfite is added. Filtering through absorbent cotton yields very pure water. Here are the proportions per liter of water:

| # | Ingredient | Quantity |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Potassium permanganate | 0.03 g |
| 1 | Common alum, powdered | 0.05 g |
| 2 | Crystallized sodium hyposulfite, powdered | 0.03 g |
| 2 | Dry commercial sodium carbonate | 0.05 g |

One can also improve the quality of a fairly good spring water, without resorting to distillation, by heating it after adding 15–20 g of sodium carbonate per hectoliter, letting it settle, and then decanting the clear portion — or, better still, filtering it.

To remove iron from water rich in it, Linde and Hess recommend treating the filtered water with wood sawdust that has been stripped of turpentine and impregnated with tin oxide. The hydrated iron oxide that forms is retained by the sawdust as a brownish deposit. The sawdust should be renewed every two months.

Sugar is one of the most important components of liqueurs, and therefore the choice of its quality must be the object of great attention on the part of the manufacturer; indeed, we have already mentioned how the classification of liqueurs is based precisely on the percentage of sugar they contain. We will therefore note that, while for the production of common or ordinary liqueurs and bitters one may use common sugars that are not perfectly refined, for fine liqueurs it is absolutely necessary to use top-quality sugar.
