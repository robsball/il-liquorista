It is essential practice to affix to every container a label clearly indicating the quality of the liquid it contains, the proportions of the components, the alcoholic strength, and, for mixtures, the date of preparation (1).

As for the layout of the workshop, no special rules can be established, since this depends on the conditions and capacity of the premises, the scale of production, etc. We can only recommend the most scrupulous cleanliness of the containers, proper ventilation of the rooms, and caution in handling materials, bearing in mind the flammability of alcohol and of certain essences. For the storage of raw materials (drugs) and of prepared liqueurs, damp and cold environments should be avoided; it is likewise necessary to keep them protected from intense light, whether natural or artificial, as light profoundly alters their color and sometimes also their flavor.

\* \* \*

Although this subject goes beyond the scope of this manual, we nevertheless think it appropriate to point out that liquor factories are subject to supervision by the tax authorities and by the health authority, and that the provisions concerning the circulation, storage, and processing of spirits are contained in the Law on Spirits of 30 January 1896 (No. 26) and in the Regulation for its implementation of 21 October 1903 (No. 419). We should add that by Royal Decree of 11 July 1909 a new law on the fiscal regime of spirits was promulgated, while the Government is currently preparing a Consolidated Text of all the various legislative and regulatory provisions concerning the production, possession, and trade, etc., of spirits.
