## Tinctures

Tinctures are aromatic spirits that are distinguished from alcoholates by their method of preparation. Whereas alcoholates are made by macerating aromatic substances in alcohol and then distilling, tinctures involve only a long maceration, or more effectively, leaching by displacement; in certain cases, digestion is preferable.

The result is always an alcoholic solution of aromatic principles, but by varying the method of preparation, the product is not at all the same. In fact, distillation yields only those aromatic substances that are volatile within the temperature limits produced; that is to say, one obtains a solution not only of essential oils, but also of other aromatic or less aromatic substances, bitter principles, alkaloids, resins, colorants, etc.

Tinctures are seldom used as such in commerce; ordinarily they are prepared directly by the liquor manufacturer and are generally called, in improper usage, infusions.

Tinctures are distinguished into simple tinctures or compound tinctures according to whether they have been prepared from a single substance or from diverse substances: those prepared from fresh plant material are called *alcoolaturi* (alcoholates).

One may furthermore consider as tinctures also the highly dilute solutions of essential oils in alcohol, whereas the concentrated ones are called essences, extracts, etc.
