# Correction of Acquavite

## Correction of Acquavite

Often acquaviti present strange or unpleasant flavors, whether due to poor quality of the distilled material, improper processing methods, or defects contracted from the containers.

When distilling diseased wines, if the disease affected the volatile parts of the wine, it is natural that the distilled product will suffer. Thus sour or acetified wines, if not first deacidified and if heads and tails are not separated during distillation, yield an acidic and unpleasant acquavite. If the acidity is less than 1.5 g per liter, it is not worth addressing, as it will disappear on its own over time by combining with the alcohol and increasing the quantity of esters that give acquavite its bouquet. If, however, it exceeds this figure, it must be neutralized with a light quantity of calcium carbonate, the exact dose of which can be determined by small-scale comparative trials. If the acquavite remains somewhat turbid, it will then need to be filtered. Sometimes acquavite has a sulfur odor because it was distilled from grape skins just fermented or from grapes heavily treated with sulfur. Aeration diminishes the defect, but to eliminate it entirely, it is better to resort to charcoal.

For each hectoliter of acquavite, take 1 kg of baker's bread (charred), pulverize it as finely as possible, and add it to the container holding the acquavite, stirring well with a stick.

After two or three days the acquavite can be removed, and if the liquid is not clear, treat it with gelatin. One should not use charcoal because it generally contains tars soluble in alcohol, which would impart a soot flavor to the acquavite. The acquavite can also be filtered, sheltered from air, through a bed of charcoal.

This charcoal process is not, however, free of drawbacks for fine acquaviti, since the pores of the charcoal can partially absorb not only hydrogen sulfide or similar products but also the characteristic bouquet of the acquavite, and often the acquavite acquires an abnormal flavor.

Treatment with copper thus seems more rational and simpler, since it is only necessary to suspend copper wire or plates in the container holding the acquavite. These will become coated with adherent copper sulfide, which can be removed with emery paper, and the treatment can be repeated if the first application was insufficient.
