They should be covered with a disc of sheet metal or wood.

In fact, in some large facilities a special device is used, which consists of a sealed cylindrical metal container equipped with a mechanical stirrer, into which various substances or preparations (water, alcohol, flavored spirit, syrup) from respective containers placed above are introduced via separate feed tubes, destined for the composition of the liqueur.

When the liqueur needs to be colored, the coloring tincture should be added in small portions until the desired shade is obtained—by means of repeated checks.

For a liqueur to be considered finished, that is, free from any roughness whatsoever and of good quality, it is necessary that after manufacture it be bottled or stored in demijohns and left to rest for some time, preferably in environments that are not too cold: cellars should therefore be avoided. It is well to remember that rest is favorable to the good quality of liqueurs and that the result of the preparation cannot be judged by tasting until after 2 or 3 days from the mixing: indeed the finesse of aroma, the sweetness and delicacy of flavor increase and improve more and more as the liqueur ages. In a special chapter we have already dealt with the means for preservation and artificial aging of liqueurs.

Regarding containers for the preservation of liqueurs, large wooden casks are to be preferred for large quantities, and stoneware or majolica vessels for small quantities, over tinned copper vessels or glass bottles: moreover, it should be observed that generally liqueurs acquire fineness and quality if the entire liquid mass is contained in large containers rather than subdividing it into small ones.
