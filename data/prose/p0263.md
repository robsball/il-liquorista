# On Artificial Fruit Ethers and Their Composition

Some so-called essences or essential oils are not true essences, but rather mixtures of ethers whose preparation generally presents no particular difficulty, requiring only careful attention and modest chemical laboratory equipment. We do not believe it necessary to describe here the procedures adopted to obtain the ethers that serve as raw materials in the mixtures constituting fruit essences, since a liquor manufacturer will rarely have occasion to prepare them himself. Rather, he may obtain them from reliable commercial suppliers, that is, from accredited firms that have specialized in this branch of industry. Moreover, those who wish to learn the details of this production may consult other works in this collection (1).

We shall therefore limit ourselves to presenting in a summary the composition of the mixtures by which the most common artificial fruit ethers are designated, to which we shall add some formulations gathered from the most authoritative treatises and formularies.

**Note.** — To prevent the confusion that may easily arise in the purchase and designation of these products, which are found in commerce under various names indicating their chemical constitution, we deem it opportune to indicate the synonymy of the most commonly used:

| Ether | Synonyms |
|-------|----------|
| Acetic ether | Ethyl acetate, ethyl acetic ether |
| Nitric ether | Sweet spirit of niter, spirit of nitroso ether |
| Butyric ether | Ethyl butyrate |
| Formic ether | Methyl ether, ethyl formate |
| Amyl valerianic ether | Amyl valerate; apple oil |
| Amyl butyric ether | Amyl butyrate |
| Benzoic ether | Ethyl benzoate |

Essences prepared with diluted alcohol have a stronger and more characteristic odor: some are additionally colored using aniline dyes, cochineal, or caramel.

(1) *Manuale del Profumiere* by A. Rossi or *Prodotti e Procedimenti* by I. Ghersi.
