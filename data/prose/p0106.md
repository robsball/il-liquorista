European rhubarb can be recognized by the absence, on its surface, of the dense network of medullary rays found in the Chinese variety; in rhapontic rhubarb, on the other hand, one finds a spongy central pith. Furthermore, European rhubarbs stain the saliva only slightly, do not crunch under the teeth, and leave a reddish powder.

Good-quality rhubarb should be in compact, dry, fibrous pieces, covered with yellow powder, and should show — both in cross-section and on the surface — the characteristics described for the Chinese variety. It is very prone to being attacked by woodworm, so it must be stored in a dark, dry place. The leaves, shoots, buds, and flowers are also edible.

The main components of rhubarb are: chrysophanic acid, cathartic acid, emodin, etc.

Medicinal properties: purgative or tonic, depending on the dose.

**Blackcurrant (Cassis).** — *Ribes nigrum* is a species distinct from redcurrant; it is widely cultivated in France for the manufacture of liqueurs and ratafias. The fruits grow in short, hanging clusters, with berries larger than those of the redcurrant. They have a sharp, tart, strongly aromatic flavor, similar to that of juniper. They are also used to flavor weak wines. The French name for these fruits is Cassis.

**Roses (Petals of).** — There is certainly no need to describe these flowers in detail here; we will only note that, among the many cultivated species and varieties of rose, either *Rosa pallida* or the red rose is used: the former is preferred in perfumery.

Centifolia roses are also used in preparing distilled rose water, aromatic spirits, and essences. Only fresh roses, or ones picked in dry weather, should be used.

**Rosemary.** — *Rosmarinus officinalis* is a well-known evergreen plant (Labiatae family), which gives off a strong, characteristic aromatic odor due to its essential oil.

**Rue.** — *Ruta graveolens* is a biennial plant about 50 cm tall. The whole plant gives off a strong, aromatic but rather unpleasant odor; its taste is bitter and acrid. When dried, it loses much of its characteristic odor.
