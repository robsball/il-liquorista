A liter of anhydrous alcohol weighs only 794.10 g at a temperature of +15.5°, and even less at higher temperatures. A spirit at 60% (percentage by weight) must therefore be stronger than a spirit at 60% percentage by volume.

Calculation in percentages by weight, that is, the determination of alcoholic content according to the Richter scale, has already been abandoned for a long time, because it leads to the incompatibility of purchasing spirit according to percentages by volume while determining its content according to percentages by weight. The new alcoholometers therefore carry only the Tralles scale.

In order to understand how to evaluate the percentage content by means of the alcoholometer, one must explain the general physical laws on which its use is based. We have already observed how weight is expressed by proportional figures, which indicate solely by how much a given volume of a liquid is lighter or heavier than an equal volume of pure water. Since the specific gravity of bodies is always constant, mixtures of bodies of different specific gravity can be recognized by this. By pouring water into alcohol, the mixture must become heavier. Conversely, the specific gravity must decrease as the percentage of alcohol increases. In the same way, specific gravity serves, in all other bodies, to establish certain particularities which constitute the value of the substances.

To determine the specific gravity of liquids, special balances called areometers (hydrometers) are used. The construction of these balances is based on the law that a body sinks into a liquid so deeply until the weight of the quantity of liquid displaced has reached its own weight. If the liquid is heavy (dense), the quantity of liquid displaced will be small and the heavy body will sink only slightly. If the liquid is light, the body will displace a greater quantity of it and will therefore sink more deeply. On a scale attached to the areometer one can read the specific gravity of the liquid.
