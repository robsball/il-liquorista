This oil is often adulterated with alcohol, chloroform, artificial essence, oil of mirbane (nitrobenzene), cedarwood oil, orange oil, etc.

To detect such adulterations, Hager recommends the following method: into a sufficiently large test tube, pour 25 c.c. of distilled water and let fall onto it 5 drops of the essence under examination.

If the essence is free of alcohol, the drops fall to the bottom as clear droplets even with slight shaking of the water column. In the presence of alcohol or chloroform, the drops become more or less cloudy, turning milky. The tube is then closed with the thumb and shaken vigorously. If the essence is pure, a clear solution generally results.

If this does not happen, the mixture is warmed to about 40°C and shaken again. Essences containing chloroform yield a milky liquid, from which the chloroform separates out in small droplets that settle to the bottom upon gentle agitation. This test is based on the solubility of bitter almond essence in water.

Artificial essence consists almost entirely of benzaldehyde; it has a less delicate odor than the natural essence, oxidizes readily, and is toxic, so it is little used despite its lower price.

To determine whether an essence is natural or consists of nitrobenzene, pour a few cubic centimeters of the suspect essence into a test tube and add a little alcoholic solution of caustic potash; shake vigorously and then let the mixture stand. If the product was natural, it will have dissolved completely in the alcoholic potash solution without any change of color; however, if resinous particles separate out which, when collected on a filter, do not dissolve in alcohol or ether, the product may be assumed to be artificial.

Several other procedures have been proposed for this kind of identification, but they fall within the competence of chemical laboratories, requiring special reagents.
