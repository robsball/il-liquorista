# Preparation and Use of Compound Essential Oils

Although these are ingredients that, when mixed with alcohol, water, or sugar in the proportions that will be indicated in due course, will compose the final product—the liqueur—these compound essential oils can be employed directly in liqueur preparation. Alternatively, when diluted with alcohol and mixed with other substances (tinctures), they constitute the so-called essences or extracts for liqueurs (see special chapter in Part III).

Nowadays, such products are found in commerce in quite varied compositions; indeed, the preparation of these compound essential oils is extremely simple and requires neither special apparatus nor special handling. Only the utmost scrupulous accuracy in weighing or measuring the individual essences in a glass vessel (preferably) and meticulous cleanliness of this vessel are to be recommended.

After the mixture has been made, it must be shaken vigorously; if it becomes turbid, it is left to clarify and then filtered. A large glass funnel and white filter paper should be used. Filtration is repeated until complete clarification is achieved (see fig. 15).

In general, it is best to prepare these essences or compound essential oils already conveniently diluted in strong alcohol, at the rate of approximately 50 parts of essence per 1000 of alcohol, keeping them thus ready for the final mixing with the other ingredients for liqueur composition. As we have already noted, the presence of alcohol serves to make the mixture more intimate, and liqueurs prepared with such alcoholic solutions turn out to have more pronounced flavor and be readily consumed.

**Observation.** Since the following recipes almost always involve mixtures of essential oils, for simplicity we shall not repeat this indication each time; by the word *anice* (anise), for example, we shall mean the essential oil of anise. Quantities are always expressed in grams.

Since the density of essential oils widely employed in liqueur manufacture oscillates within fairly narrow limits, it proves also more convenient to resort to volume measurement by means of graduated glass cylinders, rather than weighing, especially when the proportions of ingredients are small.
