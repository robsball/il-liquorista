The aroma test is better carried out as follows: heat a tin container with a lid, and pour into it a mixture of 2 to 4 drops of the oil with 50 drops of lukewarm water; cover it, and after a few minutes smell the aroma that escapes.

Rubbing a few drops of oil between the hands is a poor method. In old essences this test has no value, because some of them—such as lemon oil, for example—even when perfectly pure, acquire over time an odor of turpentine.

Adulteration with lower-quality oils can also be recognized by the degree of solubility of the oil under examination in absolute alcohol; however, the relevant ratios must be verified through specific tests.

**Considerations and rules concerning the use of essential oils.** — In the manufacture of liqueurs, simple ethereal oils or essences play the most important role. They give liqueurs their flavor and aroma. In hot-process manufacturing, most of the essential oils are supplied directly by the plants or plant parts from which the essences originate. By heating these plant parts together with the spirit in the still, the ethereal oils are dissolved by the alcohol and distill along with it. The distillate is therefore a spirit that contains in solution the respective ethereal oils, and precisely only these. The other substances that the spirit dissolves, but which are not volatile—that is, not distillable—remain in the boiler of the apparatus.

Cold-process manufacturing does not resort to distillation. It can indeed extract the essences from the plants by maceration in alcohol, but it cannot separate from the alcohol those foreign substances which are equally dissolved by it, and which may impart an unpleasant flavor to the liqueur or undesirably alter its color. Cold-process manufacturing should therefore make preferential use of essential oils.
