**Chamomile.** — Throughout Europe, *Chamomilla romana* and the common *Matricaria* grow wild: from flowers similar to those of the daisy, an essential oil is extracted.

Flowers that are not a clean, beautiful white, or that have not been properly dried, should be rejected, since they have lost much of their aroma.

**Cinnamon.** — The name cinnamon refers to the dried bark of the young branches of certain evergreen trees belonging to the genus *Cinnamomum* (Lauraceae family). Depending on its origin, it is called Ceylon Cinnamon, Fine Cinnamon, or Queen Cinnamon. It is cultivated not only in Ceylon but also in China, Malabar, India, Central America, Brazil, etc.

The best is that from Ceylon; it comes in long thin bundles about one centimeter in diameter, of a fairly light brown color. Chinese cinnamon has a coarser, darker bark in shorter bundles; it is less aromatic, although of more intense odor and pleasant, spicy, aromatic flavor.

**Cardamom.** — Throughout most of tropical Asia, *Elettaria cardamomum* and its varieties (Zingiberaceae family) are cultivated. The seeds are marketed still enclosed in the fruit to better preserve them; these fruits consist of oblong, more or less angular, leathery capsules containing many small seeds with a spicy, aromatic flavor and pleasant odor; these seeds constitute the useful part of the cardamom. The most highly regarded come from Malabar, Cambodia, Siam, Sumatra, and Java. Two varieties are especially distinguished: Lesser Cardamom and Greater Cardamom; the latter is the more prized.

The seeds (without the fruit) of *Amomum melegueta* from West Africa (from Sierra Leone to the Congo) are also found in commerce.

These seeds, even when crushed, do not have a very aromatic odor but have a spicy flavor, similar to that of pepper. They are also known as Melegueta pepper or Grains of Paradise.
