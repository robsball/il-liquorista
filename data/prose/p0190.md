# Filtration Apparatus

It is good practice that the liquid does not drip into the middle of the receiving vessel, causing splashing and loss, but rather flows down along its walls, by means of a lateral deviation of the funnel. The best funnels are those made of glass, inclined at an angle of 60°.

When the funnel closes perfectly over the neck of the vessel beneath it, so that air has no way out, an increase in pressure is produced within the vessel itself, which becomes an obstacle to filtration. To remedy this, some object is placed between the neck of the funnel and that of the bottle, so as to allow air to pass through.

It is excellent practice to cover the funnel with a sheet of glass, or even simply with tin, in order to protect it from airborne dust or to reduce the evaporation of volatile substances. For this purpose, a special arrangement, shown in Figure 18, serves very well.

The filter is placed in the funnel T, which communicates with the vessel F; this is closed with a stopper of elastic rubber (or better, of soft cork) with two holes; through one of these passes a tube r that communicates, by means of a rubber tube k, with the other tube rt in the lid.

In this way, the air expelled from vessel F passes above the liquid in the funnel, and the vapors it may contain are not lost, just as those given off by the liquid on the filter are not lost either.
