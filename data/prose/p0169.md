For example, one liter of 85° alcohol is poured into a wide-necked vessel with a capacity of about 2 liters. Separately, 125 grams of a dried vegetable substance (caraway, cinnamon, anise, etc.) are wrapped in a small bag made of light, well-washed fabric (cloth or gauze folded double). By means of a string passed through the stopper used to close the vessel, the little bag is suspended at a height of 4–5 cm above the surface of the alcohol, in such a way that it does not come into contact with the liquid. The vessel is then sealed tightly — if necessary luting the rim of the neck with paraffin — and left at rest, either in complete stillness or at ordinary room temperature, for 2–3 months.

After this period one obtains a most excellent, colorless spirit of Caraway, Cinnamon, or Anise, which can be used to prepare the corresponding liqueur.

The phenomenon by which the spirit becomes aromatized is quite simple: it is well known that alcohol, at ordinary temperature, gives off vapors. These vapors permeate the fabric of the little bag and thus the botanical drug enclosed within it, becoming saturated with essential oil. Having thus become denser and heavier, they fall back down into the alcohol in the vessel and sink to the bottom, where in part they become concentrated, while at the surface of the spirit new evaporation of alcohol occurs continuously. In this way the process continues indefinitely, in a regular cycle.

This procedure, though quite simple and elegant, has the sole drawback of being rather slow and lengthy. However, it could be greatly facilitated and accelerated if it were possible to rarefy the interior of the vessel by means of a vacuum pump, fitting the stopper with a small glass tube connected to a suction pump, and then sealing it off by flame.

In any case, this method is recommended for preparing small quantities of alcoholates, since it avoids the inconveniences, nuisances, and dangers presented by direct-fire or steam distillation.
