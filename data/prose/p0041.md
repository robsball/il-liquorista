The longer the column of charcoal is placed, the longer the alcohol stays in contact with the charcoal, and the purer it emerges. The charcoal casks have, at a certain distance from the bottom, a second perforated bottom that supports the column of charcoal. In the empty space between this and the lower bottom, the purified alcohol flows. The perforated bottom is covered with a coarse cloth, on which is spread a layer 10-15 cm high of coarse sand, previously well washed, whose purpose is to retain the particles of charcoal that pass along with the alcohol. This layer of sand is left fixed even when the charcoal is renewed. The charcoal is poured immediately on top of the sand layer and compressed from time to time so as to be uniformly even throughout.

When the cask is filled to a depth of about 50 cm, the charcoal is covered with a layer of pure straw, which is covered with a perforated lid, and finally the cask is closed with the upper lid, applied as airtight as possible. So that the alcohol entering the cask through an opening in the upper lid can flow down into the lower layers, an air outlet is created by inserting through the upper bottom a tin tube that reaches into the empty spaces and which, bent upward like an elbow, reaches up to half the height of the cask. A tap at the bottom of the cask serves to drain off the purified alcohol.

If one wishes to use bone charcoal together with wood charcoal, the former is placed in a layer of 20-30 cm above the sand layer. Charcoal in coarse powder or pieces, free of fine dust, is the most suitable for this purpose.

Some distillers mix other substances with the charcoal, such as manganese dioxide, magnesium carbonate, etc. All these substances serve no purpose whatsoever and it is therefore better to omit them.

Moistening the charcoal from time to time while filling the casks diminishes the charcoal's activity.

It is essential, however, to reduce the alcohol to 60% before pouring it into the purification casks. Alcohol at a high percentage is not completely freed of fusel oil. The portions of alcohol that pass through the charcoal first have an unpleasant taste and must be poured over the charcoal again until they are tasteless.
