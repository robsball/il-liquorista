Such impurities, which result from the pressing process, mostly separate out from the essential oil after a long period of time. It is nevertheless advisable not to wait for this to happen naturally, but rather to purify in advance any essential oil that is to be stored for a long time, by means of washing. For this purpose, one part of pure caustic soda solution is mixed with 4 parts of water, and this mixture is poured into 2 parts of citron essential oil.

The mixture is shaken frequently and then left to settle. The clear essential oil floats on top of the lye, which soon turns brown, having absorbed the impurities of the essence in the form of brown flakes. An oil washed in this way remains usable for a long time; it also dissolves in alcohol more readily than an unwashed essence.

Adulterations of citron essential oil are commonly carried out using sweet orange essence and turpentine. Both are difficult to detect.

**Cognac Oil, or Grape-Seed Oil.** — This oil is obtained from the residues of wine fermentation, and in its crude state is yellow-green; once rectified it is colorless, with a pleasant wine-like odor. The green color of some commercial products is artificial. This essence is used partly to adulterate (cut) cognac, and partly as an addition to fine liqueurs to give them a hint of wine aroma and flavor. Used in moderation, it gives excellent results.

Artificially prepared oenanthic ether has the odor, but not the flavor, of cognac essence.

**Cumin.** — Freshly prepared cumin essential oil is colorless and fluid, but with time it thickens and turns yellow and acidic. It has a penetrating, unpleasant odor, similar to the fruit, and a bitterish aromatic taste. It is composed of cymol (33%), which is a colorless liquid with a pleasant odor, and of cuminol.

**Coriander.** — Coriander essential oil is nearly colorless; it is soluble in alcohol. It is often adulterated with orange peel oil.
