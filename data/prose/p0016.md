**Liqueurs by maceration.** — These are obtained by letting aromatic substances macerate for a more or less prolonged period in strong or diluted alcohol (see *Maceration*). This method is the oldest and almost the only one followed in domestic practice, since it requires no special equipment, while industry prefers processes based on distillation or the blending of essences, which yield finer products with delicate aroma and flavor, or which are more economical to produce.

Maceration is used to obtain liqueurs based on aromatic substances that are fixed or that would be altered by the action of heat: this method is particularly suited to the preparation of ratafias and those liqueurs that must derive their coloring from the raw materials themselves, as well as for most of the bitters (amari), etc. Likewise, from an industrial standpoint, besides ratafias and bitters, the alcoholic tinctures obtained by maceration and digestion are of interest only as raw material to be used in preparing the blends intended to give color to the finished liqueurs, or as those very concentrated compound tinctures, rich in aromatic and coloring principles, which are well known and today widely used in domestic practice under the name of *extracts* (estratti), and which, suitably mixed in fixed proportions with alcohol, water, and sugar, yield the liqueur without further processing.

Reserving a more detailed description of the processes and the necessary apparatus for later (see illustration), we will note here that true tinctures are classified as simple or compound and are called *alcoolati* or *alcoolaturi*, depending on whether they were prepared using dried or fresh raw materials. Through maceration and digestion, as well as through continuous displacement leaching (see *Maceration*), which is a refinement of this method, alcohol extracts from plants not only the volatile principles but also a great number of fixed, soluble substances that could not be isolated by distillation.
