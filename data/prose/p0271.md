# Use of Hydrometers and Densimeters

## Use of Hydrometers and Densimeters

They directly indicate the density of the liquid in which they are immersed. For these instruments too, the limit of flotation in distilled water at 4°C—that is, at its maximum density—is found at the upper extremity of the stem and is marked as 1000; the divisions marked below this correspond to successive increasing densities in tenths, from 1000 to 2000.

For example, a liquid in which the immersed densimeter reads the division 1270 has a density of 1270; that is, one liter will weigh 1270 grams. This provides a means to verify the accuracy and proper construction of the densimeter by means of a simple but rigorous weighing of the liquid.

It is understood that the densimeter readings must be observed at the temperature for which it was calibrated; therefore, for different temperatures a correction is necessary. Generally this temperature is 15°.

In commerce, hydrometers are found equipped with a dual scale: the conventional Baumé scale and the corresponding density scale. In the absence of these, one resorts to the correspondence table found on the following page.

For the handling of hydrometers and densimeters for syrups, we recommend the use of a series of two or three instruments, to avoid a stem that is too long or a scale with divisions that are too crowded: in this way one will be able to observe with greater accuracy the half-divisions of degrees and even the quarters.

Likewise, one must not forget that temperature exerts a great influence on density, and therefore it is necessary that measurements, especially for syrups, be observed in the liquid brought to the normal temperature of 15°C. In fact, a syrup which at boiling temperature reads 31° Baumé, when cooled instead to 15°C will read 35°.

Finally, it must be remembered that when immersing the hydrometer in the syrup to evaluate its density, one must avoid plunging the stem too deeply into the liquid; since the liquid, adhering by its own viscosity to the portion of stem that then remains emergent, contributes to increasing the weight of the instrument and thus provides an erroneous reading.
