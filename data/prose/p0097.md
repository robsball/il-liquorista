**Galanga.** — The root of Galanga is none other than the rhizome of *Alpinia officinarum*, a plant similar to reeds/canes, cultivated in southern China.

In commerce it is called "Lesser Galanga" and comes in irregular pieces, no more than 2 cm thick and 3 to 7 cm long, often branched. They are hard, wrinkled, and reddish-brown in color. When crushed they give off a pungent, pleasant odor; they have a strong, burning, aromatic taste.

Greater Galanga differs from the above in its larger size (*Alpinia Galanga*); it comes from Java. All these rhizomes contain two essential oils, to which they owe their characteristic odor.

**♦ Cloves (Clove Buds).** — Under this name are designated the dried flower buds of *Eugenia caryophyllata*, a tree native to the Moluccas and Central America. They have a pleasant odor and a pungent, aromatic taste that are well known. The best are those from the Moluccas, known as "English cloves." They are often adulterated. One of the most common frauds consists of putting them on the market already stripped of their essential oil; to mask the fraud they are coated with a layer of fatty oil containing small amounts of essential oil. This fraud can easily be detected by placing some cloves on water: if they are spent (exhausted of oil) they float, if not, they sink.

**Alpine Genepì.** — This is the name given to *Artemisia glacialis* or *Artemisia spicata* (Compositae), a highly prized and sought-after little plant that grows among granite rocks and glacier moraines beneath the snow (1800–3000 m) in the Alps of Piedmont, Savoy, and Switzerland. It has leaves that are divided or covered with white down, yellow flowers with a very aromatic odor and a bitter taste; the fruits are fleshy black berries with a sugary taste. It is used in folk medicine in the form of an infusion (tisane) as a sudorific or stimulant.
