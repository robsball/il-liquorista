# Arak. Genuine Arak

## Arak: Genuine Arak

The term arak, rack, or arrocca designates in commerce the spirits prepared in India, China, and South America through distillation of various fermented liquids obtained from rice, plant and flower juices, sugar manufacturing residues, etc. In Malesia, this name is given to the spirit of molasses, rice, and areca palm; in India, to those from aromatic plants; in Ceylon, to those obtained from juices and flowers of certain plants, etc.

The coastal Malabar region, Ceylon, Java, and Siam are the principal centers of this manufacture.

Arak is, however, distilled in other countries as well, which produce rice or can obtain it at favorable prices. Holland, particularly Amsterdam, imports the largest quantity of arak and serves as the best source of purchase for Germany. The finest qualities bear the name Arak de Goa; the inferior ones, Arak de Batavia; the most refined qualities are distinguished also by the name Arak di Mandarini.

According to its mode of preparation, arak stands in close relationship with the alcohol obtained from cereals and potatoes.

Like them, it is not obtained directly from sugar, but from starch converted into sugar—that is, from rice starch. The process is the same as for other alcohol.

The aroma that develops during fermentation is quite pleasant and fine, so much so that it cannot be compared to the fusel oil of the alcohol qualities already described.

It too improves with aging, and more quickly in barrel than in bottle. If arak is kept in barrels, however, it naturally acquires, through contact with oak wood, an undesirable yellowish color.

This liquor is colorless or slightly yellowish; it has a very characteristic and penetrating aroma, contains on average 58° Tralles of alcohol, and appears to contain no phlegm oil. It is used especially in Holland or in Germany to prepare grog and pimento essence.
