**Common caramel.** — This is prepared in the same way as the previous product, using molasses and glucose instead of white sugar.

Caramel is used to tint all those liqueurs found on the market with a yellowish-tawny coloration. It is also used to deepen the color of curaçao and of absinthes.

Sugar coloring or caramel has, however, the drawback of causing cloudiness in liqueurs with a high alcoholic percentage, and is therefore poorly suited for use in these. The brown of catechu (see Terra Catechu) is to be preferred.

**Calendula (Marigold).** — A very common little plant found in meadows and also cultivated in gardens ("flower of every month"). The flowers are eaten in salads or dried, and constitute a rather intense coloring substance, providing a tint similar to that of saffron.

**Catechu (Terra Cattù).** — This is the name given to the dry aqueous extract obtained from the wood of certain species of Acacia, trees native to the Indies. It comes on the market in compact, shapeless masses. Several varieties are distinguished, among which the most prized are those from Peru, Lahore [Cambir], and Bengal, or the reddish kind.

Terra Catechu is poorly soluble in water, very soluble in alcohol, and provides an intense brown tint.

**Kermes (Grain of Provence).** — This is the name given to an insect, the Coccus Ilicis, which lives on a tree common in southern France and Spain, the Quercus coccifera. Once gathered and dried, these insects have the form of a small smooth pellet, the size of a pea, open on the underside, reddish-brown in color, friable, and covered with a whitish powder. On the market one distinguishes Kermes of Provence, which is the richest in coloring substance and the most prized, and Kermes of Spain.
