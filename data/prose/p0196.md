Another substance that has been recommended for clarifying liqueurs is so-called siliceous sand (Kieselguhr), which, however, before use must be carefully washed several times with distilled water, filtered, dried, and then calcined in an iron vessel. One or two tablespoons of it are used per liter of liqueur, shaking the liqueur with it and then filtering the resulting cloudy mixture.

Besides these methods, the use of milk as a clarifying agent has also been proposed, as has the use of leftover resinous drug residues (myrrh, aloe, china bark, etc.) used in the preparation of tinctures — it being understood, however, that such residues can only be used for those liqueurs whose composition already includes the bitter aromatic principles contained in the spent drugs, so that their flavor is not altered.

Clarification of liqueurs proceeds much more quickly in warm rooms than in cold ones.

Regarding the clarification of liqueurs, we may note that when time and very tall containers are available, it is preferable to wait for the liqueur to clarify naturally, and then draw it off by decanting.

### Aging

It is well known that liqueurs gain in delicacy and aroma with age. To a certain extent, one can substitute artifice for the action of time: this consists of heating the liqueur in a water bath to a temperature slightly below that used in distillation, then immediately removing it from the heat and allowing it to cool. The action of heat, in this case too, produces — as already mentioned regarding the dissolving of sugar — a more complete homogeneity, a perfect blending of the elements that make up the liqueur.

This operation must be carried out before coloring, since otherwise the vividness of the color would be affected.
