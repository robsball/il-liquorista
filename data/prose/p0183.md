# Sweetening

The quantities of sugar to be added to a given quantity of liqueur vary, and depend both on the quality of the liqueur itself and on the taste of consumers. Consequently, absolute, unchangeable figures for the amount of sugar to be used for a given quantity of liqueur cannot be established; one can only fix the limits within which the appropriate quantity falls.

Commonly, liqueurs should contain:

| Type of liqueur | Grams of sugar per liter |
|---|---|
| Crème | 350-550 |
| Rosolio | |
| Fine liqueurs | 175-325 |
| Ordinary liqueurs | 100-150 |
| Eaux-de-vie (spirits) | 20-50 |

Experience shows that a homogeneous product cannot always be obtained unless the sugar is dissolved separately in water. To achieve rapid and complete dissolution of the sugar, it is boiled with enough water that the solution reaches a syrupy density. Insoluble impurities separate out during boiling as foam on the surface and can be removed with a skimmer. The denser the solution, the more quickly and completely purification occurs (see Syrup, Part II). It must be noted, however, that in this case crystals should not be allowed to form on the walls of the boiler, since if these crystals reach a strongly sugared liqueur undissolved, they can trigger the crystallization of larger quantities of sugar. As soon as such crystals form, they should be removed from the walls of the vessel with water.

The sugar solution can be added to the aromatic spirit as soon as it has cooled somewhat.
