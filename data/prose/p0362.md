# Artificial Liqueurs

As we have already indicated in discussing the definition and classification of liqueurs, artificial liqueurs are liqueurs properly so called, and are constituted by the mixture of aromatic spirits—obtained by distillation or by extraction from plant materials through maceration or digestion, or finally by dilution of simple essential oils or compound essences—with alcohol, water, and sugar (syrup), sometimes with the addition of coloring tincture.

It is not necessary to observe how the distinction of liqueurs according to the origin or production of aromatic spirit is neither absolute nor categorical, and that therefore a liqueur should not be prepared exclusively from one source—either an alcoholate, or a tincture, or an essence as the fundamental substance: indeed, there are numerous mixtures in whose composition alcoholates, tinctures, and essences enter simultaneously, as we shall see later; and likewise there are liqueurs which can be obtained from either one or another form of fundamental product. We can cite, for example, liqueurs based on Mint, Anise, Alchermes, Vespertro, etc., for which we shall expound recipes following the various procedures now indicated.
