**Chamomile.** — The essential oil of Roman chamomile is dark blue when obtained from fresh flowers, and greenish-yellow when obtained from dried flowers.

The oil of common chamomile is extracted from the flowers of *Matricaria chamomilla*: it is a dense liquid, deep blue, almost opaque. In commerce, chamomile oil is often found substituted with turpentine oil or lemon oil distilled over chamomile flowers.

**Cinnamon.** — The essential oil extracted from Ceylon cinnamon is colorless, but with time turns golden yellow, and eventually brown. It flows sluggishly, has a penetrating, sweet, lasting flavor and a characteristic odor. Good-quality oil is heavier than water and must leave on the tongue a persistent flavor sweeter than sugar. It contains cinnamic aldehyde and eugenol.

The oil that comes from Chinese cinnamon, or Cassia, is light yellow when freshly prepared, but later turns brown. It is rather dense, with an odor similar to that of cinnamon and a flavor that is at first sweet, then burning. It is denser than water.

These oils must be kept in well-sealed yellow glass containers.

**Cardamom.** — All the seeds of the Amomum plants contain an essential oil also known as balsam of Amomum. It is yellowish, with a camphor-like odor and an acrid flavor; it is soluble in alcohol. On cooling it produces crystals very similar to camphor.

Grains of Paradise contain considerably less essential oil than the other Amomum species.

**Caraway.** — The essential oil obtained by distillation of these seeds flows freely, is light yellow when fresh, but yellows in a short time, especially if exposed to light; the finer qualities resist longer. The odor and flavor are very pronounced and characteristic, similar to those of the fruit.
