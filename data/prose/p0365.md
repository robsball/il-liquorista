## Anise Liqueur (conclusion)

Alcoholate, p. 4–3 of alcohol at 85°, p. 4–2 of water—depending on whether one wishes to obtain an ordinary product (46% alcohol), fine (52%), or finest quality (65%).

In order to make the liqueur turbid and ready when mixed with water as a beverage, one can dissolve, for every liter of 85° alcohol to be added, 1 gram of essential oil of star anise.

We do not think it worthwhile to dwell further on this product, since its use, degenerated into abuse, is now condemned by hygiene as highly harmful. Indeed, recently in some foreign states a crusade has been launched against this liqueur, proscribing its preparation and sale.

## Liqueurs by Distillation: General Principles

The aroma and flavor of distilled liqueurs can be obtained either by employing one or several previously prepared alcoholates, appropriately combined in determined proportions, or instead by preparing directly the compound alcoholate through distillation with alcohol of a mixture of plant substances. As we have already noted, to the alcoholate, however obtained, one can then also add tinctures, essential oils, essences, etc. for the final composition of the liqueur.

In the first case, the preparation of the liqueur is rapid and very simple, and can almost be compared to that by blending and dilution of essences, or cold-mixing: indeed such preparation is reduced to the use of already-prepared products.

In the second case, instead, one must proceed to the preparation of the necessary alcoholate, destined exclusively for a specific liqueur. It is superfluous to add that liqueurs obtained by this means represent the most complex and varied products, for which the initiative of the maker is exercised and the skill of the fabricant can be displayed.
