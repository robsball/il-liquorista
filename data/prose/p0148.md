# Coloring Materials

In recent years, the foreign chemical industry has developed several synthetic products with intense sweetening power, corresponding to 300-400 times that of sugar. Among these are Dulcin, Sucrol, Saccharin, Valzin, Sucrose [sic], etc.

However, these cannot be used for liqueurs, syrups, marmalades, etc., because they readily undergo alteration.

## Coloring Materials

Many of the liqueurs that can be made using the recipes in this manual, as well as others not indicated here, when obtained by distillation or by mixing essences, turn out colorless. However, to satisfy a certain public demand—which need not be debated here—it is common practice to color them in various ways using special coloring materials.

Granting the use of artificial coloring, it is necessary that it be produced with substances that are in no way harmful to the health of consumers, and that do not alter the fineness of the aroma or modify the flavor of the liqueur.

Few substances meet these requirements; most come from the plant kingdom. Some are exclusively products of the chemical industry, such as the so-called aniline dyes, that is, those extracted from coal tar.

We now provide a brief overview of these coloring materials; in another chapter we will indicate how to use them and their respective preparations (see *Coloring Tinctures*, in Part II).

**Oriental Alkanet.** — The root of *Lawsonia inermis* is dark red in color, with an astringent, bitter, and highly styptic taste. A yellow coloring matter is extracted from the leaves of this plant, though it is little used, as is the root itself. It should not be confused with false Alkanet or Alkanna (Orcanet).

**Caramel.** — This is the yellow [coloring agent] most commonly used for coloring liqueurs. It can be obtained from sugar molasses, white sugar, or glucose.
