# Kirschwasser and Cherry Distillation

One must distinguish Kirschwasser or cherry brandy (acquavite di ciliege) from the spirit that can be obtained by simply distilling these fruits.

In years when an abundance of certain fruits or adverse climatic conditions make their sale at remunerative prices difficult, it may be advisable to transform part of the harvest into brandy. For this reason, the distillation of cherries has become an important and profitable branch of industry in some regions.

For this purpose, crushed cherries are left to ferment in vats or casks and then transferred to the still. According to analyses, cherries—especially mountain varieties—contain about 8% sugar, which through fermentation yields approximately 4% pure alcohol, and thus about 6% of excellent brandy with an agreeable taste and delicate aroma.

The aroma of Kirschwasser recalls that of a good grain brandy, but proves far more pleasant because of the bitter almond flavor from the pits. This distillate too improves with aging.

Although nowadays this liqueur is prepared artificially on a large scale, the most prized product remains that obtained directly through fermentation of cherries. For this purpose, wild black cherries or woodland varieties are used; when fully ripe, they are crushed together with their pits and left to ferment in cellars in suitable vessels, optionally adding sugar to increase the alcohol yield. Once fermentation is complete, the mass is transferred to stills and distilled carefully at moderate heat; the various distillates are then blended and rectified to obtain a brandy of average strength 40°–50°, which is left to rest for a long time, as the flavor becomes increasingly delicate after a few years.

On average, one quintal of cherries yields 8–10 liters of Kirschwasser at an alcoholic strength of 45–50%.
