# On the Use of Essential Oils and Preparation of Extracts

These liquors are aromatized with essential oils alone; however, it must be observed that by this means one does not obtain the same liquors as when using alcoholic extracts of various botanicals—herbs, roots, seeds, etc.

Essential oils impart to liquors only the characteristic odor of the plant but not always its true flavor. This—as we have already repeated—is moreover determined by a content of resins and bitter substances.

Essential oils yield a refined product only if the aim is to obtain the aroma produced by the oil itself. A mint liquor, for example, will have a much finer flavor if prepared by dissolving mint essential oil than if a tincture prepared from the plant itself is used.

The preparation of these extracts is so simple that it proves feasible for anyone. However, it demands great care, cleanliness of vessels, and precision in weighing.

If the extract is intended for the preparation of a liquor that is given a fanciful name, it is necessary that none of the components predominate in such a way as to characterize any special flavor, but from the blend of all into one should result a compound pleasant to the palate.

When, however, one deals with a liquor of determined denomination—for example, Lemon Rosolio—the various accessory aromas must always allow that from which the liquor takes its name to predominate.

Wishing to compose new ones, one must have very pronounced senses of taste and smell; there is thus required a special ability, which is acquired only through long practice.

The preparation of extracts (tinctures) cannot be made, for practical reasons, with spirit of 90–95%; an extract thus prepared would become turbid when mixed with 40% spirit, as is used for liquors, or with brandy.

One may use for these preparations spirit at 80–90°; however, when dealing with substances containing resins or other matter that might—dissolving in the spirit—harm the delicacy of the product to be obtained or produce turbidity, it is advisable to use spirit at 60–70° to avoid such dissolutions.
