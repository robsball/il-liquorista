# Determination of Essential Oils, Alcohol Content, and Sugar in Liqueurs

## Determination of Essential Oils

To finally determine essential oils, a small quantity of the upper layer of liquid is placed in a porcelain capsule or in a large watch glass, or it is exposed to a temperature of approximately 36°C. The ether evaporates first in this way, then the alcohol. When there is almost no residue left in the vessel, the vapors are directed toward the nose and smelled. In this operation a practiced person can easily distinguish the individual aromas.

Continuing to hold the watch glass in a warm hand and wafting the vapors, the sensation of smell changes, because initially the most volatile essential oils evaporate, while those with a higher boiling point remain until last and only evaporate later.

Essential oils can also be determined in another way, by means of sulfuric ether.

One liter of liqueur is poured into a bottle, one-tenth of a liter of ether is added, the bottle is sealed, it is shaken vigorously for at least ten minutes, and then left at rest until the ether, in which all the aromas have dissolved, has collected at the surface.

By means of a pipette, this solution is withdrawn and then poured into a porcelain capsule and exposed to a water bath at a temperature not exceeding 30°C.

At this temperature the ether volatilizes very rapidly, leaving the essential oils, which are examined by smell.

## Determination of Alcohol Content

To ascertain the quantity of alcohol contained in the liqueur to be examined, a determined quantity is measured—for example, one liter—it is placed in the distillation apparatus or two-thirds is slowly distilled.

To the distilled liquid, distilled water is added to reduce it to one liter in total. It is shaken and a Tralles alcoholometer is immersed in the mixture; the alcohol percentage indicated by this corresponds to the alcohol percentage contained in the liqueur.
