**Bilberry (Berries).** — Bilberries are a well-known fruit of an evergreen shrub that grows in shady mountain locations. The berries are about the size of a grape seed: they are whitish-red and, when ripe, take on a black-bluish coloration. The berries are used after drying, but they must not be too old nor spoiled by worms.

**Alkanet (Orcanetto or Ancusa).** — This is the name given to *Anchusa tinctoria*, or false alkanna, a perennial plant of sandy soils in Italy, France, and Spain. Its root is used, which is found in commerce in pieces of red color, with rough bark: it is odorless and tasteless and contains an intensely coloring red substance, alkannin (ancusina), soluble in alcohol, and therefore it is widely used to tint dental waters, liqueurs, cosmetics, etc.

**Orchil (Oricello).** — Under this name are indicated various lichens, from which a violet coloring substance is obtained following special fermentations (orcin/orcein).

In commerce one finds Orchil paste, Orchil extract, Cudbear, and Persio, all of which give a violet solution: by treating this with an acid, the color turns intense red.

**Saffron.** — One of the most intense yellow coloring substances is saffron (1 cubic centimeter of the 10% alcoholic tincture is sufficient to color one liter of liqueur). However, as is well known, saffron possesses a very pronounced sui generis odor, which sometimes constitutes a significant drawback in the preparation of liqueurs. To attenuate it — since completely masking it is difficult — the following procedure may be adopted: spread the saffron evenly over a horsehair sieve and place this sieve inside an enameled iron basin containing 1½ liters of water (per 100 grams of saffron): boil the water until one liter has evaporated. After this, remove the sieve and take up the residue, once dry, with alcohol to prepare its tincture as will be indicated in the special chapter.
