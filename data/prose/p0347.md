# Whisky: Origins, Character, and Imitation Formulas

Whisky is aged in the manner practiced for brandy and cognac. Two types are distinguished: pot-still whisky, which is richer in esters and more aromatic; and rectified whisky, which is further divided into Irish whisky (made with malt and grains) and Scotch whisky (made only with roasted malt). In Germany and Russia it is known by the name Kornbranntwein.

The aroma of whisky depends also on the quality and quantity of water used in fermentation, and especially in dilution—since the principal constituent is an oil with an unpleasant odor that becomes pleasant when diluted—as well as on the nature of the malt, the method of drying, and the conditions of the environments and apparatus in which it is made and the vessels in which it is stored.

We limit ourselves to these notes, since this product is very little used among us; and, regarding imitations, we absolutely advise against those employing fusel oil as a base, which is proscribed by hygiene as harmful, unless it is in conditions of absolute purification, such as cannot be found in commerce.

We indicate instead some artificial mixtures:

### Imitations

**I.**

Prepare by mixing:
- Acetic ether: 2 g
- Butyric ether: 2 g
- Rum ether: 4.5 L
- Water: 5 L
- Brown candi sugar: 50 g

Add to the mixture ½ liter of decoction obtained from 80 g of barley malt.

**II.**

In a pot, dissolve 1.5 kg of brown crystallized sugar in 14 liters of hot water; then pour this solution while still hot over a mixture of finely powdered barley malt (2 kg) and rye malt (1 kg); stir.
