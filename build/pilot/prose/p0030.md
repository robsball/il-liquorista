## Alcohol from Potatoes

First, some brief information on the various processes for producing ethyl alcohol and on the diverse qualities found in commerce, which, carefully purified and rectified, are employed in the manufacture of liqueurs.

The high starch content found in potatoes makes them a special material that can be used for alcohol production.

Starch is not, in its natural state, a fermentable body; however, it represents the material from which the living plant, through the process of vegetation, produces sugar, or which can also be converted by artificial means (dilute mineral acids) into sugar. In seeds, the conversion occurs through the process of germination.

Therefore, if one wishes to convert the starch of cereals into sugar, one must first produce malt from it.

In the living seed, the conversion is accomplished by the action of certain nitrogenous substances (proteins). The potato does not possess these substances; the formation of sugar cannot therefore occur by natural means. The addition of approximately 5% of cereal malt to the paste of cooked potatoes, however, provokes the formation of sugar in a short time, so that the paste formed after cooling can immediately enter into vigorous fermentation by means of yeast; once this is complete, the alcohol is separated by distillation.

Besides alcohol, another ethereal body that gives the aroma is also produced. In the present case, the aroma (!) known by the name of Fusel Oil is extremely repugnant, whereas in other types of spirit it is prized. Fusel oil volatilizes less readily than alcohol, and more readily than water; but because it remains in close union with the former, it passes simultaneously into the spirit through the still.
