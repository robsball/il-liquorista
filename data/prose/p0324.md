## Testing Spirits of Wine

The most important elements for the fineness of a brandy are given by the proportion of esters and superior alcohols.

To ascertain the various proportions of such constituents in a brandy, very delicate analyses are required, which it is not appropriate to detail here: we confine ourselves only to indicating some simple tests within anyone's reach to obtain a criterion on the greater or lesser purity of spirits.

**For aldehydes:** Mix equal volumes of the alcohol to be tested and a solution of caustic potash at 20%, or heat slowly in a tube to nearly boiling. Pure alcohol does not color at all, while those rich in aldehydes color from yellow to reddish-brown according to the quantity of aldehydes present.

**For superior alcohols:** Mix in a test tube 10 c.c. of the alcohol and 10 c.c. of pure concentrated sulfuric acid, and heat with stirring to nearly boiling. If the alcohol under examination is impure, the liquid darkens. It should be noted, however, that in this reaction aldehydes give a much more intense coloration than superior alcohols.

## Cognac Properly So-Called

Cognac takes its name from the Cognac region of southern France. The best qualities are produced there.

It is a question long and vigorously discussed and debated whether the denomination cognac can be extended to all natural liquors obtained by distillation from wine, admitting a broad and generic meaning to this appellative, which would otherwise designate wine spirits in general, from whatever wine they derive.
