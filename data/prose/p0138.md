It can, however, happen that the opposite case occurs; that is, that in the distillation of essences there remain in the still—meaning they are lost—certain vegetable principles that may be quite valuable for the liqueur. This is the case, for example, with those vegetable parts which, in addition to essential oils, contain a prized bitter substance. In such a case, it would be preferable, instead of using essential oils, to employ alcoholic extraction of the vegetable parts without subsequent distillation.

It is of the utmost importance for the liqueur-maker to form a correct judgment on such cases. To reach this judgment, an exact knowledge of the aromatic and flavorful substances contained in plants, as well as of the methods for extracting them, is necessary.

As we have already pointed out earlier, in general the aroma of essential oils does not correspond to, nor can it be compared in intensity and pleasantness with, that of the plants from which they are derived.

This inferiority in the quality of the essences is, for cold-process liqueur-making, a drawback that becomes far less noticeable in hot-process manufacturing.

The latter process likewise separates the essences by distillation, but instead of water it uses alcohol, which takes possession of the essential oils even before their volatilization and releases them under gentle heat. If one further considers the fact that essential oils are subject to many adulterations—which are frequently very difficult to detect—and that even pure ones, over time, especially if exposed to light, deteriorate to the detriment of their quality (they become resinified), it must be agreed that hot-process liqueur manufacture presents an advantage over the cold process.

This advantage exists, of course, only when the essence that gives the liqueur its flavor must be obtained by distillation, rather than by extraction—that is, by maceration with alcohol without subsequent distillation.

However, a distinction must also be made among liqueurs whose flavor depends specifically on an essential oil, regarding certain types in whose preparation extracting the essence by distillation would be a mistake.
