For large-scale operations, mechanical equipment for cleaning, bottling, and capping bottles, etc., is indispensable.

16°. A machine for corking bottles, along with the corresponding cork stoppers, tinfoil capsules, etc. (1).

The workshop must also be equipped with a sufficient assortment of glass bottles of various capacities, with narrow or wide necks and with cork or ground-glass stoppers; with sturdy demijohns; with graduated containers fitted with spigots for mixing; with varnished earthenware jars for storing syrups; and with a number of casks for keeping wines and liqueurs, etc.

In this regard, it is worth remembering that, especially for colorless liqueurs, old casks are preferable, since they do not impart the coloring matter of the wood to the liqueur, which can sometimes also alter its flavor. Indeed, when new barrels must be used, it is necessary to first fill them with hot water and let it stand for a few days, repeating this treatment until the wash water comes out completely colorless.

Mixtures for small quantities of liqueurs are best made in glass containers that can then be stoppered so as to allow vigorous shaking of the blend; for large quantities, wide basins or boilers are used, or uncovered casks kept upright, stirring the liquid mixture with spatulas.

Wooden spatulas (hardwood) should also be left soaking in water for a few days before use, in order to remove any sharp taste that could be harmful to the liqueur.

The rules for the preservation of the individual raw materials will be given in the respective chapters dealing with them.
