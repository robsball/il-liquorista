# Preparing Syrup for Sweetened Liqueurs (continued)

To obtain 200 liters of alcohol at 42° the following are required:

1. 88.42 l of alcohol at 95°
2. 111.58 l of water

Total: 200 l

If sugar were a liquid, one kilogram would occupy a volume of 0.650 l (= 650 c.c.); 80 kg would therefore occupy a volume of

80 × 0.650 l = 52 l

Since 111.58 l of liquid are needed to dilute the alcohol, there remain 111.58 − 52 = 59.58 l, representing the maximum quantity of water that can be used to prepare the syrup.

A syrup made with refined sugar remains liquid if half a liter of water is used per kilogram of sugar. It is therefore sufficient to boil the 80 kg of sugar with 40 liters of water, keeping back about 19 liters of water to be mixed beforehand with the alcohol and used partly to rinse out the vessel in which the syrup was prepared.

In liqueurs that are strongly sweetened, another problem can arise which must be avoided when sweetening them: if they are kept for a long time, part of the sugar may separate out and settle at the bottom in the form of candied (crystallized) sugar.

The most effective remedy against this problem consists in adding an acid to the sugar, specifically after boiling has been completed. The acid hinders the sugar's tendency to crystallize by converting the refined sugar into invert sugar.

In punch extracts, which already contain either tartaric or citric acid, this problem does not occur; it occurs instead in extracts that lack acid.

For liqueurs, citric acid is best suited for this purpose, in the proportion of 200 g per 100 kg of sugar, or alternatively acetic acid in the proportion of 20 c.c. per 80 kg of sugar. Such a quantity of acid imparts neither a perceptible taste nor smell.
