# Liqueurs by Distillation

## On Nomenclature and Classification

We have organized each preparation by the type of raw material—whether a drug or plant—accompanied by an indication of the liqueur type obtained according to the recipe presented: for example, *Mint (Mint Cream)*, *Cacao (Cacao Rosolio)*, *Cinchona (Bitter or Elixir of Cinchona)*, and so forth. However, we have left intact the fanciful names that are generally recognized or entirely specialized: for example, *Acqua d'oro* (Gold Water), *Perfetto Amore* (Perfect Love), *Centosettanni* (Hundred Seventy Years), *Elixir Cagliostro*, *Amaro Malakoff*, *Liquore dell'Elettore* (Elector's Liqueur), *id. di Teichmayer*, *Rosolio Fiorentino* (Florentine Rosolio), *Krambambuli*, and so forth.

Regarding those denominations that indicate entirely special liqueurs (*Alchermes*, *Chartreuse*, *Vespertro*, *Trappistino*, *Usquebaugh*, etc.), it is certainly not our intent—nor have we in any way intended—to declare that we are indicating the composition of the original product. Rather, we have included in this volume certain formulas that have been proposed and recommended as more or less fanciful imitations of the typical product, the denomination of which should be considered generic, no more and no less than these: *Anisette*, *Curaçao*, *Kümmel*, and so forth.

## Liqueurs by Distillation

We shall now begin the series of artificial liqueurs prepared by distillation.

We can say very little about Spirits, since these, being unadorned with sugar, are not considered liqueurs—save for a few exceptions—but rather constitute the fundamental preparation (aromatized alcohol or spirit) with which the liqueur is composed. Indeed, as such, we have already described them in Part II.

Only *Absinthe* and occasionally *Juniper* are liqueurs not sweetened, and therefore they may be considered spirits. It must be noted, however, that the aromatized alcohols of these, which we indicated in the section *Aromatized Alcohols*, are not used in that form as liqueurs, but rather are reduced through appropriate dilution with water to an alcoholic strength inferior to the original, which may vary from 50 to 70 degrees.
