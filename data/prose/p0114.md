Essential oils are products of plant life, and their composition is therefore quite variable: they do not constitute defined chemical entities, but rather mixtures, sometimes quite complex, of defined principles.

Despite the diligent and interesting studies carried out so far on the composition of essential oils, it is not permissible to assert that their composition is known today with certainty: indeed, new facts are constantly being discovered that profoundly modify previously acquired knowledge, even for those essences that were already considered perfectly understood. Not only are analytical divergences found between products derived from plants that are designated by the same name but belong to different varieties, or from identical plants cultivated in different regions; but also between essential oils supplied by the same plants, cultivated in the same climate, whose composition shows, from one year to the next, notable divergences.

Essential oils, commonly called essences, are substances that are found already formed in, or contained within, plants, or that impart to them their odor or aroma.

This designation derives from the characteristic, generally present in these aromatic liquid substances, of leaving stains on paper that resemble those left by fatty oils; however, they differ from these, because such stains disappear after some time as the essential oil volatilizes, whereas those left by fatty or fixed oils remain unchanged.

Essential oils form in living plants through the process of vegetation; they localize already formed either in the whole plant or in specific parts. A unique exception is made by the essential oils of mustard and bitter almonds, which are not found already formed in their respective seeds, but are produced only when these, once crushed, come into contact with water.
