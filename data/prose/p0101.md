**Marjoram.** — *Origanum majorana* (Labiatae) is a perennial herb cultivated throughout almost all of Europe. It has a lively stem, woody at the base, with whitish, downy leaves. The whole plant gives off a camphor-like aromatic odor and has an aromatic taste.

**Mandarins.** — These are the fruits of *Citrus nobilis*, originally from China and now cultivated in Europe, especially in Sicily. They have a distinctive flavor and aroma. The peel contains an essential oil.

**Almonds.** — Four varieties are distinguished for use by liqueur-makers:

*Sweet almonds*, of the species *Amygdalus communis*: many varieties are counted — hard-shell, Spanish, princess, lady, Chinon, Milhaud, etc. They should be chosen full, whole, dry, sound, with a fine, pale-yellow skin. They must be white inside, easy to break, and not at all rancid; insects attack them easily, and they can be damaged by rodents. They can be kept for 2–3 years in a dry environment.

*Bitter almonds.* These differ from the sweet ones only in their intensely bitter flavor: if eaten in significant quantity they become toxic owing to the prussic acid [hydrocyanic acid] and volatile oil they contain.

*Apricot-kernel almonds*: their composition is almost identical to that of bitter almonds; they are more fragrant but less delicate in flavor than the latter; they contain prussic acid and turn rancid easily.

*Peach-kernel almonds.* These are similar to the preceding ones, but their flavor is finer and more pleasant.

Bitter-almond oil is extracted from the kernels of a variety of *Amygdalus communis*, which yield 40 to 50% oil. It is generally clear and of good flavor.

**Mastic.** — Mastic is the name given to the resin produced by a species of pistachio, *Pistacia lentiscus*. The cultivation of the lentisk and the harvesting of the resin take place in the Greek Archipelago.
