# Rum – Artificial Rum

A wine glass of rum is left for a few minutes in contact with the glass walls and then poured out. At the moment the alcohol evaporates, the aroma becomes perceptible in its entirety and more intense; the notes of cinnamon and vanilla should not be distinguished within it, and it should still be smelled in the open glass for about 24 hours.

From the time at which the aroma disappears, one can deduce the content of genuine rum.

Central America, especially the Antilles, supplies the greatest quantity of rum that enters Europe via London.

The most prized is that from Jamaica: followed by those from Cuba, Demerara, Martinique, Guadeloupe, etc. It often also bears the name of the plantations from which it comes.

As we have already noted, natural coloration is produced over time, but artificial coloration varies according to the destination market; for example, in Germany clear rum is preferred, while in England and Italy a very dark rum is preferred.

## Rum – Artificial Rum

It is extremely difficult to find genuine rum in commerce; usually it is cut with alcohol; or it is merely an imitation of that described above, which contains no trace or only a small proportion of genuine rum. It is precisely these imitations, some of which have notable qualities (such as Eafon rum), that we shall now treat extensively.

They are prepared using special extracts. The base of these extracts is Rum Ether:

### Rum Ether

It is found in commerce of fairly good quality, but can be easily obtained by mixing alcohol, wood vinegar (or crude pyroligneous acid), sulfuric acid, or by distilling in the presence of manganese dioxide and other balsamic or aromatic substances. We indicate here the proportions of some of these mixtures:

|---|---|---|---|
| Alcohol of 95° | 9 L | 11.5 L | 4 L |
| Wood vinegar | 5 L | 5 L | 2 L |
