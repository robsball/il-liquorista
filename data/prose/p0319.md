# Defects in Brandy and Their Remedies

Aeration is not advisable because it would cause considerable loss of alcohol.

When using new stills, or those that have not been used for several years, the brandy may acquire a certain coppery taste. To prevent this fault, all parts of the apparatus should be washed repeatedly with hot water, or better still, distill alcohol-enriched water at 5% for a few hours. To remedy brandy that has contracted this coppery defect, it often suffices to expose it somewhat to air and agitate it; but if the taste is very pronounced, the only effective remedy is to dilute it to about 25 degrees and distill it again. Even with perfectly cleaned stills, young brandies often have a particular taste reminiscent of metal. It is true that aging makes this defect disappear, but if one wishes to eliminate it sooner, the brandy can be vigorously aerated, or even clarified with 1 liter of fresh, unboiled milk, or with 10 grams of gelatin per hectoliter, first adding 8 grams of tannin.

The so-called "fire shocks" (*colpi di fuoco*) that occur in the distillation of grape refuse, especially over direct fire, impart a burnt taste to the brandy. Often aeration and clarification suffice to eliminate it; but if the taste is pronounced, it is advisable to filter the brandy through charred wood, or indeed to add charcoal at a dose of 150 grams per hectoliter directly to the brandy, shake vigorously, let it rest, then rack it off.

Smoke taste can be eliminated by using 400–500 grams of neutral olive oil per hectoliter of brandy.

Defects contracted from the container can be avoided by ensuring the cask is in perfect condition before storing the brandy in it. One of the most common defects is mold, which is also quite difficult to remove; one can attempt treatment with 400–500 grams of sweet wood charcoal per hectoliter, followed, after 4–5 days, by clarification using egg white or gelatin.
