# Essential Oils (continued)

**Rose.** — From roses, essential oil is obtained by distillation, especially in Bulgaria and Romania; nowadays it is used instead of the petals for preparing aromatic waters and tinctures. Several qualities of this oil are distinguished: Turkish oil, French oil, English oil, etc.: the first is the most prized; it is very dense and crystallizes at a temperature of 10-15°C; it is widely adulterated.

**Rosemary.** — The oil is extracted by distilling the plant with steam. Over time it thickens and turns brown, but when freshly prepared it is clear, colorless, and mobile. It has a burning camphor-like taste. At Grasse, in France, this oil of superb quality is produced on a large scale. Dalmatia, Sicily, Corsica, and Spain also supply it, among others.

**Sage.** — Its essential oil is obtained by distillation with water vapor: it is dense, of a greenish-yellow-brown color, with the odor and taste of sage; left in the air, after some time it congeals into a white crystalline mass. It may be found adulterated with turpentine essential oil.

**Sandalwood.** — The essential oil obtained from it (1.5 to 5%) comes for the most part from factories in Leipzig. The qualities from Calcutta, Bombay, Tellicherry, and Madagascar are less prized. This oil is somewhat syrupy, light yellow, almost brown. It is widely used in perfumery and also in medicine. It also serves to flavor certain liqueurs.

**Sassafras (Wood of).** — The essential oil is extracted from the roots, whose bark yields up to 7%. When crude it is yellowish, but when freshly rectified it is colorless; the action of air makes it yellow and reddish again.

Its odor recalls that of fennel and it has a burning taste. The commercial product may be adulterated with essential oils of turpentine, lavender, cloves, etc. Its density is higher than that of other essential oils, and even higher than that of water.

**Thyme.** — The essential oil is obtained by distilling flowering thyme with water vapor.
