In this form it is called rectified spirit or rectified alcohol, if the quality is fine: the design of the latest rectification apparatus is based on the experience that vapors of water, Fusel oil, and alcohol condense into liquids at different temperatures. The alcohol vapors rise through spaces at different temperatures, thus allowing the constituents to separate and then flow out purified.

We will indicate further on how, in practice, one can purify from Fusel oil the alcohol to be used for the manufacture of liqueurs.

Mention must be made of absolute alcohol, which is indispensable for certain uses. Strictly speaking, this would be alcohol entirely free of water, that is, at 100%.

But such a product is difficult to obtain. Absolute alcohol as found in commerce, and which serves very well for industrial use, generally contains 98% alcohol and 2% water.

Through rectification alone this grade cannot be reached. Therefore, further percentages of water are removed from alcohol at 95% by mixing it with dry calcium chloride in plates, which absorbs the water. The alcohol thus dewatered is then distilled at the lowest possible temperature.

If absolute alcohol is not kept in thoroughly dry and cool places, and sealed in bottles stoppered with a bladder cap, it absorbs moisture from the air and loses strength.

### Alcohol from Grains and Cereals

It is rarely used for liqueurs; rather, it is used as a spirit (acquavite) at a strength of 40-60%.

Grain alcohol is formed from the starch of cereals, just as potato alcohol is formed from potato starch. Generally, two types of cereals are used: wheat and rye, or wheat and barley, which are crushed, mixed with a portion of malt, kneaded, and mixed with boiling water.
