This latter portion of tincture can be added to the liquids drained off previously, or it can be kept separate in order to recover its alcohol as indicated above; and in the same way the plant residues can, in certain cases, be subjected to pressing by means of a press (see fig. 1).

As a very simple displacement apparatus, one can also use a barrel open at the top, inside which, at about a quarter of its height, a sieve is placed, or a tap is fitted immediately above the level of the bottom. The finely chopped substances to be extracted are spread over the sieve, and the liquid is poured on little by little until it is submerged. The vessel is covered and left to macerate for 24 hours. By the law of gravity, the layers of liquid that have become heavier from dissolving the soluble constituents grow denser and sink to the bottom, while the lighter layers rise toward the surface, where they in turn become denser through extraction of material and likewise sink to the bottom. This cycle repeats until the whole liquid is homogeneously saturated.

After 24 hours the liquid is drawn off and, if needed, replaced with fresh extracting liquid. In this way the botanicals are allowed to become so thoroughly exhausted that pressing becomes unnecessary.

In factories where it is necessary to extract large quantities of material at once, a so-called column apparatus is used. A series of vessels is arranged one above the other so that the drain tap of the first vessel forms the feed pipe of the second, and so on. When all the vessels are filled with botanicals, the liquid is poured into the topmost vessel; when this first one is full, the liquid is allowed to flow into the second, and so on in succession. By regulating the inflow with the outflow, the operation can proceed without interruption. It should be understood that the vessels must be kept closed if the liquid is volatile. The solvent liquid, flowing down from the first vessel charged with soluble principles and then passing through the second, third, fourth, etc., becomes so reinforced that after the last vessel it drains off exceedingly concentrated.
