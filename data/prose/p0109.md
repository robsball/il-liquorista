**Valerian.** — Wild valerian, also called common valerian or Amantilla, is Valeriana officinalis, common in shady places and along watercourses of Europe and North America. The valerian root of commerce is slightly conical, 3 to 4 cm long, 2 to 3 cm thick, brown or yellow-brown internally, whitish on the outside, and often hollow in the center; at the base it has a tuft of very long rootlets (8 to 12 cm), interlaced with one another so as to wrap around the main root. When fresh it is almost odorless, but upon drying it acquires a sharp, distinctive odor, and at the same time becomes hard and horn-like. It has a sweetish, unpleasantly aromatic taste. It is very rarely found adulterated with other roots. It contains an essential oil and other substances.

**Vanilla.** — Vanilla planifolia (Orchidaceae) is native to Mexico, where it is extensively cultivated, as well as in Java, Madagascar, etc. The fruits are dry capsules 15–24 cm long, well known as pods (siliques).

The most prized vanilla is that from Mexico, and it is found in commerce under various brands according to the length of the fruits, bundled in bunches of 50. Also found in commerce are the fruits of wild Mexican vanilla, which are shorter and less aromatic. There are related species not to be confused with the true vanilla: V. Pompona, V. Guayra [Guyana vanilla?], Vanillón, V. Bahiae [Bahia vanilla, similar in odor to tonka bean], the vanilla of Guiana, the vanilla of the Palm, etc. Bourbon vanilla (from the island) is true vanilla, but of inferior quality. The aromatic principle of vanilla is vanillin: it has been possible to prepare it chemically in a perfectly identical form.

On Réunion Island a new process is currently used to facilitate the drying of vanilla without diminishing its aromatic properties. The pods are placed in a hermetically sealed container with calcium chloride, which absorbs all the moisture.

The liquefied calcium chloride is regenerated by calcination. It is evident that vanilla dried in this way loses much less vanillin than when it is exposed for weeks to open air, according to the ordinary drying practice.
