# Alcohol from Potatoes

...some brief notes on some of the various processes for producing ethyl alcohol and on the various qualities found in commerce, which, once carefully purified and rectified, are used in the manufacture of liqueurs.

### Alcohol from Potatoes

The considerable quantity of starch contained in potatoes makes them a special raw material usable for the production of alcohol.

Starch is not, in its natural state, a fermentable substance: however, it represents the material from which the living plant, through the process of vegetation, produces sugar, or which can also be converted into sugar by artificial means (dilute mineral acids). In seeds this conversion occurs through the process of germination.

If, therefore, one wishes to convert the starch of cereals into sugar, malt is first produced from them.

In the living seed the conversion is accomplished by the action of certain nitrogenous (proteinic) substances. The potato does not possess these substances; consequently, the formation of sugar cannot take place by natural means. However, the addition of about 5% cereal malt to the mash of cooked potatoes brings about, in a short time, the formation of sugar, so that the paste formed after cooling can immediately enter, by means of yeast, into vigorous fermentation; once this is complete, the alcohol is separated by distillation.

Besides alcohol, another ethereal substance is also produced which gives the aroma. In this case, the aroma (!) known under the name of Fusel [oil] is extremely disagreeable, whereas in other kinds of spirit it is prized. Fusel oil volatilizes more slowly than alcohol, and even more slowly than water (1); but since it exists in close union with the former, it passes together with the spirit through the still.
