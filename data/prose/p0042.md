If the vat is sufficiently large, it can remain in operation for several months in succession. As soon as the charcoal's action is exhausted, the alcohol is drawn off, the vat is washed with water, and refilled with fresh charcoal. The old charcoal can be reused afterward if one has the equipment needed to reheat (reactivate) it to a red heat.

As already noted, charcoal purification does not yield alcohol as pure as that supplied by refineries. Only the first portions that pass through the charcoal are free of fusel oil. For the finest liqueurs, it is therefore necessary to obtain alcohol from refineries.

If the alcohol has no flavor at all, this is preferable, especially for very delicate liqueurs; when it has a good flavor but one that is too pronounced, it can have an unpleasant influence on the aroma of the liqueur for which it forms the base.

The use of brandy (eau-de-vie) in place of pure alcohol is inadvisable, for the reason already given — its overly pronounced flavor — and also because of its too-low proof.

The small quantity of alcohol that brandy contains is, moreover, already saturated with essential oils and therefore incapable of dissolving others; its effectiveness is thus greatly diminished, and the result of treating aromatic substances with it turns out quite poor.

## Alcoholometry

Commercial alcohol generally has a strength of 90–96%.

The determination of the alcoholic percentage in a liquid — or, more properly, in a mixture of alcohol and water, i.e., diluted alcohol — constitutes Alcoholometry, and is carried out by means of alcoholometers (as well as by other physical and chemical methods, which there is no need to mention here), which express the density or specific gravity of the liquid under examination, from which the percentage is calculated.(1)
