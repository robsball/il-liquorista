# Melissa, Spearmint, Peppermint

**Melissa (Lemon Balm).** — An essential oil is extracted from it, fluid, almost colorless, with a very pleasant odor and a non-sharp aromatic taste. It is often adulterated with lemon oil and similar substances.

**Spearmint (Menta crispa).** — The essential oil is little used in liqueur-making. When freshly rectified it is colorless, but on exposure to light it easily takes on a yellowish tint. It dissolves in equal parts of alcohol.

**Peppermint (Menta piperita).** — The essential oil is contained in all the herbaceous parts of the peppermint plant.

The essential oil of peppermint is greenish, but it decolorizes easily when filtered through animal charcoal (bone black) and rectified. However, over time this decolorized oil turns brown and thickens. It has a very strong minty odor and a pungent, aromatic taste that leaves a characteristic sensation of freshness in the mouth. Very old oil, as well as Japanese oil, has a somewhat bitter taste. It is soluble in alcohol of 95°. This oil can be adulterated with turpentine oil, eucalyptus oil, camphor oil, copaiba balsam diluted with alcohol, or mixed with mustard or ginger essence to give it a more pronounced pungent flavor; however, the most frequent adulterations are those made with common mint oil.

The essential oils of the other mint species are less used and are rarely found on the market under their true name, being instead sold under the name of peppermint. Japanese oil is not suitable for liqueur-making uses because of its bitter taste.

The best qualities come from England and America. In the southern regions of France, too, a very fine essence is obtained, but it is less penetrating and less flavorful, and consequently of minor use. Essences of German peppermint are not usable for liqueurs. The finest English essence, used exclusively for fine liqueurs, is called Mitcham peppermint essence. Among the American qualities, the most renowned are the Fritsche, Schimmel, and Hotpkins brands. By blending these qualities together, intermediate grades can be obtained.

Peppermint essence dissolves in equal parts of alcohol at 96°. It must be kept away from light, as it easily turns yellow.
