## Description of Vegetable Substances and Drugs (1)

**Abelmosco.** — Under the name Abelmosco or Ambretta are indicated the seeds of *Hibiscus abelmoschus* (Malvaceae), which grows in India, Egypt, and the Antilles; these small, brownish, irregular seeds have a scent recalling that of ambergris and musk, which develops especially when heated or rubbed between the fingers. The best come from Martinique.

**Achillea.** — *Achillea moschata*, called Iva, is native to the Alps, where it grows almost at the limit of perpetual snow; it is barely 10 cm tall and bears white flowers. It contains a great deal of achilleine, and a greenish essential oil (essential oil of Iva) with an aromatic scent similar to that of mint. It is highly prized in Switzerland and is used in the preparation of Iva-Bitter.

In our region the common species is *Achillea millefolium*, or yarrow, found in damp meadows, ditches, etc. It is about 60 cm tall, with white and red flowers. It has an aromatic odor and a bitter, astringent taste.

**Agarico bianco (White Agaric).** — *Boletus laricis* is a fungus common on larch trees in the forests of Carinthia, Circassia, the Dauphiné, etc. It forms oblong masses, as big as a fist and shaped like a club.

Its outer skin is yellowish and hard, woody, while the inner part is white, light, spongy, and tubular. It has very little odor. The taste, at first slightly sweet, quickly becomes very bitter. This fungus is found on the market stripped of its bark. It is a powerful purgative; its action is due to agaricin, or agaric acid.
