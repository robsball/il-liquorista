Another fairly simple test that can be used to evaluate the impurities contained in alcohol was proposed by Bardet, and consists of adding, in a white glass bottle, to 50 cubic centimeters of the alcohol, 2 cubic centimeters of a 0.2% solution of potassium permanganate (violet in color); the mixture is then shaken and the time noted at which decolorization of the mixture occurs: if the alcohol is pure, 45 minutes are required; for impure spirits, decolorization occurs much more rapidly, the more so the greater the fusel oil content.

One cannot of course claim that Bardet's method allows one to determine the exact quantity of impurities in an alcohol; nevertheless, this very simple test with potassium permanganate can be of good service in special cases, and can serve, for example, in industry to check the work of distilleries.

To detect higher alcohols in ethyl alcohol, the reaction proposed by Komarowsky is also clearly useful and very convenient: to 10 c.c. of the alcohol under examination, 1 c.c. of a 1‰ solution of salicylic aldehyde and 3 c.c. of concentrated sulfuric acid are added; the mixture is shaken vigorously and heated: in the presence of higher alcohols the liquid will take on a garnet-red coloration, which tends to intensify more and more.

Traube has also proposed two instruments that are quite easy to use, the Stalagmometer and the Capillarometer, which there is no need to describe here.

We omit reporting the various other tests adopted for the detection and evaluation of alcohol, since these require the equipment and technical skill of a chemist (1).
