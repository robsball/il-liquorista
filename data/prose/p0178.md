**2.** Another apparatus of this type, simpler and very convenient to use, known by the name of *Omnium Extractor*, is shown in Fig. 12. Above a small furnace stands the boiler, fitted with a water-bath or not, depending on the liquid to be used for the extraction. This liquid, upon boiling, evaporates and travels up the right-hand side tube, entering a coil in the upper vessel, which is continuously cooled, and drips down condensed into the intermediate vessel, which holds the material to be exhausted. The liquid, now charged with the soluble principles, falls back into the boiler, from where it evaporates once more, leaving behind the fixed principles, and repeats the extraction, following the cycle described above.

In this way, with only a small amount of liquid, one can obtain almost a complete extract of the material; indeed, these devices are excellent precisely for the manufacture of extracts. They can be built at little expense, but in that case it would be advisable to make one addition: namely, to fit a small connecting tube, equipped with a stopcock, between the lower part of the right-hand tube and the boiler, so as to allow the liquid that condenses along the way to fall back into the boiler, without having to pass through the tube carrying the vapor. The usefulness of this addition is quite clear. As can be seen, this apparatus is nothing more than a kind of Payen-type digester, built on a larger scale, in metal.

This process has the advantage of greatly enhancing the dissolving action of the alcohol, which can therefore reach the desired degree of aromatic richness in a shorter time, achieving a more complete exhaustion of the raw materials.
