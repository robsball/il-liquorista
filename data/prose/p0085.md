The former comes from Socotra (it is the most prized), from the Cape of Good Hope (Cape Aloe), and from India. The other comes from the Antilles (Barbados Aloe) and also from India.

Aloe has an extremely bitter, persistent taste and a somewhat unpleasant odor. It is almost completely soluble in hot water and in diluted alcohol; it is, however, almost insoluble in strong alcohol.

**Pineapple.** — *Ananassa vulgaris* is cultivated in all warm regions of the globe. The fruits contain about 10% sugary substances, mostly sucrose. They come to market fresh or preserved in their own juice or in syrup. Their aroma is very well imitated with chemical products; the so-called pineapple essence is an alcoholic solution of ethyl butyrate (see Artificial Fruit Essences).

**Dill.** — The seeds of this annual plant, which bears a strong resemblance in odor to fennel, are sometimes used in liqueur-making: they are elongated, oval, and somewhat flattened, with a strong, pungent, aromatic, and sharp odor and taste.

**Angelica.** — *Angelica archangelica* (Umbelliferae) grows on high mountains (Alps, Pyrenees, etc.), and is also cultivated in gardens.

The root is used, which is conical, blackish on the outside and white inside. It has a strong, aromatic odor, and a taste that is at first aromatic, then sharp and unpleasant. The seeds are also used; they are ovoid, elongated, whitish, and quite light.

Both these and the root, as well as the plant itself, are considered effective stomachic and cordial agents.

**Angostura.** — Angostura bark is obtained from a large tree that grows on the banks of the Orinoco (*Angustura vera*). It is found in commerce in flat or rolled pieces, with the ends cut obliquely. On the outside it is spongy and grayish-yellow; on the inside it is brown, smooth, and scattered with small crystals of calcium oxalate. It has a pleasant aromatic odor, though this is quickly lost; its taste is bitter.
