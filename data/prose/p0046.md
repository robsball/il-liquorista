...when 40 liters of water are mixed with 60 liters of anhydrous alcohol, one does not obtain 100 liters of mixture, but only 93.33 liters. To obtain 100 liters, 43.67 liters of water would be needed. In this latter case the spirit contains 60%, while in the first case it contains 62.69%.

Tralles took this particular contraction into account when establishing his scale, so that his gradation indicates the precise percentage.

His scale, however, is not exact in all cases, but only in certain isolated ones, especially when the spirit being measured is at the temperature that we consider normal, and at which Tralles himself compiled his scale, namely 15.5° C.

All bodies, whether liquid or solid, increase in volume according to the temperature to which they are heated, and decrease on cooling.

An iron bar becomes longer or thicker when heated; a glass filled to the brim with cold water overflows if it is warmed. This expansion corresponds to a decrease in specific gravity, while an increase in specific gravity corresponds to contraction.

The specific gravity of substances is therefore constant only at a fixed, determined temperature; it decreases as heat increases and rises as cold increases (1).

Solid bodies expand with heat less than liquids do, and heavy liquids expand less than light liquids. Spirit therefore changes its volume more than water does with temperature oscillations, and this change is all the more noticeable the higher the percentage of alcohol contained in the liquid. All these variations cannot be recorded by old-style alcoholometers. In newer ones, however, a thermometer enclosed within the alcoholometer itself indicates the temperature of the spirit in degrees centigrade, and the percentage scale shows the alcohol percentage at that temperature.
