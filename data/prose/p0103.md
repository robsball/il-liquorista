# Materia Medica / Botanical Ingredients (continued): Mints, Myrobalans, Myrrh, Nutmeg

**Curly Mint** (also called crisp mint), water mint, water mentastro, cultivated especially in Germany, where it is called *Kräuseminze*.

**Pennyroyal Mint (Mentuccia).** — This species of mint is found abundantly in uncultivated places throughout Europe. It is a plant barely 15 cm tall, with small oval leaves, blunt at the tip, toothed, with oil glands on both faces. Its flowers, scattered along the stem, are white or pink. The whole plant is more or less downy and gives off a strong odor similar to that of mint, but less pleasant.

**Myrobalans.** — Fruits of certain Indian trees belonging to the genus *Terminalia chebula*, or *Myrobalanus chebula*: they are elongated, olive-shaped, about the size of a date, shiny, brownish; the flesh is not very firm, brownish, crunchy, and slightly acidic. They are found in commerce dried.

They contain a large amount of tannin (30 to 50%); they are therefore astringent.

**Myrrh.** — This gum-resin is obtained from the bark of *Balsamodendron Ehrenbergianum* and related species that grow along the shores of the Red Sea, in Somalia, in Arabia, etc. The main market is Bombay. Myrrh in "tears" is the best kind: it consists of heavy, brittle grains, yellowish-brown, semi-transparent, with a shiny fracture; it has an aromatic odor and a bitter taste. The variety known as "in sorte" is much less pure, in large, yellowish tears, with less pronounced taste and odor.

Myrrh has a characteristic, pleasant aromatic odor and a bitter taste. It is soluble in water (40–65%); the part that dissolves is the gum, while the resin is insoluble. Myrrh contains about 25% of a colorless essential oil, which yellows over time and becomes viscous; its taste is at first barely perceptible, then becomes burning like that of camphor. Myrrh is now little used.
