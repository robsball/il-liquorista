# General Principles of Liqueur Composition

In general, we should note that the proportions of alcohol or sugar to be used for the different types of liqueurs are constant, while those for the aroma (alcoholate, distilled water, or tincture) and for water are variable. Specifically, for one hectolitre of liqueur, the following should be adopted:

| | Ordinary | Double | Semi-fine | Fine | Superior |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Alcohol of 85° | lit. 25 | 50 | 28 | 30–32 | 32–40 |
| Sugar | kg. 12.50 | 25 | 25 | 37–45 | 50–60 |

In the preparation of ordinary liqueurs, alcoholates or aromatized spirits may also be substituted with their respective distilled waters or vice versa; in such cases, however, account must be taken of the differences in alcohol employed, whether in excess or deficit, so that the finished liqueurs always contain 25% alcohol of 85°.

On this subject we should observe that in ancient times, when liqueur manufacturers worked with aromatic waters, they commonly prepared liqueurs by dissolving sugar in the aromatic water or by subsequently adding the necessary quantity of alcohol to it.

This procedure, however, is little to be recommended, because a liqueur so prepared must always be filtered, and the filtration of a liqueur, especially if it contains much sugar, is very slow.

On the other hand, the procedure by means of aromatic waters—which contain alcohol at the same time (since the distillation of aromatic substances was carried out not with water, but with weak alcohol)—has the advantage that liqueurs thus obtained do not require long storage, which is instead always necessary for those prepared by means of essential oils, because the taste of alcohol disappears only after some time. For this reason some manufacturers still follow the ancient procedure.

We have therefore preferred to group the recipes according to the different manufacturing procedures: that is, in the first chapter we include liqueurs obtained directly by distillation or natural ones—such as Cognac, Rum, Arrack, Kirschwasser, etc.—as well as their mixtures and imitations; subsequently we have devoted a chapter to the preparation of artificial liqueurs.
