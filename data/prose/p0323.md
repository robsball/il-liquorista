## Brandy from Grape Pomace or Grappa

Brandy from grape pomace and brandy from wine lees belong to the category of wine alcohols.

These are manufactured from the residues of winemaking. Grape pomace consists of the skins and pressed berries from the crushing of white grapes.

Lees is the name given to the sediment of wine after fermentation; it consists largely of fermentation cells. The alcohol distilled from it would rarely find buyers beyond the region of production.

In red wines, the skins, which contain the coloring matter, or the berries, containing tannin, remain in the fermenting must. The pomace is then fermented with water or sugar, and subsequently distilled. Generally these brandies are produced during the winter season after the winemaking work is complete. Until then, the pomace is preserved under a light layer of spirit. The aroma bears some resemblance to cognac, though without possessing its finesse.

If greater care were applied in their production, brandies from grape pomace could very well be used for cutting cognac.

Brandy from grape pomace or grappa is the product of the exclusive distillation of fresh grape pomace with or without water.

For this purpose, pomace is used after being subjected to pressing, keeping it exposed to air for the shortest time possible in order to avoid both loss of the contained alcohol and the risk of acetic fermentation.

The alambic still is filled with deseeded pomace, water is poured over it to prevent burning on the bottom of the still—which would produce an unpleasant empyreumatic odor—and distillation is carried out. This can be more effectively performed in an alambic heated by a bain-marie or steam, but it proceeds more slowly.
