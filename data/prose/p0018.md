# Classification of Liqueurs

Aromatized wines (which in this Manual we shall only touch on briefly) are prepared either simply by maceration, or by maceration combined with a blend of alcoholic tinctures, extracts, and residues from the manufacture of certain pharmaceutical products.

Liqueurs may also be classified, as already mentioned, according to special types, from the standpoint of the percentage of their main components. Specifically, we distinguish:

1) **Simple spirits (Acqueviti semplici).** — These are beverages or mixtures of water and alcohol, in various proportions, generally obtained by distillation of fermented beverages or by mixing alcohol with water: besides aromatic principles, they contain a rather high percentage of alcohol (40–72%) and a very small amount of sugar (2%); indeed, some spirits contain no sugar at all (Cognac, Kirschwasser, Gin, Whisky, etc.). We refer the reader to what was said above (page 3) and further on, in the special section on Natural Liqueurs.

2) **Double spirits (Acqueviti doppie).** — These are beverages which, in addition to aromatic principles or a high alcohol percentage, contain a sugar percentage of 2–12%.

3) **Liqueurs proper (Liquori propriamente detti).** — Contain 12–30% sugar and 25–50% alcohol.

4) **Creams, rosolios, and elixirs (Creme, rosoli ed elixir).** — These are very fine liqueurs, containing over 30% sugar, with a not very high alcohol percentage (20–40%).

The most commonly used liqueurs are the *rosolios*, which (the French *Crèmes*) are liqueurs in which sugar is present in a high dose, making them dense; being made with select spices, they possess a particular smoothness of flavor and aroma, for which they are rightly considered among the finest liqueurs.

5) **Ratafias or fruit liqueurs (Ratafià o liquori di frutta).** — These are prepared directly from fruit and are strongly sweetened, so as to be quite delicate and pleasant.
