# Introduction

## On Liqueurs in General

### I. — Definition, Classification, Composition and Characteristics of Liqueurs

The term "Liqueur" encompasses a varied range of liquid mixtures (indeed, in some official foreign Pharmacopoeias this designation is even applied to several medicinal preparations), but in domestic economy it is used in a more restricted sense, applied only to those beverages having alcohol as their base.

The First International Congress for the Suppression of Fraud in Foodstuffs and Pharmaceutical Products, held in Geneva in September 1908, deemed it necessary first to establish appropriate definitions for the various food substances, as a fundamental standard for recognizing their characteristics. Among these definitions, which may be considered official and international, we report the one adopted for liqueurs: "Liqueurs are flavored spirits (eaux-de-vie), obtained either by maceration of vegetable substances, or by distillation in their presence, or by addition of the product of their distillation to alcohol or water, or finally by the combined use of these various processes, sweetened or not by means of sucrose, glucose, grape sugar, or honey, and colored or not by means of harmless substances."

According to C. Girard, liqueurs are simply to be considered as beverages obtained by distillation of fermented sugary juices, and those consisting of alcohol combined with substances that generally possess aromatic properties.
