The presence of empyreumatic oils is revealed by a pale pink tint that appears in the liquid after this rest period.

A criterion that is reliable, though only for experienced persons, is provided by smell: by pouring a few drops of the alcohol onto the palm of the hand and rubbing vigorously, one can detect the odor of fusel oil, which is persistent and characteristic. This odor becomes even more noticeable if a mixture of 3 parts water to 1 part alcohol is made and shaken vigorously.

In addition, one can soak a strip of filter paper in the alcohol being examined and let it evaporate: pure wine spirit is recognized by an alcoholic, wine-like odor, whereas that derived from grain leaves behind a disagreeable smell.

To more easily recognize amyl alcohol, which is the most significant compound among the tail impurities, it has been proposed to extract it using various solvents from mixtures with ethyl alcohol. A few cubic centimeters of the alcohol under examination are poured into a test tube along with an equal volume of sulfuric ether; double the volume of distilled water is added, and the tube is shaken vigorously but carefully, keeping the opening closed with the thumb. The mixture is then left to stand for a few minutes until the liquid has separated into two distinct layers. The upper, ethereal layer is then decanted by slightly tilting the test tube into a small porcelain dish, where it is left to evaporate either in air or in a warm place. After a few minutes the ether will have evaporated, leaving a milky residue with an unpleasant and irritating odor if amyl alcohol is present.

Otto shakes the spirit with an equal volume of ether, then adds a volume of distilled water equal to the ether-alcohol mixture, causing the ether — now carrying the impurities from the alcohol — to separate out. He decants this ether, lets it evaporate at low temperature, and adds sodium acetate and sulfuric acid to the residue. Upon gentle heating, if amyl alcohol is present in the residue, the characteristic odor of amyl ether develops (a scent resembling pear essence).
