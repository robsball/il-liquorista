# Use of the Alcoholometer and Conversion Between Volume and Weight Percentage

One then reads the degree corresponding to the point where the instrument emerges at the flat surface of the liquid (not the top of the curved meniscus that clings to the stem of the alcoholometer), and immediately notes the temperature shown on the attached thermometer. If this is 15.5°C, the degree read corresponds directly to the true alcoholic strength; if it is different, one consults the tables to find the real degree corresponding to the apparent degree shown by the alcoholometer.

If the emergence point of the alcoholometer does not coincide exactly with one of the marked divisions, one must estimate by eye what fraction represents the difference between the emergence point and the line immediately below it.

Another very important criterion for evaluating alcohols is that of percentage by volume versus percentage by weight; it is worth remembering that, because the density of alcohol is lower than that of water, a given volume of alcohol does not correspond to an equal weight — without even accounting for the phenomenon of volume contraction already mentioned (1).

Generally, as already noted, alcoholometers indicate percentage by volume; from this one can calculate the percentage by weight, or vice versa, using the following formulas:

(1)  P% = (V% × 0.7938) / s

(2)  V% = (P% × s) / 0.7938

where P = percentage by weight at 15°C; V = percentage by volume, read or corrected to 15°C; s = density of the alcoholic liquid at a temperature of 15°C; 0.7938 = specific gravity of absolute anhydrous alcohol at 15°C.

(1) For more extensive information on alcoholometry, we recommend the manuals in this Collection: *L'Alcool* by F. Cantalamessa; *La distillazione del vino* by M. Da Ponte, 3rd edition, already cited.
