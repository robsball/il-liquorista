# General Principles for the Distillation of Botanical Materials

Botanical materials intended for distillation should be preferred in their fresh state rather than dried, with certain exceptions. They must be preliminarily cut into small pieces, bruised, or scraped as circumstances require. Distillation should be preceded by maceration for some time, especially for woody substances. In the alembic, the quantity of water must be such as to keep the plant material completely submerged until the operation is finished.

One must carefully observe that the liquid contained in the cucurbit of the alembic—which constitutes a decoction—does not become entrained in the distilled product.

The water should be heated rapidly to boiling, and once boiling has begun, the distillation must not be interrupted.

It is advisable to collect the distilled waters in the so-called Florentine receiver in order to easily separate those portions of essential oil that become entrained during distillation.

After adequate rest, filtration of the distilled waters is necessary to remove traces of essential oil held in suspension, whose presence would render them turbid or milk-like, and moreover acrid and unpleasant.

It is generally recommended, and especially for aromatic waters distilled from flowers, to use table salt in order to intensify the action of the water on the plant material and to prevent any fermentation during the preliminary maceration of the botanical matter. Salt also serves to raise the water temperature, thereby facilitating the passage of a greater proportion of volatile oil.

It is advisable always to proceed with distillation of limited quantities of final product.
