In preparing alcoholic extractions of plant materials, one must therefore avoid using high-percentage alcohol and use only alcohol with a strength no higher than 60%.

### Maceration

Maceration consists of leaving the solid aromatic substances in contact, for a certain period of time that varies according to the materials being treated, with a liquid that at ordinary temperature is able to draw out or extract their soluble principles, cold.

This method is also followed in large factories, since—without requiring complicated and costly equipment—it gives satisfactory results.

Any container is suitable for maceration (and digestion), provided it can be closed well, so as to avoid the loss of alcohol or essential oil, and provided it is made of a material that does not impart any special flavors to the product.

It is necessary that the substances placed to macerate be well minced, so that the operation is completed in the shortest possible time and results in the complete exhaustion of the substances themselves. Depending on the nature of these substances, they are cut into small pieces (orange peel, lemon peel and the like, tender roots), or pounded in a mortar (cinnamon, hard roots, nutmeg, etc.), or crushed (seeds); woods are rasped.

Orange peel and the like must always be cleaned of the white pith, which, besides giving no aroma at all, is harmful because it causes cloudiness.

A very important precaution to observe is to keep the substances completely submerged in the alcohol, because any parts left exposed would spoil easily, compromising the whole product.
