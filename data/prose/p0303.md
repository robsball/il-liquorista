# Dense Colors in Liqueurs

## Dense Colors in Liqueurs

The manufacture of these colors requires considerable attention in order to obtain beautiful, pure tints.

One must principally avoid contact with any metal, as otherwise uncertain colors might result.

All chemical products and preparations to be used must be completely free of iron, as must their solutions.

To prepare them, the coloring materials are boiled with two or three times the quantities of water indicated, within tinned vessels. The decoctions are passed through fine horsehair sieves and left to settle for 24 hours.

After this time, the clear colored liquid is removed, or it is treated by boiling with alum, then treated in the manner indicated below.

The majority of coloring materials used for this purpose come from plants; only a few from animals.

Above all, one must proceed cautiously in the treatment with alum, because the common product found in commerce is impure due to ferric oxide, which mixes with the coloring material and damages the color intensity.

The impurity of iron is so harmful that one cannot obtain definite colors with alum containing iron. One must therefore use only chemically pure alum, completely free of iron.

Another important rule is to use only distilled or rain water, both for boiling the colors and for preparing their solutions and for softening the day before in water the coloring extracts.

All dense colors become unusable if exposed for a long time in a hot location or if, due to poor closure of the vessel, they dry out.

They must, however, be stored in a cool place and in well-sealed vessels.
