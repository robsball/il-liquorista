3. Fruits with cotton-like epicarpia should be stripped of the fine hair covering them by means of a cloth.

4. Fruits with thick epicarpia, such as lemons, oranges, citrons, pomegranates, etc., should be peeled. The skin should not be removed from apples, pears, quinces, etc., nor from fruits having aromatic and coloring principles, such as strawberries, currants, etc., since the juice would lose a large part of its qualities.

5. The following should be degrained: grapes, elderberries, etc., since their branched stems would absorb too much juice and later communicate a different flavor.

6. Finally, they must be divided, which is done according to the texture of the substance and according to the different parts of the plant, in various ways:

a) Berries, strawberries, etc. are reduced to pulp, with the aim of opening their small vesicles or bringing the aromatic and colored pellicle into contact with the juice.

b) Cherries, currants, sour cherries, verjuice, etc. are slightly crushed in a mortar; these, being for the most part acrid, would communicate different properties to the juices.

c) Fleshy and internally non-fibrous roots are rasped entirely, etc.; and only up to the seed capsules, fruits with firm and succulent sarcocarp such as pears, apples, quinces, etc. Rasping must be done promptly so as not to expose the pulp to contact with air, which will darken it at once and render the juice more colored.

d) Woody roots and small roots, barks, leaves, flowers, and whole plants are bruised.

The plants and their parts subjected to the preliminary operations described above require further treatment by means of other operations, namely: maceration, cooking, fermentation, and pressing.
