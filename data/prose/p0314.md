# Liquors by Distillation

## Organization of the Manual

We have organized the liquor recipes by method: one section for distillation; another for maceration and digestion; another for simple mixing of essences; one for preparations via extracts or special preparation of these; we have also assigned a chapter to the preparation of fruit Ratafias; in another we have grouped Bitters, and in another Punches and Grogs; and finally we will follow with a chapter on Aromatized Wines, then Fruit Wines, etc.

It is understood that when in the recipes presented any Extract or other preparation already discussed in preceding chapters is indicated, the relative composition should be recalled precisely by searching for it through the detailed alphabetical index found at the end of the volume.

## Liquors by Distillation

As we have already noted (pp. 3, 7 and 18), all sugary substances yield more or less spirituous liquors by distillation; the eau-de-vie obtained from them retains more or less the flavor and aroma of the substance that produced it.

Substances containing abundant natural juice yield more delicate eau-de-vie, while those that are harsh and astringent impart similar flavors to it.

Likewise, we recall that the term eau-de-vie is generic, since it denotes the alcoholic product resulting from diluting ordinary alcohol with water, in order to reduce it to the degree or strength at which it is usually consumed, whereas the individual alcoholic liquids obtained from various sugary liquids are designated by distinct special names, according to the substances from which they originate.

In Part I (pp. 8, 7 and 9) we have identified those liquors that can be obtained as such from distillation with or without rectification, and that can be left for consumption without further processing.

These are the alcohols from sugar cane (Rum), from rice (Arrack), from wine (Cognac), from cereals (Grain Eau-de-vie) and from fruits (Kirsch, Slivovitz, Gin, etc.).
