## Regarding the Rules for Calculating Sugar Content

We have already discussed the determination of sugar quantity to be added by directly mixing the solution into the blend that will form the liqueur in the chapter "Sweetening of Liqueurs."

On this subject we think it opportune to note that in the composition of liqueurs for which the proportions of sugar and water are indicated for preparing the syrup with which to sweeten them, it is not good practice to substitute for the stated weight of sugar the corresponding quantity of already-prepared simple syrup and then dilute it with the quantity of water that represents the difference between what is indicated and what is already contained in the syrup. For example, should one need to compose a liqueur by mixing aromatic spirit 400, white sugar 350 dissolved in water 300, one should not substitute simple syrup 550 (whose composition is 175 sugar and 100 water, that is, 350 + 200) and dilute it with water 100 to complete the prescribed quantity of water, in addition to that already contained in the syrup; rather, one must prepare the sugar solution in water according to the stated proportions and then mix this with the flavored spirit.

This will avoid unpleasant surprises that can entirely alter a liqueur, or potential errors arising from syrups of uncertain strength due to cooking.

By mixing in determined proportions and varying according to the different types of liqueurs—syrup, water, and alcohol—one obtains what is called the Excipient for Liqueurs, or Simple Liqueur, a very practical vehicle especially for the manufacture of cold-process liqueurs by means of essences, extracts, and so forth, of which we shall treat in the chapter dedicated to this.
