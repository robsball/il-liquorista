# Measuring Small Quantities of Liquid by Drops

In some cases—especially when measuring liquids used in very small quantities, such as essential oils, essences, and aromatic extracts—instead of resorting to graduated vessels, which then require prolonged and careful washing (with alcohol) to completely remove the intense aroma, one can estimate the weight from the number of drops, using a dropper of standard bore. Since the density of essential oils varies within fairly narrow limits, it can generally be reckoned that on average 20–25 drops of an essential oil correspond to one gram.

The size of the drops is influenced by temperature, by the bore of the dropper, by its rim, by the density and cohesion of the liquid, etc.; therefore the figures given on this subject are not entirely consistent or absolute. According to the most recent findings, assuming that one gram of water corresponds to 16 drops, it can be calculated that to obtain 1 gram the following number of drops is required:

| Liquid | Drops per gram |
|---|---|
| Alcohol | 30 |
| Tinctures | 25 |
| Wines | 20 |
| Syrups | 12 |
| Ether | 50 |
| Aromatic waters | 25 |
| Essential oils | 20–25 |

As for measuring the capacity of vessels, we shall limit ourselves to indicating practical methods for establishing the content (volume) of the vessels most commonly used in liquor-makers' workshops:

1°. The volume of a hemispherical boiler is equal to two-thirds of the cube of its radius, multiplied by 3.1415 (the ratio of the circumference to the diameter).

Example. — Suppose the upper diameter is 2.50 m; the radius is then 1.25 m. The cube of the radius is the product 1.25 × 1.25 × 1.25 = 1.950.
