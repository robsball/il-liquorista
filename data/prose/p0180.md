In both cases, whether for digestion (maceration) or for distillation, one must use an alcohol of 90°.

In distillation, the drugs are treated with the necessary amount of alcohol, sufficient to exhaust the drug; they are left to macerate warm so that the spirit dissolves the greater part of the essential oils, and only afterward is distillation begun. Distillation is stopped when the spirit no longer passes over clear and the cloudiness in the distillate does not disappear.

If the drugs are not sufficiently exhausted, another distillation is carried out with a fresh quantity of alcohol.

Essences obtained directly from the drug are the most recommendable for fine liqueurs.

However, some essential oils should be noted which must also be used for the finest liqueurs, because it is not possible to produce them in the form of a spirituous essence due to the lack of fresh material — namely the essential oils of citron, of cognac, of bitter almond, of mint, of sweet orange, and of rose.

## Use — Dilution — Mixing of Essential Oils and Tinctures

The principal task of the liqueur maker consists in obtaining a perfect mixture — or at least the most nearly perfect mixture possible — of the various liquids or solutions that make up the liqueurs.

Essences are perfectly dissolved only if, together with the base of the liqueur, they produce a clear liquid. If a more or less milky cloudiness appears, the solution is not perfect; in such a case some particles of essential oil have separated out, and these produce the cloudiness. To avoid this problem one must understand from what cause the cloudiness may arise.

**1st.** Cloudiness certainly forms if the liqueur is overloaded with essential oil.

As for the quantity of essential oil to be used, experience is the guide: a given quantity of alcohol can dissolve only a given quantity of essential oil.
