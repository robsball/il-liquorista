# Purification of Alcohol

...reads 95°-96° on the Gay-Lussac centesimal alcoholometer: this, whether it comes from wine or has some other origin, constitutes a fairly pure product, and is completely free from empyreumatic oils and corresponds perfectly to all the requirements for the manufacture of liqueurs.

Simply rectified alcohol contains 94-95% pure alcohol and constitutes a less refined product, but one sufficient for mediocre liqueurs (1).

**Purification of Alcohol.** — Since spirit refineries now work with column stills, which not only bring the alcohol to the highest percentage but at the same time free it from fusel oil, liqueur manufacturers have given up buying raw alcohol in order to rectify it themselves.

However, since certain manufacturers, at least for ordinary spirits, still use raw spirit, it is worth mentioning the old method for removing fusel oil from it.

One begins by leaving the alcohol in contact with very fine vegetable charcoal and then filtering it.

Charcoal — made from wood, recently burned — is the most effective means of ridding alcohol of fusel oil. It absorbs the fusel oil without yielding any of its own constituents to the alcohol, as instead happens with the chemical products sometimes recommended for this purpose. It matters little what kind of wood the charcoal comes from; it need only be completely carbonized and contain no resinous substances. It is used in coarse powder form. Bone charcoal also has similar properties, though not as intense as wood charcoal; it is, however, an excellent means of decolorizing liquids. The two kinds are indicated when both purposes are to be achieved.

(1) To obtain 90° alcohol (rectified) from commercial alcohol, the Official Pharmacopoeia of the Kingdom prescribes distilling commercial 94°-96° alcohol, to which about 3 grams of tartaric acid per liter have been added. The resulting product is then diluted with water until it reads 90° on the Gay-Lussac alcoholometer: at which point it will have a density of 0.8340 at a temperature of 15° C.
