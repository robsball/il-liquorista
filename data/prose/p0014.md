Besides the spirit itself, fermentation also produces other liquid and volatile substances, which become closely bound to it and affect its flavor either pleasantly or unpleasantly. In the first case, the spirit is, right after distillation, a product ready for use — such as grain spirit, rum, cognac, kirsch, etc. In the other case, as with potato spirit, it is a crude product that requires repeated purification to rid it of its unpleasant taste.

In this pure state, potato or grain spirit is preferably used for making liqueurs, of which it forms the true base — the intoxicating principle.

This group of liqueurs includes the various brandies (acqueviti) obtained by distillation, either from fermented sugary juices or from the secondary products of various industries: thus the distillation of wine gives the various Cognacs; that of grape pomace and wine lees gives Grappa; that of sugarcane or molasses gives Rum; that of fermented cherry juice gives Kirsch; that of ripe plums gives Slivovitz; that of rice juice gives Arrack; that of cereals — wheat, rye, etc. — gives Whisky; that of corn gives Civica, etc.

Reserving for later a discussion of the particular characteristics of each, we note here only that these brandies contain alcohol along with various other products that vary according to the raw material used. A certain number of these products already exist in the sugary juice itself; others instead result from more or less profound transformations undergone by the natural constituents during fermentation or distillation, as we shall see further on.

Alcohol itself, being oxidized under the influence of ferments and atmospheric oxygen, contributes to the mixture that makes up the liqueur a number of products of great importance: these various compounds constitute the bouquet of the brandies.

The commercial value of these products depends on their aroma and good taste, but it also depends on the alcoholic strength, which ranges from 40 to 65%.
