# Observations

When it is necessary to use an open-flame still, even without any scorching of the substances contained in the boiler, the product obtained is considerably less delicate than that obtained using a water bath; the excessive degree of heat reached will have caused, along with the essential oils one wished to obtain, the distillation of harsh — if not empyreumatic — products as well, from which the liquor must be freed.

The operation whose purpose is this purification is precisely what is called rectification. It is generally carried out by performing a further distillation, this time using a water bath; the addition of water serves to facilitate the release of the essential oils. For the rectification to succeed well, it must be carried out carefully, taking care to achieve good cooling.

Rectification must not be confused with cohobation, which consists of pouring the already-distilled liquid back onto the mass still remaining in the boiler, without interrupting the distillation. This procedure is not advisable, because the prolonged presence of the botanicals in the still causes them to take on a harsh flavor, which can result in inferior products.

It must therefore be held that rectification is indispensable whenever an open-flame still is used, if one wishes to obtain a fine, delicate aroma.

### Observations

To properly carry out a distillation it is necessary to follow a few very simple but highly useful rules.

After carefully cleaning the various parts of the still, one must make sure that they retain no odor. The condensing coil must be washed with care to remove traces of previous distillations; this washing is done with hot water, after having drained the water from the condenser — without which the washing water could not perform its function completely, as it would cool down in the lower part of the coil.
