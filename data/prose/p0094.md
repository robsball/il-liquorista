# Botanical Ingredients (continued)

4. Many other types of cinchona bark are found in commerce, some used specifically for alkaloid extraction. Cinchona barks in general have a bitter taste, some also astringent, and are mostly odorless, except for those coming from *Cinchona officinalis*, which have a distinctive pleasant odor.

They contain several alkaloids, the principal one being quinine.

A good cinchona bark should contain 5 to 8% alkaloids. Calisaya and red cinchona are the richest in quinine.

The method of cultivation greatly influences the development of one alkaloid over another. The best way to detect adulteration is microscopic examination.

**Fragrant Cyperus (Cipero odoroso).** — In Egypt and the Mediterranean region grows *Cyperus rotundus*, also called "long rush" or "fragrant rush." The tubers of this plant are ovoid, about the size of a small nut, tawny in color, whitish and spongy inside. They have a bitter, resinous taste and a pleasant odor.

*Cyperus longus*, instead, has rhizomes with elongated swellings, brown on the outside, reddish inside, with a violet-like odor, and a bitter, astringent, aromatic taste. It is widely used, especially in India.

**Coca.** — In Peru, New Granada, Bolivia, and the Argentine Republic, *Erythroxylon coca* is cultivated on a large scale. It is a small shrub of which only the leaves are used. These are oval, ending in a small point; many are 5–7 cm long and 2–3 cm wide. They are sold dried, but do not keep for more than 6 months. A characteristic feature of coca leaves is a pair of lines running from the base, converging toward the margin, and rejoining at the tip. These leaves contain cocaine, a powerful alkaloid, and hygrine, which is an anesthetic like cocaine.
