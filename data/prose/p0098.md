**Juniper.** — The berries of *Juniperus communis*, a shrub very common in our woods, are brown in color when ripe. Their odor is aromatic, and their taste is at first somewhat sweet, then slightly bitter and mildly acrid. They contain resinous substances, sugars, volatile oil, and a peculiar bitter substance. They are used in medicine as stomachics and diuretics.

**Guaiacum or Holy Wood.** — *Guaiacum officinale* and *Guaiacum sanctum* are small to medium-sized trees native to the Antilles. From them a resin is obtained, which is found in commerce in the form of rounded tears 1 to 2.5 cm in diameter (a superior, rare quality), or in compact masses that almost always contain fragments of wood or bark. It is brittle, with a clean, glassy fracture; it is transparent when in thin pieces, of a greenish-brown color; the powder is gray, but in air or light it soon turns greenish; it has a mild, somewhat bitter taste, though irritating to the throat, and a balsamic odor. It dissolves readily in alcohol. It is composed for the most part of guaiaconic acid.

**Iris, Ireos, Orris.** — *Iris florentina* yields a rhizome that, when fresh, has no odor, but when dried acquires a very delicate perfume similar to that of violets. These plants are widely cultivated in Italy and in France. The rhizome is sold in commerce in tuberous, peeled pieces weighing 20 to 60 grams; they are oblong, whitish-yellow, with numerous scars.

In commerce, *Iris germanica* may sometimes be found mixed in; it closely resembles true orris but has a less intense and less pleasant odor. In addition, orris powder may be adulterated with potato starch, a fraud that is very difficult to detect; for this reason it is advisable always to buy the roots in whole pieces.
