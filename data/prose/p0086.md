These seeds are frequently found adulterated in commerce, mixed with others of similar appearance, especially with aged celery seeds. It is easy to detect the fraud, since when immersed in water the seeds of true anise sink to the bottom, while the foreign ones float.

**Star Anise (Badiana).** — The fruits of *Illicium anisatum* are shaped like a star with 5–8 points of a reddish-brown color; they are compressed, wrinkled, and contain an ovoid seed, flattened, smooth or shiny, brown in color, which encloses within a thin husk a whitish, oily kernel.

It has the odor and flavor of anise, but leaves an unpleasant bitter impression in the mouth. Its aroma is more delicate than that of common anise.

From these fruits a pale yellow essential oil is obtained.

**Marsh Celery (Sedano).** — *Apium graveolens* (Umbelliferae), also called Sedano or sweet celery, is a garden plant common throughout Europe: its root gives off a strong, pleasant odor and has an aromatic taste; it contains an essential oil, Apiol.

The name Appio is also given to *Selinum palustre*, whose root has a strong and aromatic odor, with a pungent and bitter taste. These roots are used medicinally as an aperitif. The seeds, on the other hand, have diuretic properties and are used as ingredients in some liqueurs.

**Orange (Arancio).** — The fruits of *Citrus aurantium* (Aurantiaceae) are familiar to everyone. The sweet varieties, or orange proper, originated in China and India, but are nowadays cultivated in the southern regions of Europe, especially along the Mediterranean coast.

From the peels, which have a bitter and aromatic taste, the essential oil is extracted; these peels are also sold commercially preserved in brine. The flowers, too, are prepared in the same way, though generally the essence is extracted from them as soon as they are gathered. These flowers can be distinguished from those of the citron and lemon by being completely white, whereas the others are more or less pink on the outside.
