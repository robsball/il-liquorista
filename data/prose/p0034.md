By "standard spirit" (proof spirit) is meant one having a density of 0.9198 at a temperature of 15.5° C., corresponding to a strength of 57.09° Tralles. Spirits stronger than the standard spirit are called *over proof*, and those weaker are called *under proof*.

---

Rectified spirit of wine, or ethyl alcohol, is the only one — as already stated — used in the manufacture of liqueurs. It is also called "good-tasting alcohol."

Crude alcohol extracted from cereals by saccharification with diastase or with acids, followed by fermentation with yeast, has a disgusting taste and is harmful to health. The same is true of alcohol obtained by fermentation of musts from beets, fruit, etc., when these have not been carefully purified and rectified, since such musts contain a number of substances formed during fermentation as a result of secondary reactions — especially higher alcohols — the whole of which is known as *fusel oil* and constitutes the impurity of the spirit.

The separation of ethyl alcohol from the impurities that accompany it is achieved through rectification, that is, the repeated and fractional distillation of the low wines (*flemme*).

The first fraction of the distillate, called the *heads* or "bad taste of the heads," consists of alcohol contaminated with the more volatile compounds that distill at a temperature below 78° C., such as acetaldehyde and certain ethers.

Next comes the *middle fraction*, or *heart*, or good-tasting alcohol — the only one that should be used for preparing liqueurs and for raising the alcoholic strength of ordinary spirituous beverages. This is the fraction that distills at a temperature of 78° C.

The last portion forms the *tails*, or "bad taste of the tails," and is quite toxic owing to its high content of high-molecular-weight alcohols that distill at temperatures above 78° C. (butyl and amyl alcohol).
