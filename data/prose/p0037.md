The property of amyl alcohol to transform, through oxidation, into valeric acid—which has an intense, characteristic odor—has also been used by Marquardt for detecting this compound in fusel oil: 30–40 cc of spirit are diluted with distilled water down to 12–15% alcohol, then vigorously shaken with 15 cc of pure chloroform. The chloroform is then washed with an equal volume of water to remove all the ethyl alcohol, after which it is allowed to evaporate.

The residue is placed, with a little water, into a test tube along with a few drops of sulfuric acid, or with enough potassium permanganate solution that the mixture still retains its violet color after 24 hours. The tightly sealed test tube is then set aside. If amyl alcohol is present, one detects—one after another—the odors of valeric aldehyde, amyl valerate ether, and finally valeric acid. The odor of this last compound is so penetrating that, by this method, the presence of as little as 0.005 [grams] of amyl alcohol can be detected.

However, the detection of this alcohol by odor can only be carried out under limited circumstances. If the spirit contains even small amounts of essential oils, their odor will mask that of amyl alcohol and of the compounds into which amyl alcohol is transformed through etherification and oxidation.

The odor test, carried out on alcohol in various ways, can be rendered rather inconclusive not only by the presence of essential oils but also by that of aldehydes, furfural, and certain fatty esters—compounds which it is nonetheless of interest to detect.

In such cases, the chemist, in order to ascertain the presence of impurities, may resort to a series of reactions of a different kind from those described so far, characterized by the appearance of specific colorations.
