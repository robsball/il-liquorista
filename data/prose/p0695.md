# Fruit Wines

We do not wish to pass over in silence, in keeping with the programme we have established, Fruit Wines; however, to avoid increasing the bulk of this volume, and considering that the manufacture of these products is of only secondary interest to the liqueur maker, as it constitutes almost a specialized industry—for which careful and delicate manipulations are required, the description of which would demand a lengthy chapter—we shall limit ourselves to the few necessary remarks as a general guide.

Fruit wines can be prepared either by fermentation or unfermented; and according to the purpose for which they are intended, they can be obtained with varying degrees of alcohol content; that is, they are distinguished into common wines and liquorous wines. We need not concern ourselves with the former.[^1]

The liquorous wines differ also according to the qualities and species of fruits from which they come: some of them are more agreeable than grape wine.

## By Fermentation

Since fruits, especially acidic ones (currants, cherries, raspberries), are poor in sugars, one cannot obtain from them, by fermentation, a wine of long keeping. To produce therefore a good and durable wine, it is necessary to add sugar to the must or freshly pressed juice, to be fermented, or to induce in it saccharification by heating at a temperature of 80–88°C for one quarter of an hour, repeating it after 10–15 hours, depending on whether the fruit is more or less acidic: then the product is exposed to fermentation at a temperature of 25°, after having added a sufficient quantity of sugar, and it is allowed to proceed until the development of gas bubbles has ceased. Then the wine is racked off, the lees are pressed, and the two products are kept separate.
