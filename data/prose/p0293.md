**Observations.** — The currant juice as it comes from the fruit contains in solution a certain quantity of a gelatinous principle (pectin), and moreover holds in suspension the skins and other fibrous residues that the berries yield in very short time, along with a great quantity of these same principles, which all coagulate into a single mass. This is the matter that gives currant juice, when used recently, the property of forming gelatin; whereas when the pectin has been separated by means of fermentation, the juice can only produce syrup.

It will be advantageous to add to the currant one-tenth of its weight of sour cherries, which greatly facilitate the separation of the gelatinous matter and permit avoiding the disagreeable taste that would result from too prolonged fermentation.

Some also color the juice with sour or sweet cherries, but this mixture imparts to it a disagreeable vinous taste.

### Pomegranate

It is prepared as that of wild plum, currant, etc.; it purifies itself in the cellar. Above all, take great care not to crush the seeds.

### Peach

Choose peaches of good quality, well ripened and sound; separate the pits; crush the pulp and reduce it to a mash by means of a little water; after 12 hours of maceration press it with the press in a strong cloth. Having done this, pour the juice into a matrass and cover with dampened parchment paper and immerse in a water bath, but not boiling. The heat coagulates the gelatinous principles, which separate in the form of flakes; after this it only remains to filter the liquid to obtain it perfectly clear. This result will be faster if a little egg white is added to the juice and beaten with it.
