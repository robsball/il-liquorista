As for the liquid to be used for the bain-marie, it is generally plain water, but other liquids whose boiling temperature is higher than that of water can very well be used; such are saline solutions and the so-called fixed oils. In saline solutions, however, the degree of concentration—and therefore also the boiling point—varies rapidly, while oils give off unpleasant empyreumatic vapors.

In certain cases, however, which we will discuss when dealing with rectification, the use of baths of this kind, well prepared and monitored, would always be preferable to that of open-flame stills, whose drawbacks and operational difficulties are well known.

It remains only to speak of steam stills, that is, those in which heating is carried out by means of water vapor circulating in a jacket, that is, between the boiler and a counter-boiler. But since this method of heating is applied only in large factories equipped with special steam generators, we have not thought it necessary to treat the subject extensively here, since the production of distilled liquors on such a scale is very rarely undertaken in our country. ### Rectification

We have said that it is preferable, in the manufacture of liqueurs, to use the bain-marie still, at least when herbs, roots, or solid substances in general are to be distilled. It should be noted, however, that this cannot be established as an absolute rule, since in quite a few cases open-flame distillation is made necessary by the difficulty of extracting, through simple bain-marie heating alone, the aromatic components from certain of the substances used.
