## Glucose — Starch Syrup

In order to increase the density of some mediocre liqueurs, without however sweetening them excessively, one can sometimes resort to the use of glucose, or more properly, starch syrup. This is a practice that has become common for reasons of economy, but which is honestly inadvisable for a good product, and is also in conflict with current hygienic regulations.

Common glucose, or potato-starch syrup, is the product obtained by boiling starch or potato starch for a certain time in the presence of dilute acids (saccharification). It appears in solid form as a crystalline or granular mass, compact, white, with a sweetish taste (its sweetness is to that of sucrose as 1:2½), soluble in water.

Generally, however, it is used in syrup form, that is, as obtained by evaporating the product of the saccharification of starchy substances to a syrupy consistency.

This syrup — of which industry supplies various qualities — can be obtained from all starchy substances by saccharifying them either with dilute acids or with diastase.
