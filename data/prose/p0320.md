# Clarification and Aging of Spirits

The filtration material is spread over a wool pad, and a sheet of filter paper or cellulose paste (the same kind used for Frick filters) is placed inside the dish. The spirits are passed or repassed through several times until clear, and then the apparatus can be considered in continuous operation. If possible, it is best to enclose this type of filter bag in a container like those used for Dutch filtration, so that the spirits absorb as little air as possible.

One can also try using shredded asbestos in the filter bag as a clarifying material, or we believe that Seitz filters may work well for spirits too.

Good results are also obtained by using Lobrija Earth, applied without needing to pass it through a filter, but directly as a clarifying agent—shredding about 100–150 g per hectoliter for domestic use and agitating the mass vigorously for about 5 minutes. The earth settles, and then one need only decant the spirits, now clear.

It often happens that when one has a wine spirit of high alcohol content—say, at 70°—and dilutes it with water to a lower strength—say, 45°—in order to age it, the spirit becomes turbid.

The cause of turbidity may be the quantity of water added: one may have added ordinary water to the spirit, which is perhaps rich in lime. To avoid this, it is advisable always to use boiled water, or better still, distilled water, and turbidity will never occur; unless the spirit was derived from starchy substances or grape pomace and was not well rectified.

Meanwhile, in the case in question, the turbid mass can be clarified with Spanish earth at a dose of 60 to 100 g per hectoliter, and decanted as soon as the liquid becomes clear.

**Aging.** — It is aging that perfects and renders the spirit transformable into cognac. For this purpose, the distillate is stored in oak barrels with rather thick staves, or kept in cool, well-ventilated warehouses. The storage should last a minimum of 4 years, preferably 5, 6, or 7 years. A certain amount of time is always necessary for a spirit to acquire the required characteristics. During storage, very complex phenomena occur—some of a physical nature, others of a chemical nature.
