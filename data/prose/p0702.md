# Examination of Liqueurs

## Composition of Liqueurs by Maceration and Digestion

When calculating the dosage of the aromatic mixture packet to produce 2 liters of liqueur, the total quantity of the overall aromatic blend must be taken into account.

## Examination of Liqueurs

Whenever one wishes to determine the composition of a liqueur containing several aromatics, with the aim of imitating a commercial product, one proceeds as follows.

Measure with precision one liter of the liquid to be examined and treat it with one liter of highly rectified alcohol, completely free of fusel oil. Make the mixture in a tall, narrow bottle and shake it vigorously for 10 minutes; let it rest for half an hour and repeat the shaking 3 to 4 times, then let it rest overnight.

After this time, two sufficiently distinct layers are observed in the bottle: the lower, denser layer consists of water and sugar; the upper, more fluid layer is the solution of aromatic substances in alcohol.

Remove the upper layer by means of a pipette, transfer it to a small distillation apparatus, and heat the flask cautiously to a maximum of 70°C. Care must be taken that the temperature does not exceed 70°, because at this temperature the alcohol and water evaporate, albeit slowly, while the essential oils for the most part remain in the flask.

When the contents of the vessel are reduced to approximately one-twentieth (50 cc), suspend heating, allow the liquid to cool completely, and pour it into a graduated cylinder, in which it is treated with twice its volume of rectified sulfuric ether and shaken vigorously. After allowing it to rest for some time, the contents separate again into two well-defined layers: the lower consists of pure water containing only traces of essential oils in solution; the upper consists of sulfuric ether, alcohol, and almost all the quantity of essential oils contained in the original quantity of liqueur subjected to examination.
