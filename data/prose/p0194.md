# Clarification (continued) and Fining

Clarifying with alum is accompanied by a loss of vegetable substances from the liqueur. But this is not the only drawback. Only a small portion of the alumina precipitates to the bottom in combination with the vegetable substances. Most of it remains dissolved in the liqueur together with the other constituent of alum, potassium sulfate, which has an unpleasant bitter-salty taste and therefore harms the flavor of the liqueur. However, no better clarifying agent is known so far, so it is still necessary to use alum. It should be added after first being dissolved in boiling water, then mixed into the liqueur.

Generally, burnt alum (calcined alum) is used, which is believed to be flavorless — though it is not, since calcining only removes the flavorless water of crystallization, while the other constituents remain. The only difference is that the same effect is obtained using about half the weight of burnt alum compared to ordinary alum.

Commercial clarifying powders are a mixture of alum and potassium carbonate. When these constituents dissolve in the liqueur, the sulfuric acid from the alum reacts with the potassium carbonate, releasing carbon dioxide and producing effervescence. From the alum, insoluble alumina forms, which precipitates to the bottom, carrying down with it the impurities of the liqueur. In this way clarification occurs, and if the proportions of the mixture are correct, the liqueur does not pick up the taste of alum; however, it does end up incorporating a large amount of potassium sulfate, which remains dissolved in it — so the result is actually worse than when using alum alone.

Fine liqueurs, even when heavily sweetened, filter easily through filter paper. This is the only clarifying method from which no harm need be feared.

**Fining.** — To give liqueurs perfect transparency, fining is carried out using albumin (egg whites), fish glue (isinglass), or the finest quality gelatin.

1. To fine with egg whites, take 3 egg whites per hectoliter of liqueur, beat them with one liter of water, and then add this to the liqueur, stirring vigorously; then let it stand at rest for 24–48 hours.
