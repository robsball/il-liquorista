1. Boiler; 14. Furnace, made of sheet iron. The upper rim of the boiler is shaped into a gutter and forms a hydraulic seal, with a drain spout at 2 for the cooling water, which passes over the lid 3 — which in this apparatus replaces the "head" (cap) of other stills. This cold water comes from the condenser by way of a special tube or regulating tap, 10. A uniform distribution of this water over the lid is achieved by means of the regulating ring 4. For the rest, the still is entirely similar to the one described previously. The boiler, once its lid has been removed, can be used for other purposes, like an ordinary boiler. To better regulate the cooling of the lid, it is covered with a thin cloth.

To use this still, it is filled to 4/5 of its capacity with the liquid to be distilled, and the lid is set in place, joining it to the swan-neck and to the coil. The rim of the lid comes to rest in the groove or gutter already described, which — being filled with water — creates a perfect seal, precisely as in gas-holder tanks.

After filling the condenser with cold water, the fire is lit in the furnace. The alcoholic vapors, guided by a special dividing plate fitted to the inner wall of the lid, skim across the inner surface of the lid itself as they make their way toward the swan-neck. Since the cooling of the lid can be regulated at will by means of the regulator already mentioned, it is possible to cause the condensation, in that part of the still, of a portion of the vapors given off by the liquid — especially the less volatile ones; the rest go on to condense in the coil and are collected from tube 13.

It is clear that, by properly regulating the flow of water to the lid of the boiler, products of varying alcoholic richness can be obtained. Should one wish to obtain products even richer in alcohol with a single distillation, one may use the still fitted with a rectifying lens, shown in Fig. 5.
