# Unfermented Fruit Wines and Liqueurs

...first crush the fruits, then strain and press the must through a press; filter it and dissolve sugar in it cold; then heat rapidly until boiling and, before it cools completely, add the desired proportion of good-quality or aromatized spirits.

A highly prized liqueur produced in America comes from interrupted fermentation of apples (selected and sound): these are first pressed; in the juice obtained, reduced to about half by evaporation, fermentation is initiated using strong yeast: after 24 hours it is interrupted and the product, optionally aromatized, is bottled.

**Unfermented Fruit Sweet Wines**

Unfermented fruit wines resemble Ratafias or fruit liqueurs already described: they are based on cooked fruit juices mixed with spirits, in which the slight acidity from repeated cooking converts into one of the constituent principles of sugary matter.

Unlike those obtained by fermentation, these sweet wines prove pleasant, without acidity or harshness, immediately after preparation and therefore require no aging.

To prepare them, fruits must be crushed or mashed, the juice pressed out promptly and exposed to cooking, adding the necessary quantity of sugar cold until it achieves the required density, which should be 18–20° on the syrup scale.

For cherries, raspberries, strawberries, currants, and blackberries, heat the juice for about one hour at 70–80°C: then let it rest in a cool place for 12–15 hours: filter it and add one liter of brandy at 55–58° for every 8 liters of must: stir well and collect promptly in bottles, which are stoppered with strong corks, secured with plaster or sealing wax.

For apricots, peaches, plums, etc., the pits must first be removed: then heat for one hour, allow to cool for about 12 hours in the cellar; then press out the juice, which is reduced to the density indicated above.
