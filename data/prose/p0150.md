**Chlorophyll.** — Liquid Chlorophyll is available commercially, and can be obtained by the following process devised by Schulz:

The leaves of a harmless plant rich in green color (spinach, lettuce, ivy, etc.) are extracted with boiling alcohol; the liquid is filtered while still hot and then left to stand in a cool place: in this way the crude coloring matter separates from the alcohol and is collected by filtration.

To purify it, it is treated with an alcoholic solution of soda (sodium hydroxide/carbonate), filtered, and a stream of carbon dioxide is passed through the liquid until no more precipitate forms; it is filtered again and the collected precipitate is treated with cold alcohol. Saturated salt (sodium chloride) solution is added to the resulting solution, and the newly formed precipitate is collected, dissolved in boiling alcohol; the alcohol is then distilled off and the residue evaporated to dryness.

The residue is washed with water, acidified with acetic acid, and extracted with ether. On evaporating this, pure chlorophyll is obtained, soluble in alcohol and in ether.

**Cochineal and Carmine.** — Cochineal is the dried female of an insect, *Coccus cacti*, native especially to Central America: these insects are globular, about 5 mm long, of a silvery-gray-brown color; they are collected and dried before they lay their eggs. When crushed, they are reduced to a dark red powder. They must not be spoiled by worms.

Several commercial grades are found: the best known are gray or 'Argentina' cochineal, 'morellona' (dark/blackish) cochineal, and wild or red cochineal.

Cochineal contains about 50% of an intensely red-colored substance. However, it is frequently adulterated by mixing in already-exhausted material dyed with other substances, mineral colorants, etc. It is used in the preparation of Carmine, which consists of the coloring principle of cochineal combined with other substances, and is manufactured industrially by various processes.

Carmine is sold commercially as a powder: it is insoluble in water and in alcohol, but readily soluble in alkaline liquids. The most prized quality is Nakarat Carmine.
