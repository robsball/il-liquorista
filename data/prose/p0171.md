In this way one comes to know not only the flavor of each individual plant substance, but one can also judge which plants, and in what proportions, are suited to being blended together. One is moreover able to quickly compose, from these individual tinctures, liqueurs in which the percentage of active drug material can easily be calculated from the quantity of tincture used.

Those who, in composing new liqueurs, intend to proceed with certainty and to spare themselves research that requires time and materials, must also be able to form beforehand an exact idea of the flavor of the liqueur to be prepared, choose the suitable materials accordingly, and approximately calculate their quantitative proportions.

The bitter extractive substances are found dissolved in the sap of the living plant, that is, in an aqueous liquid. They also dissolve well in a weak alcohol of 45 to 60%, but no longer in alcohol of 90–95%. The laxative constituent of senna leaves, for example, is completely insoluble in high-proof alcohol; other substances also dissolve in it only imperfectly. This is the first reason why, in extractions, the use of high-proof alcohol should be avoided.

A second group of extractive substances is formed by the resins, which are to be regarded as secondary formations derived from the essential principles of the plant. These are found especially in plants rich in essential oil that have aged — for example, having been stored for a long time; in such cases they can be rectified [redistilled] in order to free them of the resins.

Resins are the worst enemy of liqueur-making. Since they are soluble and kept in solution only in alcohol of at least 70%, they separate out again if they are introduced — together with essences or tinctures of high proof — into weak liqueurs, causing that milky clouding which is sometimes very difficult to dispel. From tinctures, the resin can be eliminated in a simple way by macerating the plants with alcohol of at most 60%. Tinctures made with weak alcohol cloud the liqueurs. This does not mean, however, that one should consider it wise to avoid, for the extraction of plants rich in essential oil, alcohol of 90% or higher.
