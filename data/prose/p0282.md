**Maraschino** — Marasca cherries are taken, ripe and of an acidic flavor; they are cleaned of the stem and pressed like grapes when they are crushed, keeping the pits separate. They are then left to ferment in an earthenware vessel for 48 hours, after having added a pinch of cinnamon and stirring from time to time. After this, the juice is extracted either by means of a press or by squeezing the cherries little by little by hand through a loose cloth. The expressed juice is allowed to settle and then the clear part is decanted. For every kg of juice thus obtained, 1.3 kg of white sugar or 1 gram of citric acid is used, adding these when the juice is already heated: the whole thing is boiled for 4–5 minutes, stirring well: a prolonged boil would diminish the aroma. It is left to cool and poured into bottles, which are well stoppered and kept in the cellar.

One may also add, before fermentation, the seeds of the cherries dried in the sun and pounded in a mortar, using 25 grams for every kg of marasche cherries.

**Currant** — To 1 kg of currant not at complete maturity or 300 grams of fine cherry or an equal amount of raspberries, the pits and stems (or petioles) are removed, and the juice is expressed into an earthenware vessel; it is passed through a sieve or left to rest for three or four days in a cool place, keeping the vessel well sealed so that the fragrance does not dissipate.

The liquid is then strained through a small cloth, 100 grams of sugar per 100 grams of juice obtained is added, and it is heated in a water bath. When the sugar is completely dissolved, the heat is removed and the liquid is cooled, and thus the syrup is obtained, which is preserved in hermetically sealed bottles.

The sojourn of the juice in a cool place, before adding the sugar to it, has the purpose of clarifying it by means of the fermentation that establishes itself there, and making it deposit the mucilaginous parts, which then remain on the cloth through which it is strained.
