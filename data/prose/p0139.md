As already noted, the whole question comes down to deciding whether one should use the separated essential oil or the alcoholic extract of the plant material. The great convenience of working with essential oils has encouraged many abuses, introducing into liquor-making certain essences that have no real reason to be there. In the case of lower-quality liquors, such essential oils or essences may be used; but never in finer liquors.

Considering the overall results of observations on the various essential oils, the following conclusions may be drawn:

1. Distilled essential oils contain only the volatile constituents of the plants, in a state of perfect purity, but not in their full original character. Over time they lose quality, especially if exposed to light.

2. If only these volatile constituents need to be retained in the flavor of a liquor, cold-process manufacturing can and should make use of essential oils.

However, even in such cases one might prefer alcoholic tinctures distilled over the plant material, which are not subject to deterioration.

3. If the liquor must contain, besides the volatile components, also non-volatile ones — usually bitter-tasting substances — such essential oils cannot be used; instead, non-distilled alcoholic extracts (tinctures) of the plants must be employed.

## Animal Substances

In liquor-making today, only ambergris and musk are still used. In the past, civet and castoreum were also employed.

**Ambergris (grey amber)** is an intestinal concretion found in sperm whales. It is collected on the coasts of Madagascar, Java, etc. It has recently been discovered that the development of this substance's characteristic odor is due to the slow action of a specific microbe. It occurs in ovoid masses, often covered with marine encrustations; when fractured, it reveals the concentric, zonal structure typical of such concretions.
