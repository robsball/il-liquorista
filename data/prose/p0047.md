The scale of the official Tralles and Gay-Lussac alcoholometers runs from 0° to 100°: each division from 0° to 50° corresponds to one degree; the following degrees are divided into half-degrees. The stem of the alcoholometers must be cylindrical, polished, and free of irregularities on its surface.

For comparing these alcoholometers, which are linked by a relationship to the density of the liquid, suitable comparative tables were calculated, such as the one shown on pages 38-39.

It must be repeated that the density of a spirit varies not only with the alcoholic strength, but also varies considerably with temperature, whereas, as we have mentioned, alcoholometers are graduated at a temperature of 15° or 15.5° C. Therefore these simple instruments are built with an attached thermometer, so that, by immersing them in the liquid, one can observe at the same time both the degree reading and the temperature. To correct for this, when the temperature observed during the measurement differs from that at which the alcoholometer is graduated — which can lead to significant differences in the evaluation of the alcohol content — tables are used, such as those published in the Official Bulletin of Excise Duties (Bollettino ufficiale delle gabelle) of 1882 for the use of the official alcoholometer, based on a temperature of +15.50 centigrade degrees, which we reproduce here on pages 40-44.

However, even in the absence of tables, the actual value can be corrected by adopting the Francoeur formula:

x = c + 0.4 × t

where x is the real percentage at 15° C., c is the percentage read on the hydrometer at the observed temperature t; +0.4 is added for such t if it is below 15° C.; −0.4 is subtracted for such t if it is above 15° C. The correction obtained by means of this formula, however, is only approximate, and it is therefore preferable to use the special tables.
