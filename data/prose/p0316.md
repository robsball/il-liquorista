# Brandy (Acquavite)

## Brandy from Wine — Cognac

Brandy from wine is an alcoholic liquid obtained by distillation of the alcohol produced from fermentation of grape must; it is, in other words, the product of the exclusive distillation of wine.

Brandy can therefore be distilled from any natural wine. However, it should be distilled especially from white wines, whose aroma is more delicate and pronounced than that of red wines.

Even highly alcoholic, healthy white wines of good quality can yield excellent brandies: but it is advisable to use such raw material only when white wine is sold at a low price, otherwise the product would not yield value proportionate to its cost. Red wine, if defective, when distilled produces brandy of mediocre quality.

According to its origin and also according to its flavor, the distillate is finer.

Through the action of aging for 5 to 6 years at least in oak casks, brandy acquires characteristics that make it potable and pleasant, so much so that it can be considered as cognac. The quality and finesse of brandy depend on the nature of the wine used, on the care taken in distillation, on the wood of the storage vessel, and on the duration of the aging that the liquid undergoes before being destined for consumption.

Fine brandy or fine cognac is obtained only from healthy, light, acidulous white wines without any defect.

To obtain very good brandies, the grapes are submitted to pressing, the must is fermented in covered vats, and then the wine obtained is distilled.

Brandy from wine is generally prepared by a single direct distillation.
