**2°** The volume of a rectangular tank — that is, a rectangular parallelepiped — equals the product of its three internal dimensions: length, width, and height.

**3°** The volume of a cylindrical boiler (a right cylinder) equals the product of its base times its height — that is, the area of the circular base (found by multiplying the square of the radius by the constant ratio 3.1415) multiplied by the height of the cylinder.

*Example.* — Given a diameter of 2.20 m and a height of 4.30 m, the radius is 2.20 : 2 = 1.10; multiplied by itself, this gives 1.21. Then 1.21 × 3.1415 = 3.8012, the area of the base. Multiplying this by 4.30 (the height) gives 16.345 cubic meters, corresponding to 163.45 hectoliters.

**4°** To assess the capacity of a round cask, take the square of the larger radius (half the larger diameter multiplied by itself), the square of the smaller radius (likewise), and the product of the larger radius by the smaller. Sum these three values and multiply the result by the height (or length) of the cask and by the constant 1.0472.

A brief note on estimating the tare of liquid-holding containers whose tare is not marked, and which must therefore be weighed gross, will also be useful here. For this purpose we report some rules drawn from the tariffs of the main municipalities regarding the consumption tax (dazio consumo).

For casks, barrels, and tubs, the tare is calculated as 10% of the gross weight if hooped entirely in wood, or 15% if hooped entirely in iron.

For demijohns with a wicker covering, 18%; for thick-glass bottles, whether cased or not, 30%; for flasks, carboys, and plain glass vessels, 10%; if wicker-covered, 12%; if wrapped in straw or grass matting, 25%.
