The three zones are crossed by thin, brown, dotted medullary rays, quite distinct, which serve to distinguish this root from that of Bryony and from a gentian called American Colombo, which closely resembles it. Colombo root contains three bitter principles: Berberine, Colombic acid, and Colombin; the latter especially has a very bitter, persistent taste.

Colombo root, once reduced to powder, is greenish-yellow; when macerated in spirit it colors it intensely. It has no odor but a persistent bitter taste.

**Cumin.** — Under the name of cumin seeds one finds in commerce the fruits of *Cuminum cyminum*, an umbelliferous plant native to Egypt, cultivated throughout southern Europe, in China, Arabia, India, etc. The fruits are ovoid, somewhat elongated, flattened laterally, of an opaque yellowish color, and have a strong, aromatic odor and taste. They are longer than German cumin, or caraway (almost double); they have abundant white, oily albumen. The best seeds come from Malta.

**Coriander.** — The fruits of *Coriandrum sativum* have the shape and size of peppercorns, and are yellowish in color. When fresh they have a rather repugnant odor, which becomes pleasant once dried. They are cultivated extensively in Egypt, Spain, Germany, and especially in France (Touraine).

**Cretan Dauco.** — This name refers to the fruits or seeds of *Athamanta cretensis*; they are slightly downy, semi-cylindrical, about 5 mm long. The seeds found in commerce are frequently adulterated with those of *Daucus carota*: these, however, are only 2 mm long, flattened on one side and convex on the other, covered with long hairs different from the downy coating of the Cretan Dauco. They are grayish-yellow in color, with an aromatic odor and a pleasantly pungent taste.
