# Aging of Spirits: Chemical and Physical Transformations

First of all, there is a diminution in the volume of the liquid, owing to which after a certain number of years the containers present a void space. The alcoholic strength of the liquid also diminishes. Subsequently, from being colorless, the liquid becomes pale straw-yellow, then amber-yellow; then dark yellow. The flavor loses its burning quality and acquires at first a slight taste of vanilla and tannin, and a greater delicacy. If aging in the same container is prolonged considerably, the flavor becomes rough due to excess tannic substance.

These transformations in flavor are due to the dissolution of soluble matter from the wood of the cask, namely: tannin, quercetin, gallic acid, and acerin; but it is the first two substances united with vanilla that contribute to giving the distillate special color and flavor.

To avoid getting an astringent and rough brandy, it is necessary to use wooden casks poor in tannic substance, such as: oak from Stettin, Lübeck, or the Rhine, or from America. Excellent are the woods from Berry, Charente, Limousin, etc. Oak from Slavonia also gives good results, if the barrels are well purified with fine brandy before filling them. One can also, after a few years of storage, transfer the brandy into other used containers that are therefore free of extractive substances.

The second category of phenomena is represented by chemical reactions, the principal agent of which is the oxygen from the air, which, penetrating through the pores of the wood as evaporation takes place, dissolves in the liquid upon which it acts in various ways.

From the sum of these phenomena that unfold during aging, brandy gradually acquires body, color, flavor, and special aromas.

To age brandies that, when freshly extracted from grape refuse, have a sour, biting flavor, the practice in France is to pour, for every liter of new brandy, five to six drops of ammonia, stirring vigorously for a certain time. Within a few days, the acetic acid being neutralized, the brandy loses its sourness and appears as good as that which has several years of storage. This addition is in no way harmful to health, provided care is taken to separate the clear part from the small deposit that forms.
