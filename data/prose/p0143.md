# Sugar

Commercial grades of sugar would take us too far afield, so we will limit ourselves to a few brief notes (1).

Of all the various sweetening substances, those generally grouped under the name of saccharose sugar are cane sugar and beet sugar.

Both cane sugar and beet sugar are offered by the trade in various grades, depending on the degree of refining to which they were subjected; thus, starting from ordinary yellowish-brown sugar, a very impure product, one arrives through successive gradations at pilé-extra, which represents the finest and purest saccharose.

We will recall the main types:

Raw colonial sugars and raw exotic sugars: these are more or less yellowish-brown, crystallized in coarse grain or in crystalline masses containing molasses;

Raw domestic sugars: colored pale yellow or yellowish-brown, in small crystals;

Refined sugar: of which one distinguishes the following qualities — loaf, crystalline, pilé, centrifuged, cube, powdered, and candied.

Commercial raw sugars contain a more or less high percentage of crystallized sugar along with a certain amount of water, invert sugar or glucose, and mineral and organic substances.

In the manufacture of liqueurs, saccharose is used for fine liqueurs, and it is the only sugar that allows good products to be obtained.

Refined sugar therefore represents the finest quality, regardless of whether it comes from cane or beet.

Crystalline sugar and yellowish raw sugar should be used with great caution. One must never use sugar whose impurity can already be detected by smell. The improper taste comes from adhering molasses, which is often also the cause of the yellowish color. Another sign of impurity is provided by the sugar's tendency to become damp, which likewise comes from the molasses.
