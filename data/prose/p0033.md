# Commercial Grades of Alcohol

It can be mixed with water in any proportion; such a mixture produces a rise in temperature and a decrease in volume (contraction).

**Commercial grades of alcohol.** — In commerce, several qualities of ethyl alcohol are distinguished:

1) **Absolute alcohol**, chemically pure, anhydrous: a product used only in chemical laboratories;

2) **Commercial absolute alcohol**: contains 0.3–1% water; it is very pure.

3) **Concentrated alcohol or fine rectified spirit**: reads 95°–97° on the Gay-Lussac alcoholometer (42°–45° Baumé);

4) **First-quality rectified spirit**: contains 94–96% alcohol; it is pure;

5) **Second-quality rectified spirit**: reads 92°–94° on the Gay-Lussac alcoholometer and is somewhat impure;

6) **Concentrated alcohol or highly rectified spirit**: reads 90°–91° on the Gay-Lussac alcoholometer (39° Baumé).

7) **Diluted alcohol or rectified spirit**: reads 68°–69° on the Gay-Lussac alcoholometer (26°–28° Baumé).

Besides these, the names *triple spirit* are given to alcohol of 96–97%, *double spirit* to that of 88–90%, and *simple spirit* to that of a lower grade.

As a curiosity, we may mention that in France it is customary to use an empirical classification of commercial spirits, based on the so-called **Proof of Holland** (*Prova d'Olanda*), according to which spirits are distinguished into fractions such as 3/6, 4/6, 5/6, 7/6, etc.

The **Proof of Holland** is obtained by vigorously shaking a brandy of a certain alcoholic strength in a bottle that is not completely full: a foam (called *pearl*) forms on the surface and persists for some time even at rest. The brandy that shows this phenomenon [without dilution] is taken as the reference type, marked 50°. Spirits of higher alcoholic strength are evaluated by the amount of water that must be added to obtain the foam phenomenon: thus, a brandy of *3/6* is one that requires 3 volumes of itself and 3 volumes of water to give 6 volumes of alcohol showing the Proof of Holland: the spirit *3/6* contains 85% alcohol, and is further distinguished into *3/6* of good taste and *3/6* of poor taste.
