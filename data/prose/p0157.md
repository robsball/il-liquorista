# Stills (Simple Apparatus)

These apparatus, built of copper — usually tinned — were considerably improved, as we shall see; it is worth noting, however, that for certain operations the best results are still obtained with simple, though not primitive, apparatus, such as, for example, those used in Bulgaria for the distillation of roses.

It is precisely these simple stills that the liquor-maker uses, though not the rudimentary ones.

Fig. 2 represents one of the simplest open-fire stills, called a swan-neck still.

In it three main parts can be distinguished:

*Fig. 2.*

1. **The boiler (A)**, anciently called the *cucurbit*, intended to hold the substances to be distilled.

2. **The head or cap (B)**, placed above the boiler, through which — either directly or by means of a delivery tube — it is put in communication with the condenser.

3. **The condenser (D)**, which contains cold cooling water, into which the delivery tube ends; this tube, in order to have a greater cooling surface, is shaped into a coil and is called the *worm* (C).
