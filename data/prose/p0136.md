Adulteration with alcohol is less harmful than that with fixed oils; unlike the latter, it does not have the drawback of making the essential oil viscous — instead it makes it more fluid — and it does not alter its color.

Such mixing does not diminish the quality of the essence, but only its value, and surprisingly so even when the addition exceeds 20%. Smaller additions of alcohol are sometimes necessary in order to keep the essence clear.

**Fixed Oils.** — An essential oil adulterated with the addition of fixed oils will be less fluid the greater the quantity of such oils added to it.

Pour 2 or 3 drops of the essential oil onto a piece of filter paper and heat it gently; the essential oil will volatilize, and if fixed oil had been mixed in with it, a persistent stain will remain on the paper. If this stain can be removed by treating it with absolute alcohol, it is a sign of castor oil, oleine, or resins.

One can also pour a certain quantity of essential oil into a graduated tube and add 8 volumes of alcohol, shaking well. The alcohol dissolves the volatile oil and leaves the fixed oil, which settles to the bottom of the tube so that its volume can be read off. If some of this oil clings to the walls of the tube, its descent can be helped along with light taps.

**Common Volatile Oils.** — Expensive essential oils may sometimes be found mixed with similar, cheaper ones: for example, essences of citron, bergamot, and bitter orange mixed with essence of sweet orange; English peppermint essence mixed with American peppermint essence; anise essence with fennel essence; rose essence with palmarosa essence, etc. To this category also belongs the worst of all adulterations — cutting with turpentine essence.

Adulteration with common essential oils, such as those of turpentine, rosemary, lavender, etc., is very difficult to detect.
