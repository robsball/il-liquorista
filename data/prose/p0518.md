Generally, the plant materials are extracted first with strong alcohol and then with the necessary quantity of water; the liquids from the two extractions are combined and left standing for a long time to clarify. This method achieves complete extraction; moreover, the spirit, which is always retained in considerable quantity by the plant material itself, is almost entirely displaced by the water.

Sometimes these extracts can be simply concentrated tinctures of a single substance.

Without specifying the exact strength for each individual plant, we note that the following tinctures are prepared from 500 g of plant material per 1 kg of 80° alcohol: Bergamot, Cacao, Catechu, Cinchona, Bitter Orange (Curaçao); or with 70° alcohol instead: Blessed Thistle, Gentian, Galangal, Lavender, Marjoram, Rosemary, Juniper.

The following tinctures are prepared with 1 kg of plant material per 1 kg of 75° alcohol: Angelica, Aloe, Bitter Almond, Calamus, Lemon, Ginger, Cumin, Coffee, Rose.

For Clove and Tonka Bean tinctures, only 100 g of these materials are used; for Rhubarb, Mace, Cinnamon, Cardamom tinctures, 200 g per 1 kg of 85° alcohol.

Clearly, the various aromatic spirits we described in the chapter on liqueur preparation by maceration could be obtained as extracts—that is, in concentrated form—and then diluted with alcohol or mixed with water and sugar to prepare the liqueur.

We have already indicated the various methods of preparing the materials that, when combined together, constitute extracts: namely, tinctures, alcoholates, essences, and aromatic waters. However, mixtures of different simple tinctures to compose a mixed tincture should be avoided as much as possible, for the reasons we have already mentioned when discussing these.

It is advisable to use tinctures, aromatic spirits, extracts, etc., after some time has elapsed since their preparation, especially for the production of fine liqueurs and bitters: the more they are aged, the better the result obtained.
