# Clarification

Coloring should only be done for those liqueurs that will not later require clarification by artificial means. With cloudy liqueurs one cannot yet judge their color, so it is pointless to color them; instead, they should be colored once the liqueur has been clarified.

### Clarification

One of the essential conditions that a liqueur must satisfy is perfect clarity — a quality that is, moreover, rightly sought in almost all beverages, whether alcoholic or not.

It is an indisputable fact that a liqueur could be a true nectar, yet it would not be appreciated at all if cloudy; and even if justice were done to its good qualities, one would still end up lamenting its lack of transparency.

Moreover, this distrust is not without good justification, since in most cases the substances that cloud a liqueur are due to imperfect, careless workmanship, and often they actually alter the flavor as well.

Any clarification performed with a substance that cannot later be removed from the liqueur is harmful to the liqueur's quality. Artificial clarification is a manipulation that the liqueur maker must avoid as much as possible. The cloudiness that forms during the preparation of liqueurs generally disappears in a short time without the need to resort to artificial means.

Strong cloudiness occurs following careless use of essential oils, and in such cases it is not always possible to do without artificial clarifying agents, or at least it is not always possible to wait for clarification to occur on its own.

The best clarifying agent, and also the most commonly used, is alum — a double salt, which is aluminum potassium sulfate. Aluminum sulfate is the active part of alum. It has the property of combining with dissolved vegetable substances and precipitating them out of solution.

Alum cannot therefore be used to clarify colored liqueurs or those containing vegetable extractive substances.
