# Alcohol — Origin — Production — Testing — Purification — Alcoholometry — Dilution

## OF THE RAW MATERIALS

### Alcohol

*Origin — Production — Testing — Purification — Alcoholometry — Dilution*

Ordinary alcohol, or ethyl or "vinic" alcohol (also called spirit of wine, aqua ardens, aquavite, etc.), is, as we have already noted, the principal constituent — indeed the very base — of liqueurs. As is well known, it is the product of the fermentation of sugary substances.

The First International Congress for the Suppression of Fraud in Foodstuffs, held in Geneva in September 1908 (see page 1), established the following definition: "Ordinary alcohol is the product of the distillation, followed by rectification, of a fermented liquid, whatever it may be."

While fermented liquids such as wine, beer, and cider were known from the most remote antiquity, alcohol itself was not known and isolated until the Middle Ages. Just how and when it came into popular use in the form of aquavite or liqueur is not known with precision; it is certain, however, that in the fourteenth century the people of Modena traded in aquavite and even sent it beyond the Alps. The use of these alcoholic beverages then spread through Europe during the medieval wars; and the plagues, during which the use of "aqua vitae" (water of life) was recommended [as a remedy], also contributed to popularizing the use of this beverage.

At first, alcohol was obtained exclusively by distilling wine, of which it is one of the principal constituents. Nowadays, however, almost all the alcohol found on the market is obtained from the fermentation of saccharified mashes made from cereals (wheat, rice, maize), potatoes, and sugar beets...
