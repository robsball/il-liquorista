After having arranged — or, as it is said, charged — the apparatus, using the indicated substances and the quantity of alcohol and water, it is covered and luted as we have just described, then heated to a temperature of about 50-60°C, or left to itself. After 24 hours, the quantity of aromatic spirit indicated by the recipe is distilled off, discarding however the small portions that distill cloudy at the start of the operation.

The distillation must proceed with regularity, which depends on good regulation of the fire or on continuous supervision. The best conditions occur when a continuous thin stream of distillate flows from the coil (worm). Too strong a fire causes the fusel oils to rise, giving the product a very bad empyreumatic flavor.

It must therefore be remembered that too strong a burst of fire can cause, in naked-flame stills, a sudden projection of the liquid itself from the boiler into the coil, forcing the operator to abandon the operation.

In the event that alcoholic vapors should find or open a path through the joints of the pieces and catch fire, the fire must be extinguished at once, and the flames smothered with wet cloths.

The watery liquid remaining in the still, after cooling, is drained off through the discharge tap, and in certain cases it is worth keeping it to use in subsequent operations where one wishes to obtain an aromatized spirit of the same kind, substituting it for the water to be used.
