The liquid to be filtered is placed in a long-necked glass vessel of the necessary capacity (a flask, a bottle, a round-bottom flask, etc.), without filling it completely. A rubber or perforated cork stopper is fitted to the mouth of the neck, and into the hole of this stopper is fitted a glass tube protruding about 10 cm beyond the stopper. The vessel, thus arranged, is then suspended upside down above the funnel, so that the end of the tube reaches into the middle of the filter cavity, about 2 cm below its rim. The liquid will then flow into the funnel until it reaches the level of the tube's opening; the tube, with its end just touching the surface of the liquid that has flowed into the filter, will—by an elementary physical law—prevent it from overflowing. Meanwhile, as the level gradually drops due to the liquid passing through as filtrate, new portions will regularly descend to restore and maintain the level of the liquid surface in the filter at the mouth of the tube, and so on until the vessel containing the liquid to be filtered has been completely emptied.

For large quantities of liqueurs, continuous or pressure filters may finally be used.

## Coloring

As a result of blending, especially of tinctures, liqueurs take on an uncertain color, of unattractive appearance, which must be corrected by means of coloring matter. The coloring of liqueurs takes as its starting point the color of the plant [used], which imparts to the liqueur its predominant flavor. The more evident this color is in the liqueur, the more successful the coloring may be considered. This principle should always be maintained, avoiding any coloring that is too pronounced or unnatural—first of all because such coloring looks poor to the eye of a connoisseur, and secondly because strong coloring affects the flavor of the liqueurs. Naturally, the coloring matters used must be harmless.
