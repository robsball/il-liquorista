**Hyssop.** — *Hyssopus officinalis* (Labiatae) is a small plant common in Italy; it has spiked flowers, violet, sometimes red or white. The stems (30–40 cm) are quadrangular. It gives off an aromatic odor and has a slightly acrid, bitterish taste. The flowering tops are used; these must be chosen well dried and stripped of leaves or blackened flowers. It contains an essential oil that is also used in perfumery.

**Kola (Kola Nuts).** — These are the seeds of *Sterculia acuminata* (Malvaceae), which grows along the western coast of equatorial Africa (Cameroon). They contain 2–3% caffeine and many other special substances, not all well defined.

They are used roasted like coffee, then infused, or by macerating them in spirit, wine, etc.

They are often found mixed with similar nuts, such as those of *Heritiera litoralis* (False Kola), from which, however, they can easily be distinguished.

**Laurel.** — *Laurus nobilis* is common in our gardens and grows wild in warm regions. The berries, similar to small olives, have a bitter aromatic odor. The deep green, persistent leaves also have an aromatic odor and taste.

**Lavender.** — True Lavender, or *Lavandula officinalis*, belongs to the Labiatae. It grows wild in mountainous areas of Spain, Corsica, Italy, and North Africa, and is cultivated abundantly in England, Germany, etc. It is 30–60 cm tall. All parts of the plant, but especially the flowers, give off a distinctive fragrant odor, slightly camphoraceous, which they retain for a long time even after drying, and they contain an abundance of essential oil.

**Rosewood (Legno-rosa).** — This is obtained especially from the roots of *Convolvulus scoparius* and *Convolvulus floridus*, that is, from the rosewood of the Canary Islands. From it an essential oil is extracted that is somewhat viscous, yellowish, which resinifies over time and turns brown; its odor resembles that of roses; its taste is bitterly aromatic.
