# Basic Principles of Alcoholometry

A few notes on the fundamental principles of alcoholometry and the use of alcoholometers will not be superfluous.

The value of a spirit is calculated from the percentage of pure alcohol it contains, that is, alcohol free of water. The proportion in which pure alcohol stands to the water contained in the spirit is expressed as a percentage figure, that is, by the number of parts of pure alcohol contained in 100 parts of spirit. Thus a spirit that contains 60 parts of pure alcohol and 40 parts of water in 100 parts is indicated as 60%.

The term "part" can denote a given proportion either by volume or by weight; indeed, in earlier times, both proportions were used when speaking of spirits. The old alcoolometers had two scales: that of Richter, which indicated the percentage by weight, and that of Tralles, which indicated the percentage by volume or, as is now said, the percentage per liter.

A spirit that read 60% on the Richter scale contained, in 100 kg, 60 kg of alcohol and 40 kg of water. A spirit at 60% according to Tralles contained, in 100 liters, 60 liters of pure alcohol and 40 liters of water. That equal percentages by weight and by volume do not correspond to equal alcoholic strength was demonstrated by the two scales placed side by side on the old alcoolometers. The difference is explained by the fact that, for one constituent of the spirit — water — one liter

therefore represents the fundamental unit of comparison, that is, its specific gravity is established as 1. A body of which a certain volume has twice the weight of an equal volume of water thus has a specific gravity of 2, while the specific gravity of a body weighing half as much would be indicated as 0.5.

The distinction between proportions by volume and by weight is perhaps clearer as follows.

One liter of pure (distilled) water weighs 1000 grams (= 1 kg). One liter of ether, with a specific gravity of 0.725–0.733, must therefore weigh 725–733 grams (= 0.725–0.733 kg). Ether is thus specifically lighter than alcohol at 95%, because the latter has a specific gravity of 0.8, so that one liter of it weighs 0.800 kg.

We limit ourselves here to noting that the temperature of a substance alters its specific gravity, and that therefore all such data must be referred to a specific temperature (see pages 35–36).
