# Liqueurs by Blending of Tinctures

## Liqueurs by Blending of Tinctures

This class encompasses liqueurs prepared by maceration or direct digestion of plant materials, particularly exemplified by fruit Ratafias, which represent the type of liqueurs prepared through maceration or direct infusion of vegetables. Special chapters devoted to Ratafias and Amari will follow.

We observe that for this class of liqueurs, two different procedures may be followed: either simple tinctures (called infusions), previously prepared, are blended together and then alcohol, water, and sugar or wine syrup, coloring agents, etc., are added; or alternatively, the compound tincture is prepared directly by maceration or digestion in alcohol of a mixture of plant substances or herbs suitably combined in determined proportions, to obtain the desired quality and intensity of flavor and aroma.

It is important to note that for this class of liqueurs especially—more than for others—notable inequalities of result can occur, even when following the same recipe; because these, while employing the most meticulous attention and care in manipulations, weighing, etc., and in the selection of plant materials, depend upon and are indeed related to the quality of the plant materials themselves, the state of their harvest, storage, or the ripeness of the fruits, and the influences of temperature, season, etc. Therefore, for these liqueurs in particular, the skill and taste of the manufacturer must demonstrate itself in introducing such modifications and variations as may be suggested by diverse circumstances and adapted to the recipes adopted.

Similarly, for the reasons just mentioned, it would be difficult to establish absolute and fixed limits in the proportions of tinctures and other ingredients to be used for the various classes of liqueurs.
