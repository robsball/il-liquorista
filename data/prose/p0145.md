Starch adulteration can be detected by adding a few drops of iodine tincture to a solution made with water and the suspect sugar: if a blue coloration appears, this indicates the presence of starch.

Sugar, and especially raw (brown) sugar—which is more hydrated—can, due to elevated temperature, undergo alcoholic and acid fermentations that alter it. These fermentations could also occur in crystallized sugar if it contains glucose, since glucose readily absorbs moisture.

Sugar varieties that show a bluish appearance should be rejected, since this tint is produced by the presence of ultramarine, which then imparts to the liquid a cloudiness and almost an iridescence that is very difficult, if not nearly impossible, to eliminate, and which causes other significant problems. In fact, ultramarine—a complex mineral substance—when it comes into contact with an organic acid such as citric acid, decomposes, releasing the repugnant odor of hydrogen sulfide (rotten eggs), which renders the liqueur undrinkable.

On the market there is a lower grade of sugar than the loaf form, made of sugar powder mixed with irregular lumps and fragments, more or less coarse: it looks less white than loaf sugar, but does not contain ultramarine, though it sometimes shows a yellowish tint.

Regarding the value of different sugar qualities, there is a mistaken belief that should be corrected.

Some manufacturers consider white rock candy (candito bianco) to be a finer and purer quality because they believe it is made exclusively from cane sugar, and therefore use it for the finest liqueurs. This opinion is in fact mistaken, because only a small portion of the sugar currently sold commercially is actually of Indian (cane) origin.

White rock candy differs from refined sugar in nothing other than its crystalline form. The sugar manufacturer crystallizes either one form or the other from the same concentrated sugar solution, depending on momentary demand. From the sugar solution, refined sugar is obtained if the solution is agitated, or rock candy if crystallization is allowed to proceed undisturbed and slowly.
