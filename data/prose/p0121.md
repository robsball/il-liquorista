# List of the Principal Essential Oils

The essential oils used in the manufacture of liqueurs must be absolutely pure, free from any adulteration, and must therefore come from firms that offer sufficient guarantees of honesty, since it is very difficult for the buyer to check and verify the purity of these products, the necessary tests being rather delicate and complicated and within the competence only of chemical laboratories (determination of physical constants, i.e. boiling point, rotatory power, etc.).

In the table given on pages 108 and 109, we set out the list of the principal essential oils, indicating their origin, the average quantity extracted from the plants, as well as their density and color.

We shall now briefly review the most important of these essential oils used in the manufacture of liqueurs, indicating their main characteristics and properties, and referring, as regards their origin, to the plants already summarily described.

**Angelica.** — Two essential oils are extracted from angelica: the one extracted from the root is at first colorless, but later turns yellow; it has a strongly aromatic odor and flavor. The one extracted from the seeds is yellow and has a much more delicate odor. It may be found adulterated with turpentine essence.

**Common Anise.** — The essential oil is extracted from anise seeds by steam distillation. The rectified product is colorless, fluid, dense, and of a sweetish flavor; in air it easily alters, becoming resinous; at a temperature of +5–8°C it solidifies into a crystalline mass; the addition of a small proportion of alcohol prevents this crystallization, which, however, should not be taken as a sure sign of the product's purity, since other essential oils of lower quality show the same crystallization behavior (see Fennel). It is highly soluble, almost in any proportion, in alcohol (95%).

**Star Anise (or Indian Anise).** — This essential oil is obtained by steam distillation of star anise seeds. It is clear, light yellow, and darkens in air.
