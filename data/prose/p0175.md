If the residues left from extraction are not completely exhausted, they can be extracted a second time. These weak extracts are then set aside to prepare, the next time, the same type of concentrated tincture or extract, or else they are used in the preparation of lower-value liqueurs.

### Digestion

The only difference between maceration and digestion consists in this: that in the former one operates at ordinary temperature, while in the latter one operates at 35-50°. Digestion, however, is little practiced, and only in cases where speed in preparation is required.

The substances to be digested are prepared exactly as for maceration.

For digestion one can also use a simple still, placing in the boiler the chopped and finely divided drugs together with the alcohol and water, and setting up the apparatus as if one were going to proceed with a distillation; instead, the still is heated—understood to be a water bath—only up to 40-50°C, repeating the treatment twice a day for 2-3 consecutive days; after which the liquid, now completely cold, is drawn off from the discharge tap.

For digestion it is more useful to employ continuously operating apparatus, in which the aromatic substances are continuously passed through by a current of alcohol vapors given off from a separate vessel, into which they then return after having taken up the aromatic principles, until, judging that they have become sufficiently enriched, the operation is stopped.
