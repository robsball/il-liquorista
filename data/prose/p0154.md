These colors may prove harmful to health, even though the proportion used is minimal, and for this reason their use has been regulated by special rules.

**N.B.** The use of these colors in Italy has been regulated by the lists of harmful colors published by Royal Decree of 7 February 1892 (No. 55) and subsequent amendments by Royal Decree of 7 August 1892 (No. 434), 29 January 1893 (No. 132), and 24 March 1895 (No. 101).

According to this last amendment, only the following three are declared harmful organic colors and therefore not permitted:

1) **Dinitrocresol** — A mixture of the alkaline salts of dinitro-ortho-cresol and dinitro-para-cresol (Syn.: substitute gold yellow called saffron yellow — Victoria yellow — Victoria orange — aniline orange).

2) **Martius Yellow**, dinitro-β-naphthol (Syn.: naphthol yellow, naphthalene yellow, Manchester yellow, saffron yellow, gold yellow).

3) **Metanil Yellow** — sodium salt of m-amidobenzenesulfonic acid — azo-diphenylamine (Syn.: M.N. Yellow, Tropeolin Q).

All other colors would therefore be permitted, though always subject to the restrictions set out in the Regulation for the application of the hygiene-related provisions contained in the Spirits Law of 20 February 1890, in the Regulation of 3 February 1901 for the application of the 1888 Law for the Protection of Public Health and Hygiene, and in local health regulations (see the end of the volume). Merely as a matter of curiosity, we note that the aniline colors permitted as non-harmful by the earlier list of 29 January 1893 were the following:

| Column 1 | Column 2 |
|---|---|
| Azoflavine | Naphthol Yellow S |
| Bordeaux | Tonneau |
| Chrysoidine | Rocelline |
| Sulfonated Fuchsine | Biebrich Scarlet |
| Gentian Violet | Tropeolin |

Besides these aniline colors, it should also be remembered that among colors of organic origin, gamboge and picric acid are prohibited for coloring food substances.
