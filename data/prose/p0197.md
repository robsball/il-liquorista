A more effective and simple process, although it requires the use of special apparatus, was proposed by Villon and consists of leaving the liquor in contact with oxygen under pressure, at a temperature that varies depending on the result one wishes to achieve.

The necessary apparatus consists of a copper vessel, sufficiently strong and fitted with a pressure gauge, into which the liquor to be treated is poured. By means of a regulating valve connected to a cylinder of compressed oxygen, this gas is introduced until the gauge indicates 2 atmospheres of pressure. The vessel is then exposed, under these conditions, to gradual heating until the gauge shows 5-6 atmospheres; at that point the heating is stopped and the whole is left to stand for 24 hours, after which the compressed gas is allowed to escape and the liquor is then removed.

It is advisable to repeat this very simple treatment for two or three consecutive days, in order to achieve a more complete result (1).

Liquors, brandies, and wines treated in this way acquire a more delicate flavor and do not become cloudy.

The use of electricity combined with the action of oxygen has also been proposed, or better still, according to Sauer, with very pure hydrogen peroxide (oxygenated water).

Another process consists of exposing the alcoholic liquid inside wooden vessels, preferably oak, to the action of a warm atmosphere, partially or completely saturated with humidity. In place of a warm, humid atmosphere, any other suitable moist heat may be used. In practice, the wooden vessels containing the alcoholic liquid are stacked one upon another in cellars impermeable to steam, or in other suitable chambers, so that the moist heat surrounds each vessel or circulates around it. The necessary heat and humidity are produced by means of one or more jets of steam, or by means of a nebulizer with hot water. The vessels may also be immersed in hot water. The length of time for which the vessels must remain under treatment varies, as does the degree of heat and humidity...
