# Materials Glossary (continued)

Under the name Serpentaria, however, other plants different from the one described above are also designated, such as Dracunculus (Arum Dracunculus), Bistort (Polygonum bistorta), etc.

**Tansy or Athanasia.** — Tanacetum vulgare (Compositae) is a plant that grows in damp places; its numerous flowers are a solid, beautiful yellow. The whole plant gives off a strong, unpleasant odor and has a bitter, aromatic, nauseating taste.

**Dandelion.** — The root of the dandelion comes from Leontodon Taraxacum, a very common plant found in meadows, also commonly called Dente di Leone (Lion's Tooth), Soffione, etc. When dry it is hard, wrinkled, and brown. It has no odor; it has a bitter taste. It is tonic, aperitive, and diuretic.

**Tea.** — The leaves of Thea chinensis and of various related species are sold commercially in dried form and are distinguished into black teas and green teas, according to the method of preparation; the former undergo a light fermentation before drying, the latter are dried immediately and are often artificially colored. The most prized variety of black tea is known by the name Pekoe Tea or Pai-Ho; it is scented with flowers of Olea fragrans and often contains seeds of this plant; its infusion is golden yellow. We will not go into detail about the many other commercial varieties of tea, since in the few cases where these leaves are used in liqueur-making, Pekoe is used. Naturally, other varieties may also be used, though yielding less refined products.

**Thyme.** — Also called sermollino, pepolino. This quite common little plant (Thymus vulgaris) belongs to the Labiatae family; it gives off a strong aromatic odor, especially when rubbed, and has an aromatic or pungent taste. Its essential oil and thymol are extracted from it.

**Tormentil.** — Potentilla tormentilla has a root about the thickness of a finger, conical, almost woody, reddish on the outside, black on the inside, and provided with many small rootlets. It contains about 20% tannin, for which reason it is sometimes used in medicine.

**Dried Grapes - Raisins.** — Many varieties are found in commerce, from Malaga, Greece, Calabria, Lipari, etc. Those used in the preparation of liqueurs must be well preserved, blond or red in color, not brown or black, and of pleasant taste and aroma.
