# Vermouth: Production Method

## Vermouth: Production Method

Maceration or digestion of a mixture composed of diverse aromatic and bitter herbs and botanicals in alcohol.

The composition of the mixture of aromatic and bitter herbs and botanicals for the preparation of extracts—especially regarding both the quality and quantity of the botanicals—is jealously guarded as a trade secret by every producing house. Some of these houses operate the Vermouth production industry on a large scale, particularly in Piedmont, and annually export several thousand hectolitres. The special flavour and thus the characteristic merit of each type of Vermouth depends largely upon the quality of this aromatic and bitter composition.

Setting aside direct maceration of dry aromatic and bitter herbs in wine, as this is a very simple and almost primitive operation now abandoned for Vermouth production, we note that the best procedure for preparing the aromatic extract consists of digestion.

Some producers, instead of preparing the extract of aromatic and bitter herbs by maceration, resort to distillation of these to obtain an alcoholate, which is then added to the wine.

One may also proceed as follows: The botanicals or herbs are first weighed, chopped, and ground according to their nature; they are then weighed and intimately mixed, and introduced into alcohol in such a manner that they remain entirely submerged. After 15–20 days of maceration, stirring the mass daily (in barrels or demijohns), the liquid is drawn off and preserved separately in casks or demijohns. An equal amount of wine and alcohol is then added, and after several days of maceration, the mass (liquid and solid parts) is poured into the alembic's kettle and slowly distilled to recover all the alcohol.

After the aromatic extract has been mixed with wine and any sugar has been added, the product obtained must be left to rest for several months, then repeatedly filtered so that it becomes clear and homogeneous.
