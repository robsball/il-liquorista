With chemically pure alcohol, no coloration is obtained even after a long time; but alcohols containing aldehydes produce tints ranging from pale pink to deep violet. One must wait a certain amount of time, at least 10 minutes, for the tint to reach its maximum intensity; this intensity is maintained for several hours and then decreases.

Comparison with types of alcohol having a known aldehyde content allows, to a certain extent, the determination of the quantity of aldehydes in the liquid being tested.

The reagent is prepared as follows: 1 gram of fuchsin is dissolved in boiling water, and with additional cold water the solution is brought to a volume of one liter. 20 cc of sodium bisulfite solution at 30° Baumé are added. After several hours, when the liquid retains only a slightly yellowish coloration, 10 cc of concentrated hydrochloric acid are poured in. The liquid thus prepared keeps unaltered for a very long time.

One might perhaps object that this method is extremely sensitive; and indeed it is quite difficult to find an alcohol that does not give a pink tint when tested with decolorized fuchsin. With a bit of practice, however, one learns to recognize the intensity of tint that should be considered negligible.

A somewhat less sensitive method, but for that reason more recommendable for the detection of aldehydes, is the reaction with metaphenylenediamine hydrochloride. It can be carried out as follows, as recommended by Windisch: For each test, an amount of metaphenylenediamine hydrochloride the size of what fits on the tip of a knife is dissolved in twice that quantity of distilled, boiled water, and this solution, while still warm, is poured drop by drop onto the alcohol to be examined, placed in a white porcelain dish. At the boundary of contact between the two liquids, depending on the quantity of aldehydes present, a faintly yellow or reddish-yellow zone forms. This coloration disappears upon the addition of ammonia or a fixed alkali, and returns upon acidifying with hydrochloric acid.
