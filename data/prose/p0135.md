If the essential oil is adulterated, a small plastic mass is seen to form, which settles at the bottom of the test tube; if the oil is pure, the tannin remains unchanged in suspension in the liquid.

One may also mix equal volumes of water and essential oil in a graduated tube and shake the mixture vigorously until it becomes milky. On standing, if the oil contained alcohol, the alcohol will have combined with the water, increasing its volume at the expense of the volume of the essence, which will be correspondingly reduced.

Some essential oils can also be examined by adding a small crystal of alcohol-soluble fuchsine. In the presence of alcohol, the fuchsine dissolves and colors the essence; otherwise this does not occur. The test is carried out as follows:

A few cc of the essential oil are introduced into a narrow, long, and thoroughly dried test tube; it is stoppered with a plug of soft cotton containing a crystal of fuchsine, and then heated. If the essential oil contains alcohol, the cotton will turn red as a result of the fuchsine dissolving in the alcohol that condenses there. Even minute traces of alcohol can thus be detected.

The essences of almond, clove, cinnamon, cherry laurel, and mustard dissolve fuchsine even in the absence of alcohol. Consequently, this test cannot be applied to these essences. The essences of cumin, common anise, star anise, fennel, citron, orange, bergamot, juniper berries, mace, ginger, and others do not dissolve fuchsine and can therefore give a reliable test; with peppermint essence, however, the test is unreliable.

A further test is the following:

25 cc of essential oil are poured into a small flask arranged so as to collect the distilled products. It is heated in a boiling water bath for 10–15 minutes. Nothing should pass over during the distillation; if, on the contrary, some product is obtained, the presence of alcohol should be suspected.
