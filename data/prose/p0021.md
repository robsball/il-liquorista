For a given recipe, it will often happen that different results are obtained, since this can depend either on the differing quality or grade of the raw materials, or on the different temperature conditions under which the operation was carried out (summer, winter).

Reserving for the special chapter on the manufacture of liqueurs the detailed description of the procedures and necessary rules, for now we shall note that the various mixtures should be prepared cold, except in special cases, since in this way one risks less loss of highly volatile aromatic substances.

The use of sugar dissolved hot is far preferable to that of sugar dissolved cold: the fineness and the smooth, velvety quality of the liqueur are, in the first case, much more developed and pronounced.

The order to be followed when mixing is as follows:

1. Plain alcohol. — 2. Aromatized alcohol. — 3. Sugar or syrup. — 4. Water. — 5. Coloring matter — then everything is mixed together thoroughly; and, as will be described further on, if necessary one proceeds to clarification (fining), filtration, etc.

The coloring substance must be added in small portions, until the desired tint is reached.

During mixing it must be stirred continuously with a spatula to obtain perfect homogeneity.

To judge the result of the product obtained, one must wait until it has rested for a period of at least two or three days: indeed, sometimes, in order to hasten the homogeneity and the blending of the aroma in the prepared liqueur, it is necessary to resort to a special process which the French call *Tranchage*, which consists of exposing the liqueur to heating at a temperature of 80° in a closed vessel, so as not to lose the volatile products; in this way one produces almost an artificial aging, since it is known that liqueurs, as they age, acquire a more delicate, refined, and pleasant flavor.

In general, liqueurs should be kept for at least a year before beginning their sale and consumption.
