It is understood that every manufacturer must dilute the essences in strong alcohol before adding the water or dissolved sugar, and must wait until they are completely dissolved and homogeneously distributed in the alcohol.

When subsequently adding water, it is necessary to avoid having the essence, upon contact between the alcohol and the water — as the water dilutes the alcohol — separate out in large part: as long as the alcohol remains strong enough, it redissolves the portions that separate. But if, toward the end of the dilution, the alcohol becomes too weak, its dissolving power gradually diminishes, and thus portions of the separated essence remain undissolved which the alcohol, under other circumstances, would still have been able to dissolve.

This problem can be minimized by proceeding with the greatest care when adding the water. That is, toward the end of the operation, the water should be allowed to flow into the alcohol in a thin stream, and this addition should be paused frequently in order to stir the mixture and keep it homogeneous.

When combining the tinctures with the base excipient of the liqueur, it is above all important to know whether one is dealing with non-clouding tinctures — to which category the 60% (non-distilled) extracts also belong — or with dilutions of essential oils or essences, which are therefore of high percentage. In the first case, the tinctures are added to the already-finished base of the liqueur.

Mixing them with strong alcohol would produce clouding, at least in the case of the non-distilled extracts. High-percentage dilutions, on the other hand, are treated like essences and must be combined with the alcohol before being mixed into the base excipient of the liqueur.

As for the commercial non-clouding tinctures, these must:

1. Not cloud the liqueur.
2. Impart to the liqueur the flavor of the plant material in its full purity and intensity; they must be a distillate of the plant material with 60% alcohol, and not a distillate of essential oils with spirit.
