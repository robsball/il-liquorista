# Aromatic Substances — Herbs and Plants — Selection and Preservation

In the manufacture of liqueurs, use is made of the aromatic, bitter, and sometimes even medicinal properties of many plants, the most important of which we will describe, noting their most relevant characteristics. Very few animal-derived aromatic substances are used for the same purpose.

Since the quality of the liqueur produced depends in large part on the good quality and proper preservation of these substances, one must have a thorough knowledge of them in order to know how to select the most suitable ones and reject those that might compromise the success of the process. We will therefore indicate some guidelines that may serve as a guide for the less experienced, while noting that, in this case as in many others, experience is the best guide.

We consider it unnecessary to describe the methods of gathering these substances, since it is quite rare for the liqueur-maker to personally undertake such collection. However, he may need to instruct others on these matters, or may need, in special cases, to use freshly gathered plants.

The parts of plants used in liqueur-making are the roots, leaves, flowers, seeds, fruits, barks, and wood. Roots, wood, and seeds are generally found on the market in dried form, and we will describe their characteristics in each individual case. In general, one should reject those plant materials that are worm-eaten or damaged by insects, or moldy or too old, as well as those found to be contaminated with a foreign aroma different from their own.

Fresh plants should be gathered in dry, clear weather, with the sun high, so that they are no longer wet with dew; naturally, the most vigorous specimens should be chosen.
