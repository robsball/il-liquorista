The distillate is distilled and then sweetened with sugar dissolved in aromatized water and mixed with alcohol. Finally it is filtered.

Some producers also crush the pits of cherries, and in this case they obtain Rosolio made from marasca cherry pits. The most renowned is maraschino from Zara. It is difficult to find genuine maraschino in commerce. Since various recipes and procedures are used by individual producers, maraschino varies considerably from one factory to another. Consequently, there are various recipes: some of these have already been explained; others will be indicated in the following chapter (Liqueurs by Essences).

Maraschinos, coming for the most part from stone fruits, owe their fragrance to the skin of the fruit, and they also have a very pronounced stone fruit flavor. These liqueurs rarely have sufficient strength from the first distillation; and it is necessary to rectify them, either over direct fire or in a water bath, if one has the facility; some producers add at that moment to the still some leaves from the tree and the pits of the fruits to increase the fragrance. These spirits gain much if they are promptly cooled with ice.

The caustic and disagreeable flavor of most of the fruit spirits found in commerce must be attributed to poor handling.

The fermentation of the fruits must take place, as much as possible, in large vessels and in wooden casks, which must be carefully washed to remove any off-flavors, mixing together with the fruits a few leaves. The distilled liqueur is brought to the degree deemed appropriate, or it is ordinarily mixed with a simple syrup perfectly clarified, in which approximately 6 parts of sugar are incorporated per 30 of liqueur and a quantity of water proportional to the strength of the spirit. It is filtered, if deemed appropriate: for the most part this is unnecessary when the syrup is well made and clear.
