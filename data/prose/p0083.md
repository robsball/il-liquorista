# Preparation and Preservation of Aromatic Plants

Drying is carried out by laying the plants on wooden or wicker racks, turning them from time to time until fully dried, so as to prevent fermentation or yellowing of the leaves.

Care must be taken not to pile too thick a layer of plant material on the racks. Direct exposure to sunlight should also be avoided, as it can be harmful — especially when the coloring properties of the plant are also to be used. Drying in a warm but shaded place, or in a drying oven, is preferable.

As for flowers, one should choose the most fragrant ones, well developed but not withered, and above all gathered in dry weather; moisture promotes a fermentation that destroys much of the fragrance and gives rise to an unpleasant grassy odor. We think it worth mentioning that, according to recent studies, it has been established that the deterioration of these substances (drugs/herbs) is due mainly to oxidases (enzymes with strong oxidizing power), which in some cases disappear during drying, at least in part; in others, however, they persist significantly, and in such cases they can only be neutralized by the action of heat at 100°C, or by drying in a prolonged current of dry, warm air.

As for fruits, except in special cases where it is necessary to use them unripe (bitter oranges, cherries, sour cherries, walnuts, etc.), they should be fully ripe, well selected, and of the most aromatic quality. One should avoid using fruit that has ripened poorly, or that has been stored too long, or is overripe — and, needless to say, fruit that has been bruised or damaged during transport.

Since the worst enemy of dried aromatic substances is moisture, they must be kept in a very dry place, in small paper packets or bags, ensuring good ventilation; for those substances prone to insect infestation, tightly sealed tin boxes or glass jars may be used instead.
