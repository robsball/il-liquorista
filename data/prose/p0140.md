Ambergris softens at about 60°C; it is lighter than water (specific gravity 0.90–0.92). Its very pleasant odor is due to an essential oil that it contains in the proportion of 13%; this oil is soluble in alcohol and in ether.

**Musk** is a secretion from the skin of a species of deer (a kind of roe deer) living in the mountains of central Asia, *Moschus moschiferus*. This substance is contained in a special pouch near the navel of the male animal, where it exists in a semi-fluid state; it has a brown color and an extraordinarily intense and diffusible odor. It later becomes solid, granular, somewhat greasy to the touch, and yellowish-brown in color, and it is in this form that it is sold commercially, where several qualities can be found. The most highly prized are those from Nanking, or China, and from Tonkin.

The chemist Baur succeeded in preparing musk by chemical synthesis (trinitrobutyltoluene), with properties perfectly identical to those of natural musk; more recently, an oily product with the fragrance of musk has also been obtained synthetically, by oxidation, from copal, yellow amber, and similar substances.

## Water

Particular attention must be paid to the purity of the water used in the manufacture of liqueurs, both for diluting the alcohol and for dissolving the sugar in preparing syrup. Spring water is generally used; however, to obtain fine liqueurs it is necessary that the water be chemically pure, and therefore distilled water is preferable, or at least rainwater, collected and stored with the proper precautions.

To obtain distilled water, stills are used — which we will describe further on — heated by direct fire, using ordinary water of good quality and discarding the first and last portions that come over during distillation.
