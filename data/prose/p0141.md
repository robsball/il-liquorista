Sulfurous or ferruginous waters must be rejected; likewise hard, calcareous waters (which become cloudy on heating, do not cook vegetables well, and do not allow soap to dissolve properly), and those containing a high proportion of mineral salts (fixed residue exceeding 0.60 g per liter), as well as those rich in organic substances (which can easily be recognized if, after adding 0.10 g of potassium permanganate to a liter of water, the liquid fails to retain the violet coloration acquired when boiled in a clean vessel).

The richness of calcareous salts in a potable water (which can be assessed either by the hydrometric test or recognized by adding a few drops of logwood tincture, which upon contact with calcium carbonate will change its color from yellow to a more or less intense violet) — besides being detrimental to the flavor of the liqueur, hindering the formation of the ethers that determine its bouquet — also causes cloudiness and deposits from the separation of the salts that were dissolved in it and that precipitate out upon mixing with alcohol; so that such liqueurs never manage to attain the necessary clarity.

If good spring, well, or cistern water is not available, it will be necessary to filter it; paper filtration, being slow and costly, would not eliminate any bad tastes the water might have. For this purpose, charcoal or alternating-layer sand filters are needed, of which various types can be found commercially. On this point we have only one piece of advice to give the reader: to stick to simple systems.
