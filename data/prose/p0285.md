# Syrups Glucosated

## Syrups Glucosated

We have already alluded to the use of glucose or starch syrup in the preparation of syrups, an application that must, however, be declared in order to avoid incurring the penalties imposed by current regulations for the protection of public health and hygiene.

The proportions of starch syrup to be used in mixture with sugar may vary according to the taste of the liqueur manufacturer or consumer; it will be necessary, however, to recall that starch syrup or glucose possesses a sweetening power one-third lower than that of cane sugar.

Some manufacturers resort to the use of starch syrup not so much for reasons of sugar economy, as to achieve greater density and body in liqueurs, especially in ordinary or so-called semi-fine varieties. In general, the proportions adopted by these producers are: sugar 18 kg, white starch syrup (36°) 12 liters (15 kg).

Finally, we observe that one may verify that an ordinary liqueur contains the appropriate quantity of sugar when it reads 5 degrees on the syrup hydrometer; a semi-fine liqueur, 10 degrees.

We shall finally indicate the composition of a mixed syrup of sugar and starch or glucosated.

### Currant Syrup

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit |
|---|---|---|
| White sugar | 6.600 | kg |
| Tartaric acid | 0.020 | g |
| Currant preserve | 1.800 | liters |
| Red wine | 1.800 | liters |
| Raspberry vinegar | 0.300 | g |
| Starch syrup | 3 | g |

This latter ingredient may be added to the sugar in the pan together with the other liquids, and the mass exposed to heating for the shortest possible duration; or the glucose may be added after the cooking of the sugar, or better still, before filtration.
