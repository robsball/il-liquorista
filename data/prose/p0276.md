## Sugar Syrup Preparation

100 parts of sugar dissolved in 50 parts of water and heated to the proper cooking temperature, as indicated above, should yield exactly 100 liters of sugar syrup.

For practical purposes and to know when to stop the operation, a mark can be made in the kettle corresponding to a volume of 100 liters, and evaporation of the syrup is continued until the level of the mass reaches this limit. As we have already noted: 100 kg of sugar occupies a volume corresponding to 62.50 liters; adding 50 liters to 100 kg gives a total of 112.50 liters, from which therefore it is necessary to eliminate 12.50 liters through evaporation.

## Clarification and Decolorization of Sugar

Regarding the quality of sugar to be used, we have already treated this in the chapter on sugar; and therefore we will not repeat ourselves. We nevertheless recommend that sugar used for fine, colorless or lightly colored liqueurs must be of the very highest quality: only for dark-colored liqueurs of strong aroma and for bitters may lower-quality sugar be used.

The solution of refined sugar, even of the best quality, in pure water—even distilled water—never produces a clear liquid. Indeed, in general this turns out turbid and yellowish; and even when heated to boiling to make syrup, this achieves imperfect clarity and is therefore entirely unsuitable for use in liqueur manufacture, especially for fine liqueurs.

It must also be remembered that prolonged boiling, especially if the syrup is concentrated, colors it yellow and modifies the crystallizable sugar by partially transforming it into non-crystallizable sugar.

It is therefore necessary to proceed with clarification and decolorization to obtain a usable product.
