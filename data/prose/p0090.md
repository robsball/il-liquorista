# Cacao, Coffee, Aromatic Calamus, Germander

The cacao beans, about the size of a broad bean, are covered with a hard, dry pulp; they are violet or reddish in color and have a slightly bitter but not unpleasant taste. When shelled, they contain on average almost 50% fat (cocoa butter). The husk represents about 15% of the weight of the raw beans.

**Coffee.** — Common coffee is the seed of *Coffea arabica*, a plant native to southern Ethiopia and now cultivated in all the warm regions of the world (Rubiaceae family).

The most prized varieties are Mocha, with a sweet aroma and flavor, small, rounded beans of yellowish color; Bourbon and Martinique, greenish in color; Puerto Rico, Guatemala, Java, and a few others. Brazilian coffees — Rio, Santos, Bahia — are the least prized, the last especially for its empyreumatic (burnt) flavor.

The quality of coffee depends not only on the variety of the plant that produces it and the region where it is grown, but also on the way it is harvested and processed before being brought to market.

Coffee beans contain caffeine, caffetannic acid, nitrogenous substances, sugars, etc. During roasting, an aromatic principle develops, called *Caffeone*, while the beans lose 15–20% of their weight and increase in volume. Adulterated coffees, mixed with various substitutes, are frequently found on the market.

**Aromatic Calamus.** — *Acorus calamus* is an herbaceous plant that grows in humid places of southern Europe and in India.

The rhizome is used, sold dried, in pieces 25–40 cm long and 1–2 cm in diameter; they are knotty, nearly cylindrical, yellow-brown on the outside and whitish inside, with a spongy texture.

The surface is covered with triangular scars corresponding to the leaves, and other small circular ones corresponding to the roots branching off from the rhizome. The taste of this rhizome is bitterish and pungent, and the odor aromatic. It is often worm-eaten. Very similar to aromatic calamus is the so-called wild calamus (yellow iris, or yellow lily), which is *Iris pseudacorus*.
