They do not constitute immediate principles, as is demonstrated by the variability of their boiling point. For the most part they are composed of a hydrocarbon principle and an oxygenated principle; probably they are merely the vehicle of the aromatic substance, which so far it has not been possible to isolate.

Essential oils are prepared by distilling the plant materials in which they are contained, together with water, as is done for aromatic waters. Generally fresh plants are used, but in certain cases better results are obtained with dried plants.

To distill them, the plant parts, finely chopped, are placed together with a sufficient quantity of water in the boiler of the distilling apparatus.

The essential oils volatilize immediately together with the water, or more precisely at a temperature considerably lower than that at which they could volatilize without water. Upon condensing in the condenser, the essential oils and water separate and flow separately into the receiver. The essential oil floats on the water if it is lighter, or sinks to the bottom if heavier. The second distillation (rectification) is likewise carried out in the presence of water.

Although the presence of water brings about a noticeable decrease in the volatilization temperature of the essential oils, this temperature nevertheless remains high enough to harm their odor and flavor. Consequently, all essential oils obtained by distillation are far from having the fineness and delicacy that they originally possess in the plant.

This difference is especially striking in the essences of flowers and spices. Compare the scent of living rose and orange blossoms, the aroma of cloves, of cardamom seeds, etc., when finely chopped, with the odor of their respective essential oils, and one can note the difference in intensity and quality.
