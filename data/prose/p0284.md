The middle layers whose temperature is too high are likewise harmful to syrups: these do not delay in fermenting; they become turbid, then foaming, and finally acidify and are lost completely. The remedy in this case is heating done in time; in this way the carbon dioxide, which causes the syrup to foam, and the alcohol that has formed are dispersed. Sometimes, depending on circumstances, it is better to resort to a new clarification and to evaporation to a suitable consistency.

Another alteration is moldiness, which occurs when the syrup is bottled too soon, when it is left exposed, or when the containers are damp.

It can happen that deposits form in the containers, which invade the entire mass. This problem manifests itself when the syrup has been cooked too much. In this case too, slight reheating will remedy it.

To be free from any alteration, syrups must be stored in well-sealed bottles and placed in a sound cellar or any cool environment.

**Test.** — Syrups are frequently prepared with glucose rather than pure sugar. By treating a tablespoon (25 g) with 8–10 drops of a saturated solution of iodine in alcohol (50%), if glucose is present a persistent brown coloration manifests with turbidity; whereas with pure sugar this disappears rapidly.

The presence of glucose in syrups can be recognized by placing a tablespoon (about 10 grams) of the suspect syrup in a glass balloon and adding 10 grams of a weak potash solution. It is then heated to boiling over a spirit lamp.

If the syrup is free from glucose it takes on, with boiling, a fine golden-yellow color; if instead it is mixed with glucose it acquires the brown color of coffee and emits a characteristic "caramel odor."
