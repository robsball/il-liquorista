# Syrups and Their Preparation

## Introduction to Syrups

We shall now provide some information on syrups properly speaking, and on some that may find application in liqueur manufacture.

Syrups may be simple or compound; and both categories may be prepared with pure sugar, or mixed with glucose.

To ensure good preservation of syrups and prevent crystallization or fermentation, it is important to note that they should exhibit the following densities:

| | At boiling | At cold temperature |
|---|---|---|
| | Winter | Summer | Winter | Summer |
| **Simple** | 1.23 (29°) | 1.26 (30°) | 1.30 (34°) | 1.31 (35°) |
| **Compound** | 1.26 (30°) | 1.28 (32°) | 1.31 (35°) | 1.34 (37°) |

We shall now proceed to give instructions for the preparation of some of the most commonly used syrups.

When preparing compound syrups, the necessary ingredients and aromatic substances are added to simple sugar syrup, sometimes modifying the cooking process slightly depending on circumstances.

## Preparation of Syrups

### Simple or Sugar Syrup

This syrup is used as such in the preparation of fine and superfine liqueurs; it must be prepared with refined sugar, in the proportions already indicated or according to the prescription of the Official Pharmacopoeia of the Kingdom, and must be carefully clarified.

During cooking, it is necessary to promote vigorous but brief boiling to avoid prolonged heating, which could produce an irreparable brown coloration; noting however that the liquid must not boil over the edges of the container, since this would make it necessary to add fresh water to redissolve the adhering layer, thus diluting the liquid and prolonging the heating required to concentrate it again.

Pour it while still lukewarm into the containers in which it is to be stored.
