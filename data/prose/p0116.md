Essential oils extracted by pressing have a more delicate scent, but they keep less well than those extracted by distillation. Moreover, when the essential oil is extracted by pressing the whole mass, instead of proceeding as indicated above (with sponges), the resulting products are cloudy due to the presence of mucilage.

Essential oils obtained by pressing dissolve less readily in alcohol than those obtained by distillation.

Essential oils can also be extracted from plants by other methods, such as maceration or enfleurage, both based on treating the plant material with beef fat, petroleum jelly, or olive oil, substances which fix scents with extreme ease; the oils are then extracted from these using alcohol. These two methods are of special interest only in perfumery, and we therefore refrain from describing them here.

Most essential or ethereal oils, when fresh and well preserved, are liquid or lighter than water, free-flowing, colorless or variously colored, clear, and highly volatile. They all have a strong or penetrating odor, which more or less resembles or recalls that of the substance from which they derive, though never as pleasant. They have an irritating, acrid, caustic taste.

Their boiling point is quite variable, ranging from 80°C (oil of lavender) to 270°C (oil of patchouli); indeed, because of this characteristic, the term "essential" or "ethereal" oil would seem improper, since this term is generally used to designate a liquid with a low boiling point. Although the boiling point of essential oils is higher than that of water, they are nevertheless easily carried over in distillation by steam.

In general, the denser oils are the least volatile.

They ignite very easily, producing a very luminous and sooty flame.
