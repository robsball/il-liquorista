# On the Purity and Adulteration of Sugar

The liquor-maker must verify that the refining of the sugar has brought it to a state of perfect purity and that it contains no fraudulent adulterations. To detect such adulterations, it is advisable, in case of doubt, to consult a chemical analysis laboratory.

Pure sugar in white loaves is odorless, with a distinctive sweet taste and no secondary flavor; it crunches under the teeth. When powdered, however, the taste is altered and becomes unpleasant: sugared water prepared with a solution of powdered sugar turns out repugnant.

It is soluble in water and in diluted alcohol, but insoluble in absolute alcohol and in ether. At ordinary temperature, water dissolves about three times its own weight of sugar.

To verify the genuineness of sugar, whatever its grade — white, yellow, or brown, refined or not — it is useful to bear in mind the following requirements:

1) It must dissolve completely in water, imparting to the solution the familiar sweet taste;

2) The aqueous solution must be neutral; that is, it must show no reaction whatsoever on litmus paper;

3) When burned on a small metal plate, it must leave only minimal traces of ash;

4) When carefully stirred with concentrated sulfuric acid while cold, it must turn brown and char.

The principal frauds that may be committed with sugar are the following:

1. Addition of solid glucose; sugar adulterated with glucose is greasy to the touch and less sweet than ordinary sugar;

2. Addition of starch or starch flour;

3. Addition of mineral substances (marble/chalk);

4. Excess water.

To detect the presence of glucose, it suffices to use a solution of soda or potash, which is added to the sugared water solution; if the color that results is yellowish-red, this is a sign of the presence of glucose, since soda or potash reduce glucose but not sucrose.

When mineral substances are present, they remain insoluble in water, whereas genuine sugar must leave no residue.
