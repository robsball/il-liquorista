However, these glucoses generally come to market not perfectly refined, and therefore contain gum, dextrin, free mineral acids, etc. (arsenic has even been found present in them!), which compromise the success and preservation of the liqueurs in whose composition they are used. Therefore the use of glucose should be limited to the manufacture of lower-quality or specialty liqueurs.

Finally, we note that when needed, for certain dense liqueurs in which the presence of glucose is appropriate, one can easily obtain it by inverting the cane sugar used for the syrup: it will suffice to add to the sugar solution, before heating it, a small proportion (5 g per quintal) of an organic acid (tartaric acid or citric acid). — It is in fact well known that to prevent the crystallization of dense syrups, a bit more citric acid is added to them.

### Glycerin

In consideration of glycerin's notable solvent property toward coloring substances and toward the aromatic and extractive principles of plants; as well as its highly favorable action in preserving liquids containing organic substances, glycerin finds wide use in the manufacture of both liqueurs and, simply, syrups.

It is superfluous to point out that the glycerin to be used must also be pure and of excellent quality; that is, refined glycerin is to be preferred, or better still the purest grade: it should have a density of 1.240–1.260 (i.e., 28°–30° on the Baumé areometer) and be clear, heavy, oily, colorless and odorless, with a slightly sweet taste. We recall that glycerin is highly soluble in water and in alcohol in every proportion; and indeed, being extremely hygroscopic, it must be kept in well-sealed containers.
