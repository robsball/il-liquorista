# Alcoholometers

...indicate directly the alcoholic percentage, by volume or by weight, this quantity being expressed by the number marked at the point where the surface of the liquid meets the stem of the areometer; or else they indicate the density or specific gravity of the spirit, which corresponds to a given alcoholic strength.

The main alcoholometers are:

1) **Gay-Lussac alcoholometer, or centesimal**: this indicates the percentage by volume and is calibrated at a temperature of 15° C.

2) **Tralles alcoholometer**, which indicates the percentage by volume, and is calibrated for a temperature of 15° C: it is recognized as the official standard in Italy.

3) **Richter alcoholometer**, which indicates the percentage by weight of alcohol, at a temperature of 15° C: it is little used.

4) **Baumé areometer**, with a conventional graduation, which marks 40° for absolute alcohol, and 30° for commercial alcohol.

5) **Cartier areometer or spirit-weigher**, similar to the Baumé: the 10° mark corresponds to distilled water, and the ascending scale stops at 44°, corresponding approximately to absolute, anhydrous alcohol.

These last two are nowadays little used, precisely because of their somewhat empirical graduation, which does not express the percentage of alcohol directly but only a value corresponding to a given alcohol content, so that special tables are required to convert it.

We should also mention, besides the now-disused Beck areometer, the Sykes hydrometer, made of metal, adopted in England, empirically graduated, which is a spirit-weigher that must be immersed to 0° of its scale in alcohol of density 0.825 (40° Baumé) at 15.5° C, and is called the Standard Alcohol [hydrometer]; the Gendar hydrometer, similar to this last one and adopted in the United States; and the Russian metallic alcoholometer, with additional weights, likewise similar to the Sykes hydrometer.

Tralles originally established the scale of percentages per liter by measuring, in a cylinder perfectly divided into 100 parts by volume, first 99, then 98, then 97, and so on down to 1 part by volume of pure alcohol, making up the missing quantity to 100 parts with as much water as was necessary.
