# Caraway and Citron Essential Oils

The best product comes from Dutch seeds; the poorest from a wild variety of seed grown in Sweden and Norway; this latter, however, differs from the former in having a less intense and pleasant aroma. It should be noted that in commerce the two qualities are often found mixed together.

The rectified essential oil must be dissolved in an equal quantity of alcohol (95%).

This oil is often adulterated with oil extracted from cumin, with essence of turpentine, alcohol, etc. It is also frequently replaced with oil extracted from the seeds of wild caraway, which is less fine and aromatic.

From the caraway plant an essential oil called Kümmelspreuöl is also produced, of quite poor quality.

**Citron.** — The essential oil of fresh citron is greenish-yellow in color and is quite difficult to dissolve in alcohol; to dissolve it, at least 50 parts of 95% alcohol are needed, and it is advisable to let the solution rest for a few days before using it, so as to prevent the essential oil from separating out again after water is added.

The essential oil of rectified white citron dissolves much more easily, but has lost, due to the distillation process, its pleasant odor and flavor, and therefore cannot be used for this purpose.

The same applies to the essence — the so-called essence of citron peel — obtained by distilling essential citron oil over dried citron peels.

One might consider the use of this oil superfluous if it were possible to work at the same cost using alcoholic extracts of fresh citron peels, as with the essence. But since this essential oil is obtained by pressing rather than by distillation, it retains, at least when fresh and pure, the odor and flavor of the fresh peels, so that one can dispense with using a tincture or extract of them. This, however, does not hold true for old essences, because these contain a greater or lesser amount of vegetable mucilage, which quickly causes them to deteriorate.
